## Part 1: Early Life & Education

Morris Chang was born in 1931 in Ningbo, Zhejiang, China. He came to the US in 1949, attended Harvard for one year, then transferred to MIT where he earned BS and MS degrees in Mechanical Engineering. He later earned a PhD in Electrical Engineering from Stanford.

---

## Part 2: Career at Sylvania & Texas Instruments (1955–1983)

### Sylvania (1955–1958)
Chang joined Sylvania Semiconductor in 1955 with four job offers in hand:
> "I had four job offers and just by a very lucky break I chose Sylvania Semiconductor—without really knowing the implications, the ramifications. And I worked for Sylvania Semiconductor for three years when I decided, and I was right as later events proved, that Sylvania wasn't going anywhere in semiconductors."

### Texas Instruments (1958–1983)
Chang spent 25 years at TI, rising to Senior Vice President of the Worldwide Semiconductor Group. Key insights from this period:

**On learning curve pricing (his core strategic insight at TI):**
> "The learning curve... every time the cumulative volume doubles, the cost comes down by a fixed percentage."

This concept became foundational to TSMC's competitive strategy decades later.

**On leaving TI:**
> "I left TI without any job offer, and I left because I felt that essentially I had been put out to pasture at TI. I felt sure that I would still continue to have a job, in fact even a job with a good title, but my hope of further advancement at TI was gone."

### General Instrument (1983–1985)
> "General Instrument was mainly about acquisitions, mergers, and then after a business became successful, sale of that business, so that General Instrument could acquire new businesses, and remake them, or improve them and sell them again. It was just very different from what I was used to at Texas Instruments."

## Part 3: Founding TSMC (1985–1987)

Chang was invited by the Taiwan government to lead ITRI (Industrial Technology Research Institute) in 1985. In 1987 he founded TSMC.

> "I wanted to establish a company that would be the foundry for the world's semiconductor industry. At that time, there was no such company."

> "When I founded a semiconductor company, there was only one path: to make it a world-class one."

---

## Part 4: NVIDIA Relationship (1997–Present)

### First Contact (1997)
> "They were a... NVIDIA, the company that Jensen was the CEO of, was a small company, but they had developed some really promising chips. But they were looking for a foundry."

> "I had always told ourselves people that we should never... our salespeople... that we should never be negligent in talking to future customers, even if the customer seems very small."

Chang called Jensen Huang directly after receiving a letter:
> "He immediately impressed me with his articulateness and also impressed me with his optimism."

> "He told me that NVIDIA was in financial difficulties, but the chip that he want now to have foundried would not only save the company, it will also make NVIDIA a major customer of TSMC."

> "His prediction came true. Not only did it solve NVIDIA's financial problems, it prevented bankruptcy."

TSMC went beyond normal service for NVIDIA:
> "I sent two production planners to NVIDIA for about a month... to make sure Jensen got a very nice production plan and on-time delivery."

### Jensen Huang CEO Succession Invitation
Chang seriously considered inviting Jensen Huang to succeed him as TSMC CEO:
> "Although I was quite familiar with the talent within the company, there was one person outside the company who, in terms of character, vision, professional experience, and knowledge in semiconductors—across all criteria—was worth considering as a successor for TSMC. I knew I should speak with him, and that person was Jensen Huang."

> "I spent 10 minutes explaining my deep aspirations of TSMC and how TSMC's package for CEO would be much higher than his income at that time, but he had no question and did not refuse me directly, but simply said, 'I already have a job'."

### 2009 NVIDIA Settlement
When Chang returned as CEO in 2009, he resolved a dispute with NVIDIA over 40nm yield issues:
> "I worked out a number... And I also knew that NVIDIA's customers were after them."

> "Our offer is effective 48 hours. If you do not, there is not gonna be, we're not gonna argue, we're not going to bargain."

Jensen accepted within two days. The settlement was over $100 million.

## Part 5: Employment & Talent Philosophy

Chang's approach to human capital was deeply counter-cyclical:
> "We almost never really fired people even after the probation period."

On refusing performance-based layoffs:
> "People will not respect us if we lay off by performance ratings. Because it's very subjective."

> "If you need the people back within a year, you shouldn't lay off."

> "No matter what happens, you will always need people... semiconductor industry... you will always need people."

---

## Part 6: R&D Budget Philosophy

Chang established an immutable 8% of revenue for R&D:
> "I wanted to make him at ease, you know what I mean? He doesn't have to argue, he doesn't have to request every year."

> "8%, regardless of whether there's a recession or not. And it's just 8% of revenue."

---

## Part 7: 28nm Sweet Spot & Strategic Vision

> "28 is going to be the sweet spot. It's just like tennis racket, you know? You hit the ball with a sweet spot of your racket."

Chang invoked Shakespeare to describe the strategic moment:
> "There's a tide in the affairs of man, which taken at its flood leads on to fortune... this was 28 nanometer was going to be our tide."

---

## Part 8: Capital Expenditure & Board Battles

When the board resisted a major CapEx increase:
> "They didn't even wait until they get to that dinner... We want to talk to the chairman. We don't like this idea at all."

Chang's response:
> "I am still the guy that's responsible for the operation of the company. So you need to let me go ahead with this one."

## Part 9: Apple Relationship

### Meeting Jeff Williams (2010)
Foxconn founder Terry Gou introduced Jeff Williams (then Apple COO) to Chang on November 9, 2010. Williams expressed Apple's desire for TSMC to manufacture iPhone logic chips.

### Tim Cook Meeting (2011)
> Chang met Tim Cook in April 2011 in Silicon Valley. Cook told him not to worry about Intel's competition in foundry services.

Chang reflected on Apple's capacity demands with characteristic financial discipline:
> "I decided that I would take half of what Apple asked for... He said, well, I think you can eliminate your dividend. Your shareholders will understand that. I said, well, no."

### IBM Rejection
Chang rejected IBM's joint R&D proposal without hesitation:
> "IBM still consider themselves to be the senior partner in any partnership they established... We declined without having to think about it at all."

## Part 10: Strategic Humility & Lessons

> "No, I didn't [see how big smartphones would become]... Maybe the business environment... maybe he knew... he had a more detailed visibility than I did."

> "I did not really think that solar or LED would really replace our integrated circuits business. But I knew the integrated circuits business was gonna be great."

On succession mistakes:
> "Sometimes you have to let the CEO make his own mistakes and learn from them... not if the whole company is going down the drain."

On his growth philosophy:
> "Never stop at two" — referring to his conviction that TSMC should continuously push for scale beyond what seemed sufficient.
