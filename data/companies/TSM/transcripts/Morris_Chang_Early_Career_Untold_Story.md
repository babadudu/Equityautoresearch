# Morris Chang — The Untold Story: Early Career & Path to TSMC
**Source**: [Synergy Startup Substack](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)
**Coverage Period**: 1931-1987 (birth through TSMC founding)
**Format**: Biographical analysis with direct quotes from interviews and autobiography

---

## Part 1: Childhood in War (1931-1949)

Morris Chang was born in 1931 in Ningbo, Zhejiang Province, China. His childhood was defined by the Sino-Japanese War, World War II, and the Chinese Civil War.

> "The early part of my life between birth and 18, the background was war, poverty, injustice. So those three things—war, poverty and injustice—they dominated the backdrop, the environment in which I grew up."

This wartime upbringing instilled in Chang a deep resilience and pragmatism that would later define his leadership style at TSMC.

---

## Part 2: American Education (1949-1955)

Chang came to the US in 1949 at age 18. He attended Harvard University for one year, then transferred to MIT, earning a Bachelor's and Master's degree in Mechanical Engineering. He later returned for a PhD in Electrical Engineering at Stanford University.

---

## Part 3: Sylvania Semiconductor (1955-1958)

Chang's first job in semiconductors came at Sylvania:

> "I had four job offers and just by a very lucky break I chose Sylvania Semiconductor—without really knowing the implications, the ramifications. And I worked for Sylvania Semiconductor for three years when I decided, and I was right as later events proved, that Sylvania wasn't going anywhere in semiconductors."

This early career lesson — recognizing when an organization lacks the capability to compete — would recur throughout his career and informed his later assessment of competitors.

---

## Part 4: Texas Instruments (1958-1983) — 25 Formative Years

Chang spent a quarter century at TI, rising from engineer to Senior Vice President of the Worldwide Semiconductor Group. Key developments:

**Learning Curve Pricing**: At TI, Chang deeply studied the "experience curve" — the principle that every doubling of cumulative production volume yields a fixed percentage cost reduction. This became his most powerful strategic weapon at TSMC decades later: TSMC's massive cumulative production volume creates cost advantages that new entrants simply cannot replicate.

**Departure from TI**:
> "I left TI without any job offer, and I left because I felt that essentially I had been put out to pasture at TI. I felt sure that I would still continue to have a job, in fact even a job with a good title, but my hope of further advancement at TI was gone."

Chang was passed over for the CEO role despite being the most qualified semiconductor executive — a decision driven by corporate politics rather than merit.

---

## Part 5: General Instrument (1983-1985)

> "General Instrument was mainly about acquisitions, mergers, and then after a business became successful, sale of that business, so that General Instrument could acquire new businesses, and remake them, or improve them and sell them again. It was just very different from what I was used to at Texas Instruments."

The misfit at GI was productive: it confirmed to Chang that he needed to build something rather than manage acquisitions.

---

## Part 6: Return to Taiwan & TSMC Founding (1985-1987)

In 1985, K.T. Li (李國鼎), Taiwan's "father of economic development," invited Chang to lead ITRI (Industrial Technology Research Institute). Chang accepted, arriving in Taiwan at age 54.

In 1987, at age 56, he founded TSMC with a radical idea: a semiconductor company that only manufactured chips, never designed them. The "pure-play foundry" model was born.

> "I wanted to establish a company that would be the foundry for the world's semiconductor industry. At that time, there was no such company."

---

## Key Themes

1. **Pattern recognition**: Each career stage taught Chang what works (TI's scale economics) and what doesn't (Sylvania's lack of ambition, GI's deal-driven culture)
2. **Late bloomer**: Founded TSMC at 56 — his greatest achievement came in the second half of his career
3. **War-forged resilience**: Childhood in wartime China created a leader who remained calm under extreme pressure
4. **Learning curve obsession**: The TI-era insight about cumulative volume → cost advantage became TSMC's most durable competitive moat
