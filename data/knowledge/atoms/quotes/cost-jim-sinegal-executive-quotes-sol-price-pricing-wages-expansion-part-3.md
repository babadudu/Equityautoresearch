# Jim Sinegal Executive Quotes — Sol Price Influence, Pricing Discipline, Wages & Expansion Philosophy (Part 3)

## Background

James D. Sinegal (born 1936, Pittsburgh, PA) co-founded Costco in 1983 with attorney Jeff Brotman and served as CEO for 29 years (1983–2012), growing the company from one warehouse to 600+ locations and $99B in revenue. His philosophy was shaped almost entirely by Sol Price, founder of FedMart (1954) and Price Club (1976), under whom Sinegal worked for 29 years before striking out on his own.

---

## On Learning Everything From Sol Price

When a journalist noted that Sinegal must have learned a great deal from working under Sol Price for nearly three decades, Sinegal replied:

> "No, that's inaccurate. I didn't learn a lot, I learned everything. Absolutely everything I know, I learned from Sol."
— Jim Sinegal, [Acquired Podcast — Costco, 2023/08/20](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/costco)

Elsewhere, he elaborated on the nature of that apprenticeship:

> "I was like a fly on the wall watching Sol. I watched everything he did and I learned everything from him."
— Jim Sinegal, [Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal)

**Context:** Sinegal began working for Sol Price at FedMart in 1954 at age 18, as a part-time bag boy. He followed Price when Price Club was founded and remained in his orbit until 1983. The mentor-apprentice relationship is the direct origin of every core Costco principle.

---

## Sol Price on His Own Limitations (as Cited in the 1993 Merger Context)

When Price Club merged with Costco in 1993 — effectively the student absorbing the teacher's company — Sol Price offered a characteristically candid self-assessment:

> "We're good at creating businesses, we're not as good at running businesses."
— Sol Price, [Acquired Podcast — Costco, 2023/08/20](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/costco)

**Context:** The merger united ~200 warehouses and ~$16B in combined revenue. Price was reportedly ambivalent about the deal.

---

## On Sustaining Belief in the Business Model Through Skepticism

In the early years, Sinegal faced persistent doubts about the warehouse club model's durability:

> "Everyone is always going to question what business model you have. They're going to say you probably won't be in business six months from now."
— Jim Sinegal, [MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success)

**Context:** Costco reached $1B in revenue within 3 years of founding (1986) and $3B within 6 years — among the fastest ramp-ups in retail history at the time.

---

## On the Four Non-Negotiable Priorities

Sinegal articulated Costco's operating hierarchy in a formulation that became a foundational principle:

> "We think we have four things we have to do. We have to obey the law, take care of our customers, take care of our employees and respect our suppliers. Pretty much in that order."
— Jim Sinegal, [Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal)

**Context:** This ordering — with shareholders conspicuously absent from the list — reflected Sinegal's conviction that financial returns would naturally follow from executing on these four priorities consistently.

---

## On Paying the Highest Wages in Retail (Against Wall Street Pressure)

Analysts routinely questioned Costco's above-market wage structure, arguing it compressed margins unnecessarily. Sinegal's response:

> "No one was going to be able to say we're making money off the backs of our employees, because we were going to pay the highest wages in all of retail."
— Jim Sinegal, [MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success)

He explained the practical reasoning behind the wage philosophy:

> "If I could stock every shelf, ring every register, buy every product and push every cart, I would. But I can't, and you've got to hire people to do it. You want them to do it as well as if you were doing it, and you only do that by teaching."
— Jim Sinegal, [MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success)

**Context:** Sinegal's own CEO compensation was famously modest — a base salary maintained around $350K (plus ~$200K bonus) for most of his tenure — far below peer-company CEO pay. This was an explicit signal that the wage philosophy applied at all levels.

---

## On Delegation and the Risk of Micromanagement

> "The biggest mistake any boss can make is running around and doing everything yourself. If you haven't learned to delegate, you haven't learned a very valuable lesson."
— Jim Sinegal, [MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success)

On internal talent development:

> "We home-grow all of our management."
— Jim Sinegal, [Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal)

---

## On Pricing Discipline as Addictive Temptation

Sinegal's most vivid articulation of why pricing integrity must be structural rather than discretionary:

> "We pass the savings on to the customer, every time. Do you know how tempting it is to make another $7 on a pair? But once you do it, it's like taking heroin. You can't stop."
— Jim Sinegal, [MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success)

**Context:** The "heroin" metaphor refers to the institutional danger of incrementally raising margins above the 14% markup cap. Sinegal argued that every exception erodes the discipline that underpins member trust — and that trust, once broken, is irreversible.

---

## On Expansion Discipline: Not a Kamikaze Pilot

> "We're not kamikaze pilots. We want to do things in a sensible fashion."
— Jim Sinegal, [Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal)

> "We don't have any grand scheme that says, for example, that we have to be in Latin America by the year 2015 or have 1000 Costco's in ten years."
— Jim Sinegal, [Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal)

**Context:** These statements — made during the growth years — capture Sinegal's deliberate rejection of growth targets for their own sake. The discipline of saying no to premature expansion is credited with keeping the unit economics and culture intact as Costco scaled globally.

---

## On Store Visit Philosophy

> "I try to approach the visits from the standpoint of a customer. Does the building have the right goods out? Is it well-stocked and clean and safe?"
— Jim Sinegal, [Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal)

**Context:** Sinegal was legendary for visiting stores personally during his CEO tenure. His store-walk methodology — walking as a customer, not as an auditor — was a discipline he passed down through Costco's management culture.
