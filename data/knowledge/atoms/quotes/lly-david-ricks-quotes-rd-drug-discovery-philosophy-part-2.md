## David Ricks on R&D, Drug Discovery Philosophy & Cost Structure

### The Unique Nature of Drug Discovery

On how pharmaceutical R&D differs fundamentally from all other industries:

> "Our business is one of the few businesses that you do actual discovery... Something never happened before — and now it's happened."

On the binary, stochastic nature of clinical outcomes:

> "It's literally like the card flipping over and you win the hand or you don't."

*Source: [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)*

**Context:** Ricks uses this framing to explain both the extraordinary R&D investment required and the high margins needed to fund sustained innovation. Unlike most industries where incremental engineering produces incremental improvements, drug development has a fundamentally binary outcome structure: massive investments either produce a breakthrough molecule or produce nothing commercially viable.

---

### R&D Investment Scale

On R&D spending as a proportion of revenue:

> "We spend almost 25% of sales this year on R&D."

> "We'll spend $14 billion this year total."

*Source: [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)*

On how this compares to sovereign research budgets:

> "Above Germany and Japan as a nation and headed closer to the NIH."

*Source: [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)*

**Context:** At 2025 revenue of ~$65B, 25% R&D spending = ~$16B, placing Lilly among the top five largest R&D spenders globally. Ricks frequently uses the national comparator to contextualize the scale — framing pharmaceutical pricing as the mechanism that funds scientific infrastructure that governments cannot replicate at equivalent pace or focus.

### The True Cost of Drug Development

On the average cost to bring a new drug to market:

> "The average drug costs three and a half, 4 billion to make."

On where the cost is concentrated:

> "Most of that is the last step." (referring to Phase 3 clinical trials)

*Source: [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)*

**Context:** The $3.5–$4B figure reflects the fully-loaded cost including failures — the capital expenditure on drugs that never reach market must be amortized across successful launches. Phase 3 trials (the final, largest clinical study phase) account for the majority of this cost because they require thousands of patients over multiple years. This cost structure creates the high-margin business model: a drug like Mounjaro, once approved, must generate sufficient profit to fund the next generation of $4B bets.

### Insulin Pricing — Candid Acknowledgment of Industry Dysfunction

On the historical list price vs. net price disconnect in insulin:

> "Our actual net on insulin really hasn't changed. It's like $30–$40, but the list price got up to 275."

On how the incentive system drove this outcome:

> "We competed on this spread... We kept raising the list price."

*Source: [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)*

**Context:** This is a rare candid admission from a pharmaceutical CEO about the PBM rebate system's distorting effect on list prices. Lilly addressed this in 2023 by proactively cutting the list price of Humalog to $35 — the same as the actual net cost for most patients. This action pre-empted regulatory action, improved Lilly's public standing, and positioned Ricks as an unusually transparent industry voice on drug pricing dysfunction.

### Mission-Driven Focus

On why Lilly divested Elanco (animal health) and maintained a narrow focus:

> "That's what Lilly's for. Doing things at scale, but also focusing on where the most suffering is."

*Source: [Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)*

On the company's pioneering role in GLP-1 history:

> "We're the first innovator in the space, having launched the first GLP-1 in the world in mid-2000s."

*Source: [T. Rowe Price, 2025-11](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)*

**Context:** Lilly's first GLP-1 (exenatide, marketed as Byetta, developed jointly with Amylin Pharmaceuticals) launched in 2005 — twenty years before tirzepatide's market dominance. Ricks uses this historical continuity to emphasize that Lilly's GLP-1 leadership was earned through sustained scientific investment over decades, not an overnight breakthrough.
