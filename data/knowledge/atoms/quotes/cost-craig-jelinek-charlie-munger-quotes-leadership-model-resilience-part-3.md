# Craig Jelinek & Charlie Munger Quotes — Leadership Continuity, Pricing Pledges & Business Model Admiration (Part 3)

## Craig Jelinek — Background

Craig Jelinek served as Costco's second CEO from 2012 to 2023. Like his predecessor Jim Sinegal, Jelinek was a "lifer" — he joined Costco in the 1980s and worked his way up from warehouse operations over more than 30 years before assuming the top role. His tenure oversaw revenue growth from ~$99B (2012) to ~$242B (2023), an 11-year CAGR of approximately 8.5%, with international warehouse count expanding from ~150 to ~280 locations. Jelinek was notably reticent about media appearances, making his few interviews particularly significant as primary sources.

---

## On the $1.50 Hot Dog — A Pledge, Not a Policy

Asked in 2013 whether Costco would ever abandon its iconic $1.50 hot dog and soda combination — priced at that level since 1985 — Jelinek made what has since become one of the most frequently cited commitments in American retail:

> "Trust me, that's never going to change. If I have to subsidize it personally, it's not going to change."
— Craig Jelinek, [Motley Fool, 2013/08/01](https://www.fool.com/investing/general/2013/08/01/an-interview-with-costco-ceo-craig-jelinek.aspx)

**Context:** The hot dog price has been held at $1.50 since 1985, surviving multiple inflationary cycles. Costco vertically integrated its hot dog supply chain (producing its own Kirkland Signature hot dogs) partly to enable this price floor. The pledge has been echoed by Ron Vachris: "Not on my watch." The hot dog is widely understood as a symbol of the broader Costco value covenant with members — that profit pressure will not override the membership promise.

## On the U.S. Senate — Defending the High-Wage Strategy (2022)

Jelinek submitted formal written testimony to the U.S. Senate Budget Committee in 2022, defending Costco's wage model as a business — not charitable — imperative. The testimony argued that higher wages reduce turnover, increase productivity, and generate superior long-run returns compared to low-wage retail models.

Source: [U.S. Senate Budget Committee](https://www.budget.senate.gov/download/craig-jelinek-testimony)

## Charlie Munger — On Costco as a Model Institution

Charlie Munger (Berkshire Hathaway vice chairman, 1924–2023) was one of Costco's most prominent long-term admirers. He held Costco shares in his personal portfolio through the Daily Journal Corporation and maintained his position until his death:

> "Costco is one of the most admirable capitalistic institutions in the world."
— Charlie Munger, Daily Journal Annual Meeting, 2020

Source: [YouTube — Charlie Munger on Costco, Daily Journal Annual Meeting](https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=charlie+munger+costco+daily+journal)

**Context:** Munger's admiration was rooted in Costco's structural alignment between employee welfare, member value, and long-run shareholder returns — a model he viewed as demonstrating that ethical business practices and financial outperformance are complementary, not competing. He frequently cited Costco alongside Berkshire-owned companies as examples of the "good business" framework: durable moat, honest management, reinvestment discipline.
