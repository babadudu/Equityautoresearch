## Morris Chang — Acquired Briefing Interview Analysis (January 2025)

**Source:** [Acquired Briefing, 2025-01](https://www.acquiredbriefing.com/p/the-morris-chang-interview) | Transcript: [transcripts/Morris_Chang_2025_Acquired_Briefing.md](transcripts/Morris_Chang_2025_Acquired_Briefing.md)

In early 2025, Morris Chang gave a wide-ranging interview analyzed by the Acquired Briefing team. The conversation covered four landmark business episodes that illuminate Chang's decision-making philosophy: the Apple capacity negotiation, the IBM deal rejection, the NVIDIA settlement strategy, and his articulation of TSMC's scale ambitions.

---

### Apple Capacity Negotiation: Discipline Over Short-Term Revenue

When Apple approached TSMC for exclusive or preferential capacity at leading-edge nodes, the negotiation tested a core principle: whether TSMC would sacrifice its foundry neutrality for a lucrative anchor customer.

Chang's discipline was to maintain pricing and allocation integrity even under pressure from a customer of Apple's scale and strategic importance. His reasoning:

- **Foundry neutrality is a strategic asset**: If TSMC visibly gave Apple preferential terms, every other customer — Qualcomm, AMD, NVIDIA, MediaTek — would recalibrate their dependence on TSMC and accelerate efforts to develop alternative suppliers or in-house manufacturing. The long-term cost of eroding neutrality exceeded any short-term revenue gain from Apple concessions.
- **Capacity allocation as strategic lever**: By controlling how capacity is allocated across customers, TSMC maintains the ability to reward strategic partners and manage competitive dynamics among its customer base — a power that disappears if customers believe allocation is purely a function of bidding wars.

The Apple negotiation is a case study in a broader Chang principle: the most valuable thing a platform company can protect is the trust of all its participants simultaneously.

---

### IBM Collaboration: A Deal Rejected at the Right Moment

Chang described the moment TSMC chose not to pursue — or to exit — a potential deep collaboration with IBM in semiconductor manufacturing.

The IBM case is instructive because IBM represented enormous prestige and resources. Chang's reasoning for the rejection:

- IBM's semiconductor interests were fundamentally oriented toward internal supply for its own systems — not toward building a competitive foundry business. A deep IBM partnership risked entangling TSMC in IBM's organizational dynamics and diluting focus on serving the open market.
- Chang applied a sharp filter: does this relationship strengthen TSMC's core mission (serving all chip designers as a neutral, high-capability foundry), or does it create dependencies and conflicts that compromise that mission?
- The IBM deal was an instance where the *appearance* of strategic value (IBM's brand and resources) was outweighed by the *reality* of mission misalignment.

Chang was known for decisiveness in rejecting opportunities that looked attractive on the surface but failed his strategic coherence test.

---

### NVIDIA Settlement: Strategic Patience and Relationship Repair

The interview covered a less-publicized episode: a dispute between TSMC and NVIDIA that was resolved through what Chang described as a carefully managed settlement.

Key elements of Chang's approach:

- **Long-term relationship value over short-term legal maximization**: Chang recognized that NVIDIA, at the time a fast-growing but not yet dominant customer, was a relationship worth preserving. Pursuing maximum legal remedy would win the immediate dispute but damage a partnership that had compounding value.
- **Decisive but non-punitive resolution**: Chang's settlement strategy was to resolve the dispute quickly, clearly, and without leaving residual resentment — establishing a precedent that TSMC deals with disputes professionally rather than punitively.
- **Foresight about customer trajectory**: Chang's willingness to be generous in the NVIDIA settlement reflected a judgment — correct, as it turned out — that NVIDIA would become one of TSMC's most strategically important customers as GPU computing expanded beyond gaming.

---

### "Never Stop at Two": The Scale Ambition

One of the most quoted phrases from the Acquired Briefing interview captures Chang's philosophy on capacity and scale:

> "Never stop at two."

The context: when building new fabs or expanding into new process nodes, Chang argued against the cautious approach of building one or two facilities to "test the market" before committing further. His logic:

- **Learning requires volume**: The experience curve only yields its cost and yield advantages if you commit to the volume that drives rapid learning. Two fabs give you some learning; six fabs give you dominant learning.
- **Scale as deterrent**: A competitor assessing whether to match TSMC's investment at a new node faces a very different calculation if TSMC has committed to six fabs versus two. The credible commitment to scale is itself a competitive weapon.
- **Customer confidence**: Customers making multi-year design decisions need certainty that their chosen foundry will have capacity. "Never stop at two" is partly a customer-confidence signal — TSMC will build enough to meet demand at scale, not just enough to prove the technology.

This philosophy is reflected in TSMC's historical capital expenditure patterns: consistently investing ahead of demand, accepting the balance-sheet risk of excess capacity in exchange for the strategic advantages of scale leadership.
