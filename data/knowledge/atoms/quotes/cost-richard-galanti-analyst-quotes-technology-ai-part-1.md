# Richard Galanti and Analyst Quotes — AI Strategy, Technology ROI, and Tariff Resilience

This atom collects quotes from Costco's long-tenured former CFO Richard Galanti and external analysts commenting on Costco's operational and financial strategy. Galanti served as CFO from 1984 to 2023 — a 39-year tenure — and was the company's primary voice on financial strategy and technology investment during that period. Michael Lasser is a senior retail analyst at UBS covering Costco and large-cap retail.

---

## Richard Galanti on AI Adoption Maturity

> **"[Costco is in the] early innings [of AI], but is exploring implementations across operational and merchandising departments."**
> — Richard Galanti (then CFO), *Digital Commerce 360*, August 28, 2025 ([source](https://www.digitalcommerce360.com/2025/08/28/how-costco-is-using-ai/))

**Context:** This framing — "early innings" — was Galanti's characterization of Costco's AI journey as of late 2025. Despite the modest self-assessment, Costco had already deployed machine learning at scale: a demand forecasting model for bakery operations piloted in 30 stores in 2018 saved approximately $100 million and was subsequently rolled out system-wide. Galanti's language reflects Costco's characteristic cultural humility about technology — the company rarely leads with innovation as a narrative, preferring to let operational results speak. The "early innings" framing likely also reflects genuine ambiguity about the marginal AI opportunities beyond the initial wave of demand forecasting and inventory optimization.

## Richard Galanti on Technology ROI Covering Labor Costs

> **"[Technology investment has] fully offset wage investments and the impact of extended operating hours."**
> — Richard Galanti (CFO), *Digital Commerce 360*, August 28, 2025 ([source](https://www.digitalcommerce360.com/2025/08/28/how-costco-is-using-ai/))

**Context:** This statement, made in a quarterly earnings call context, is a quantitative claim with significant strategic implications. Costco pays wages well above retail industry averages — starting wages were raised to $20/hour in 2023 and $21/hour in 2024, with senior employees earning $30+/hour. Critics have argued this wage structure, combined with extended operating hours, would compress margins over time. Galanti's assertion that technology and automation investments have "fully offset" these costs suggests that the AI/automation ROI is measurable and material — not a qualitative benefit but a financial offset that appears in operating leverage metrics. This represents a key piece of evidence that Costco's approach of investing simultaneously in human capital (high wages, low turnover) and operational technology (AI demand forecasting, robotics) is not a tradeoff but a complementary strategy.

## UBS Analyst Michael Lasser on Tariff Resilience

> **"[Costco and Walmart will have] the most resilient earnings outlook [amid tariff uncertainty]."**
> — Michael Lasser (UBS analyst), *PYMNTS*, March 2025 ([source](https://www.pymnts.com/earnings/2025/costco-leans-into-memberships-amid-tariff-uncertainty/))

**Context:** Lasser's assessment was made in the context of the 2025 tariff escalation cycle, when the Trump administration's broad tariff regime threatened to significantly impact gross margins across U.S. retail. His selection of Costco and Walmart as the most resilient names reflects several structural advantages: (1) both have highly diversified sourcing bases and the procurement scale to demand supplier cost concessions; (2) both have established private label programs (Kirkland Signature and Great Value/Equate, respectively) that provide sourcing flexibility unavailable with branded merchandise; (3) Costco specifically benefits from its membership fee structure — the ~$4.8B in annual membership revenue, earned at near-zero marginal cost, provides a profit buffer that pure merchandise retailers lack. When tariffs compress product margins, Costco's earnings are partially insulated because ~70% of its operating income comes from the fee line rather than merchandise margin. Lasser's "resilient" framing is consistent with Costco's own Q3 FY2025 communications, in which management emphasized the membership fee as a financial shock absorber.

## Constellation Research on Costco's AI Cultural Approach

> **"[Costco is] approaching AI in a very Costco way."**
> — Constellation Research analyst commentary, 2025 ([source](https://www.constellationr.com/blog-news/insights/costco-approaching-ai-very-costco-way))

**Context:** This characterization — analytical shorthand for Costco's AI investment philosophy — captures the company's distinctive approach: quiet, cost-focused, and ROI-driven rather than headline-seeking. Rather than announcing AI partnerships or generative AI pilots at investor days (as many retailers did in 2023–2025), Costco embedded AI tools into specific operational processes (bakery demand forecasting, member churn prediction, retail media targeting) where measurable savings could be quantified. The company's IT architecture is built on Microsoft Azure with Google Cloud's Vertex AI platform, suggesting a pragmatic multi-cloud approach rather than a deep strategic bet on any single AI ecosystem. The "very Costco way" framing implies: no marquee announcements, no AI-as-narrative, just incremental deployment where the ROI justifies the investment — consistent with Sinegal's decades-old philosophy that any spend not convertible into member value is unnecessary overhead.
