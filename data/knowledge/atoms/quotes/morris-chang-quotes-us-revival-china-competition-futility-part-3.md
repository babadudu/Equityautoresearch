## Morris Chang on US Semiconductor Revival: "A Very Expensive Exercise in Futility"

### On US Domestic Semiconductor Manufacturing (Brookings Institution, April 2022)

Morris Chang offered one of his most pointed critiques of US semiconductor policy in a 2022 Brookings Institution address, arguing that government subsidies alone cannot reconstruct a manufacturing ecosystem that took Taiwan decades to build:

> "A very expensive exercise in futility."

This was Chang's summary verdict on American ambitions to revive domestic semiconductor manufacturing at the frontier. He elaborated that competitive advantage in semiconductor manufacturing is not a product that can be purchased — it is accumulated through years of engineering culture, workforce density, and operational learning:

> "Short-term subsidies cannot make up for long-term competitive disadvantages."

([Source: Brookings Institution, 2022-04](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/Vying-for-Talent-Morris-Chang-20220414.pdf))

Chang's argument is grounded in a structural claim: the United States lost its semiconductor manufacturing base gradually over 30+ years, and rebuilding it requires not just capital but the reformation of an entire talent pipeline, supplier ecosystem, and operational culture — none of which respond to subsidy timelines.

---

### On China as a Competitive Rival (Last Public Speech, April 2023)

In what turned out to be his final major public address, Morris Chang offered a calibrated — and at the time somewhat contrarian — assessment of China's semiconductor capabilities:

> "Mainland is not yet a rival, especially in terms of wafer manufacturing."

([Source: Interconnected Blog, 2023-04](https://interconnected.blog/morris-changs-last-speech/))

This statement is notable for what it does *not* say: Chang did not dismiss China's trajectory or ambitions. His qualifier — "especially in terms of wafer manufacturing" — was precise. China's design capabilities, equipment companies, and materials supply chains are all developing; it is specifically the leading-edge wafer fabrication where the gap remains widest.

Crucially, Chang's risk framing was behavioral rather than technical: he implied that the danger from China does not arise from its imminent ability to replicate TSMC's process nodes, but rather from the possibility that Chinese leadership perceives the *window of technological inferiority as closing*, which could increase the incentive for coercive action before parity is reached.

---

### Interpretive Context

These two statements — on US revival and on China's capabilities — form a coherent geopolitical thesis:

1. **The US cannot quickly reconstruct frontier manufacturing** because competitive advantage in semiconductors is path-dependent, rooted in decades of compounding learning, not injections of capital.
2. **China is not yet a peer rival** in wafer fabrication, but the shrinking gap creates its own strategic risk.
3. **Taiwan's irreplaceability is a time-bound fact**, not a permanent condition — and the most dangerous period may be precisely when rivals conclude the gap is closing.

Chang's view implicitly critiques both US complacency (assuming subsidies solve the problem) and US alarmism (overstating current Chinese capabilities), while pointing to a specific strategic vulnerability: the behavioral responses of rivals who perceive a narrowing window.
