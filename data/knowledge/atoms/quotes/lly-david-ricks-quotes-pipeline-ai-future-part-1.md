## David Ricks — Pipeline Innovation, AI in Drug Discovery & Future of Medicine

**David Ricks** is Chairman and CEO of Eli Lilly and Company. The following quotes are drawn from multiple interviews (2024–2026) covering AI's role in pharmaceutical R&D, Lilly's Alzheimer's franchise, the oral GLP-1 opportunity (orforglipron), addiction medicine, and the decade-long pipeline vision.

---

### On AI in Drug Discovery — Current State

> **"Very early innings."**
> — David Ricks, *Fortune*, November 2024 ([source](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/))

Context: Ricks's baseline characterization of AI's current utility in pharmaceutical R&D. He is neither dismissive nor hyperbolic — AI is generating real value but has not yet transformed the core drug discovery bottleneck.

> **"A few are billion-dollar items with a $250,000 investment."**
> — David Ricks, *Fortune*, November 2024 ([source](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/))

Context: Illustrating the asymmetric return profile of AI in drug discovery — small computational investments can identify molecules or targets that would have taken years of lab work to discover conventionally. Ricks cites this as the defining economic characteristic of AI-assisted R&D.

---

### On AlphaFold and Protein Structure Prediction

> **"It solves a pretty discrete problem really pretty well. Not perfectly, not good enough actually, for drugging the target."**
> — David Ricks, *Fortune*, November 2024 ([source](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/))

Context: Ricks gives a technically precise critique of AlphaFold-style protein structure prediction. While it accurately predicts static protein folding, it doesn't resolve the dynamic conformational complexity needed to design small-molecule drugs that bind specific protein pockets. The gap between "knowing the structure" and "designing a drug" remains large.

### On "Repurposed AI" — LLMs Applied to Genomics

> **"DNA is another language. It's got four letters... You can train it on the human genome and predict things."**
> — David Ricks, *Chief Executive*, 2025 ([source](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/))

Context: Ricks describes Lilly's approach to AI as "repurposed" — taking large language model architectures trained on text and applying the same sequence-learning capability to genomic data. The four-letter DNA alphabet (A, T, G, C) maps structurally to natural language tokens. He sees this as where transformational AI utility in drug discovery will emerge, not in narrow protein-folding tools.

### On the Next Decade — The Brain as the Largest Health Category

> **"The decade of the brain... brain health is probably the biggest category."**
> — David Ricks, *Fortune*, November 2024 ([source](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/))

Context: Ricks identifies neuroscience — particularly Alzheimer's disease — as Lilly's second major growth platform after GLP-1. With Kisunla (donanemab) approved for early-stage Alzheimer's in 2024, Lilly is positioned in what Ricks sees as the largest untapped therapeutic category. Global prevalence: ~55 million Alzheimer's patients, current treatment penetration <1%.

### On Kisunla (Donanemab) and Amyloid Clearance

> **"More than 80% reduction in like a year. Even to undetectable levels."**
> — David Ricks, *T. Rowe Price*, November 2025 ([source](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly))

Context: Ricks is describing Kisunla's clinical performance on amyloid plaque clearance — the primary pathological hallmark of Alzheimer's disease. An 80%+ reduction to "undetectable levels" is a clinically unprecedented outcome in Alzheimer's treatment. The therapeutic question being resolved in ongoing studies is whether amyloid clearance translates to cognitive preservation at scale.

### On Orforglipron — The Oral GLP-1 Manufacturing Advantage

> **"We all know the production systems for injectables have really been stretched — and we continue to build sites. With oral technology we can make more and get this medicine to more people around the world."**
> — David Ricks, *Fortune Well*, January 2025 ([source](https://fortune.com/well/article/eli-lilly-weight-loss-pill-orforglipron-glp1-2026/))

Context: Ricks is making the supply-chain argument for orforglipron. Injectable GLP-1s require specialized peptide synthesis (SPPS) facilities — capital-intensive, yield-constrained, with a 2–3 year build cycle. Orforglipron is a small molecule and can be manufactured using existing pharmaceutical infrastructure globally. This is not just a patient convenience story — it's a fundamental manufacturing cost and capacity story.

> **"The cost of goods is fundamentally different for a small molecule versus a peptide."**
> — David Ricks, *Plain English with Derek Thompson*, February 24, 2026 ([source](https://www.theringer.com/podcasts/plain-english-with-derek-thompson/2026/02/24/the-future-of-glp-1-drugs-and-ai-medicine-with-eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks))

Context: The definitive statement of orforglipron's economic thesis. Peptide synthesis is inherently expensive — solid-phase synthesis is "essentially a blind process" where yield losses compound with each amino acid addition (see Pharma's Almanac). Small molecule synthesis follows standard organic chemistry routes with far higher yields and lower COGS. This enables Lilly to price orforglipron meaningfully lower than injectable tirzepatide while maintaining margins, and to access markets (developing countries, uninsured populations) where injectable pricing is prohibitive.

### On GLP-1 as "Anti-Hedonics" — The Addiction Medicine Frontier

> **"These medicines can be useful for other things we don't think about connected to weight."**
> — David Ricks, *Economic Club of Washington DC*, December 10, 2024 ([source](https://www.economicclub.org/events/david-ricks))

> **"They're reducing that desire cycle. So next year, you'll see Lilly start large studies in alcohol abuse and nicotine use, even in drug abuse."**
> — David Ricks, *Economic Club of Washington DC*, December 10, 2024 ([source](https://www.economicclub.org/events/david-ricks))

Context: Ricks coined the term "anti-hedonics" to describe GLP-1's mechanism in addiction. The GLP-1 receptor is expressed in the brain's reward circuitry; activation reduces craving and hedonic drive — not just appetite but also alcohol craving, nicotine addiction, and potentially substance use disorders. Lilly announced plans to begin Phase 3 studies in alcohol use disorder and nicotine dependence in 2025. The US has ~29 million people with alcohol use disorder and ~31 million tobacco users — a TAM addition that could double GLP-1's long-term market.

### On GLP-1 and Type 2 Diabetes Prevention

> **"This is a profound result."**
> — David Ricks, *CNBC*, August 20, 2024 ([source](https://www.cnbc.com/video/2024/08/20/eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks-on-3-year-obesity-drug-study-this-is-a-profound-result.html))

Context: Responding to the SURMOUNT-1 three-year extension study showing tirzepatide reduces type 2 diabetes incidence by 94% in high-risk obese/prediabetic patients. Ricks describes this as "profound" because it transforms GLP-1 from a treatment drug to a prevention drug — a category reclassification with enormous implications for insurance coverage mandates, public health policy, and long-term TAM expansion.

---

### On Obesity Drug Demand vs. Manufacturing Supply

Note: The following is an industry observation from the research, not a direct Ricks quote, but contextualizes the environment Ricks is navigating:

GLP-1 demand has grown at nearly **three times faster than manufacturing capacity** ([ADA Ventures](https://www.adaventures.com/the-glp-1-economy)), and 65% of potential patients have not initiated therapy due to cost. Ricks's strategy across orforglipron, Medicare negotiations, and manufacturing expansion is a coordinated response to this structural supply-demand imbalance.

### Pharma's Almanac — On GLP-1 Manufacturing Complexity

> **"Essentially blind process, with little real-time feedback or control."**
> — Pharma's Almanac, 2025, describing solid-phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) ([source](https://www.pharmasalmanac.com/articles/how-are-glp-1-therapies-reshaping-the-pharmaceutical-market-and-whats-next))

> **"Not suitable for the economical and sustainable production of large volume peptides."**
> — Matt Bio, Cambrex, quoted in *Pharma's Almanac*, 2025 ([source](https://www.pharmasalmanac.com/articles/how-are-glp-1-therapies-reshaping-the-pharmaceutical-market-and-whats-next))

Context: Industry commentary explaining why orforglipron's small-molecule synthesis route is strategically significant. Traditional SPPS — used to manufacture injectable tirzepatide and semaglutide — accumulates yield losses with each amino acid coupling step. As GLP-1 peptides grow longer (retatrutide is a longer chain than semaglutide), manufacturing complexity and cost increase nonlinearly. The industry is moving toward liquid-phase synthesis (LPPS) and convergent fragment coupling, but these remain far more complex than conventional small-molecule routes — which is exactly what orforglipron uses.
