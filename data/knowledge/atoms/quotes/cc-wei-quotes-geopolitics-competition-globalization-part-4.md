## CC Wei on Geopolitical Neutrality: Walking the US-China Tightrope

**Context:** At the Q4 2024 earnings call, analysts pressed Wei on TSMC's diplomatic positioning after Donald Trump's election victory — specifically whether TSMC had different communication approaches with the incoming versus outgoing US administration regarding Taiwan policy, CHIPS Act conditions, and potential tariffs.

> "We have a very frank and open communication with the current government and with the future one also. I cannot say anything more than that."
— CC Wei, Chairman & CEO, TSMC; [TSMC Q4 2024 Earnings Call Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/01/16/taiwan-semiconductor-manufacturing-tsm-q4-2024-ear/)

**Context:** The measured non-answer is itself revealing. TSMC occupies a unique position in global geopolitics: it is simultaneously a Taiwanese national champion, a beneficiary of US CHIPS Act subsidies ($6.6 billion), a supplier to Chinese companies (though increasingly restricted), and a company whose Taiwan operations are central to US-China strategic competition. Wei's deliberate vagueness reflects the impossible tightrope TSMC must walk — it cannot be seen as aligned with any single government without risking relationships with others. The phrase "I cannot say anything more than that" signals awareness of the geopolitical sensitivity of even describing TSMC's government relationships.

## CC Wei on Competitor Capital Allocation: Money Cannot Buy Technology Leadership

**Context:** In January 2026, Wei was asked about Intel Foundry's prospects and whether Intel's massive capital commitments — supported by US government backing and CHIPS Act incentives — could accelerate its path to competitiveness. Wei's response was unusually direct for a CEO commenting on a named competitor.

> "In the development of semiconductor technology to this point, receiving investment has not helped to improve competitiveness, right?"
— CC Wei, Chairman & CEO, TSMC; [WCCFTech, 2026-01](https://wccftech.com/tsmcs-ceo-says-intel-foundry-wont-be-competitive-by-just-throwing-money/)

**Context:** Wei's comment applies equally to Intel, Samsung, and China's SMIC — all of which have received substantial government investment (CHIPS Act, Korean semiconductor subsidies, China's Big Fund) without closing the technology gap with TSMC. The implicit argument is that semiconductor process technology leadership is accumulated through decades of organizational learning, process engineering culture, customer co-development, and production-floor iteration that cannot be replicated by capital injection alone. TSMC's own history — building from a blank-slate foundry model in 1987 to process leader by the 2010s — supports this thesis: the lead time required to develop production-worthy technology at each node is measured in years, not months, regardless of funding.

## CC Wei on the Improbability of TSMC's Global Expansion

**Context:** In a Fortune Asia profile published in July 2025, Wei reflected on the extraordinary geopolitical transformation of TSMC's role — from a Taiwan-centric contract manufacturer to the centerpiece of US, Japanese, and European semiconductor sovereignty strategies, committing $165 billion to US expansion alone.

> "If someone had told me 10 years ago that I'd meet two presidents in one week to discuss hundreds of billions of dollars in investments to expand our global footprint, I would have thought they were joking."
— CC Wei, Chairman & CEO, TSMC; [Fortune Asia, 2025-07-30](https://fortune.com/asia/2025/07/30/tsmc-ai-boom-cc-wei-wendell-huang-global-500/)

**Context:** The quote captures the historical discontinuity in TSMC's strategic position. Before 2020, TSMC was primarily discussed in terms of its technological roadmap and customer relationships. By 2025, Wei was meeting US President Biden/Trump, Japanese Prime Minister Ishiba/Takaichi, and European Commission leaders within single weeks — not as a supplier seeking government approval, but as an infrastructure provider whose location decisions shape national security. The $1.65 trillion in global revenue that depends on TSMC's advanced processes (per Hudson Institute, 2025) transformed TSMC from a technology company into a geopolitical actor.
