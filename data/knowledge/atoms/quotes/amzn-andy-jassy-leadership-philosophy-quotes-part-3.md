## Andy Jassy on Customer Obsession, Leadership Principles, and Organizational Design

### On the Core Customer Force

Jassy articulated the cultural gravity that underpins Amazon's response to competitive pressure from buyers:

> "There is this very strong force inside the company that we're here to make customers' lives easier and better every day."
> — Andy Jassy, [How Leaders Lead, 2024](https://howleaderslead.com/leaders/227/Andy-Jassy/)

**Context:** Quoted in the context of explaining Amazon's approach to buyer bargaining power — rather than locking customers in or penalizing switching, Amazon focuses on continuously improving the experience. This philosophy is the cultural foundation for Prime's design, AWS's pricing reductions, and the relentless addition of services.

### On Customer Obsession as an Institutional Mandate

Jassy formalized this customer force in a 2022 TIME interview:

> "We exist to make customers' lives better, and we think about it as needing to relentlessly invent to make that so."
> — Andy Jassy, [TIME, Mar 2022](https://time.com/collections/time100-companies-2022/6159473/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-interview/)

**Context:** This quote defines customer obsession not as a service orientation but as an innovation mandate — the reason Amazon must invent is that standing still means failing the customer. It connects the culture of invention directly to the customer mission.

### On Competing Across Many Fronts

In his 2024 shareholder letter, Jassy acknowledged the intensity of Amazon's competitive environment:

> "fiercely competitive market segments, with highly talented, well-funded, ambitious companies."
> — Andy Jassy, [2024 Annual Letter](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/company-news/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-2024-letter-to-shareholders)

**Context:** This rare admission of competitive vulnerability — from a CEO of one of the world's most valuable companies — signals that Amazon does not take its market positions for granted. It also underpins the aggressive capex investment cycle: Amazon is investing heavily precisely because competitors are doing the same.

### On Delegation and Organizational Rhythm

Jassy described the tension between delegation and leadership presence in large organizations:

> "You have to massively delegate to be successful, but you can still be in the weekly rhythms of the business."
> — Andy Jassy, [How Leaders Lead, 2024](https://howleaderslead.com/leaders/227/Andy-Jassy/)

**Context:** This quote captures Jassy's approach to managing Amazon's extraordinary scale — 1.5M+ employees across retail, cloud, logistics, advertising, and media. The phrase "weekly rhythms" refers to Amazon's OP1/OP2 planning cycles, business reviews, and two-pizza team structures that create discipline without requiring top-down micromanagement.

### On Intellectual Humility as a Leadership Practice

Jassy described how he practices Amazon's "Are Right, A Lot" leadership principle:

> "I often question my most closely held beliefs on a particular topic to see if they're really right. Is there another way to think about it? Can we do better for customers?"
> — Andy Jassy, [About Amazon, 2024](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-leadership-principles-video-podcast)

**Context:** This quote reflects the "Are Right, A Lot" LP, which explicitly includes the expectation that strong leaders change their minds when presented with better data. It also demonstrates how the leadership principles are not static slogans but active frameworks Jassy applies to himself.

### On the "Ownership" Leadership Principle

Jassy explained what Amazon means by Ownership — one of the most important of its 16 leadership principles:

> "Yes, it means when you tell your team, you've got something, that you've got it and that you don't have to be reminded constantly of getting it done. But it's also a way of thinking about the business and the company that's more holistic and long-term."
> — Andy Jassy, [About Amazon, 2024](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-leadership-principles-video-podcast)

**Context:** The Ownership LP is central to how Amazon runs highly autonomous teams at scale. This definition distinguishes mere accountability (not dropping the ball) from the deeper expectation — that employees think like shareholders, with a long-term, cross-functional perspective.

### On Organizational Flattening and Bureaucracy Reduction

Jassy described the 2024–2025 org restructuring initiative to reduce managerial overhead:

> "We really want our owners doing the real work. We'd like to try and limit pre-meetings for pre-meetings."
> — Andy Jassy, [HBR, May 2025](https://hbr.org/podcast/2025/05/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-on-agility-ai-strategy-and-the-changing-role-of-managers)

**Context:** Jassy launched an internal "bureaucracy mailbox" that resulted in 375 process improvements. Separately, he announced a target to increase the ratio of individual contributors to managers by 15%, reducing middle management layers. The "pre-meetings for pre-meetings" line is a pointed criticism of the coordination overhead that accumulates in large organizations — and a signal that Amazon's cultural instinct is toward action over process.

### On Trustworthiness as a Leadership Behavior

Jassy defined what Amazon means by "Earn Trust" — another of its leadership principles:

> "What we mean is being honest, authentic, straightforward, listening intently but challenging respectfully if you disagree, and then delivering what you said you would."
> — Andy Jassy, [About Amazon, 2024](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-leadership-principles-video-podcast)

**Context:** This definition is operationally specific — it includes both the interpersonal dimension (honesty, authenticity) and the execution dimension (delivering on commitments). The phrase "challenging respectfully if you disagree" reflects Amazon's expectation that leaders voice disagreement before decisions are made but fully commit once a decision is reached ("Disagree and Commit" LP).
