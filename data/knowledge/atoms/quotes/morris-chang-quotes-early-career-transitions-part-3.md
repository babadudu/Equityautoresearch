## Morris Chang — Early Career, Texas Instruments, and the Path to Founding TSMC

Morris Chang (張忠謀) founded TSMC in 1987 at age 55, after a 25-year career at Texas Instruments and brief stints at Sylvania and General Instrument. The following quotes document his formative career experiences and the circumstances that led him to create the pure-play foundry model.

---

### On His Early Life

**Context:** Multiple interviews and autobiography excerpts. Chang frequently references the disruption of his childhood as formative.

> "The early part of my life between birth and 18, the background was war, poverty, injustice."

**Source:** [Synergy Startup Substack — TSMC: The Untold Story of Morris Chang](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)

**Significance:** Chang was born in 1931 in China, lived through the Second Sino-Japanese War and the Chinese Civil War, and immigrated to the United States in 1949. The childhood of instability and displacement, by his own account, shaped the resilience and long-term orientation that defined his management philosophy.

---

### On Leaving Sylvania for Texas Instruments (1958)

**Context:** Chang joined Sylvania Semiconductor in 1955, one of his first jobs after graduate school. After three years, he assessed the company's semiconductor ambitions and made a decision.

> "Sylvania wasn't going anywhere in semiconductors."

**Source:** [Synergy Startup Substack — TSMC: The Untold Story of Morris Chang](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)

**Significance:** This crisp judgment reflects Chang's career philosophy: assess organizational trajectory early, and move decisively. The move to TI in 1958 placed him at the epicenter of the semiconductor revolution — TI had co-invented the integrated circuit and was competing directly with Fairchild Semiconductor under Jack Kilby's leadership.

---

### On Leaving Texas Instruments (1983)

**Context:** After 25 years at TI, rising from engineer to Vice President of worldwide semiconductor operations, Chang was passed over for the CEO role. He describes his departure with characteristic directness.

> "I left TI without any job offer, and I left because I felt that essentially I had been put out to pasture at TI."

**Source:** [Synergy Startup Substack — TSMC: The Untold Story of Morris Chang](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)

**Significance:** Chang's 25 years at TI gave him deep mastery of semiconductor manufacturing economics, learning curves, and scale effects. His departure without a job lined up — at age 52 — reflects both confidence in his own capabilities and a refusal to accept organizational marginalization. The TI years would prove to be the intellectual foundation for TSMC's manufacturing philosophy.

---

### On His Time at General Instrument (1983–1985)

**Context:** After leaving TI, Chang briefly served as President of General Instrument Corporation. He found the experience culturally jarring.

> "General Instrument was mainly about acquisitions, mergers...it was just very different from what I was used to at Texas Instruments."

**Source:** [Synergy Startup Substack — TSMC: The Untold Story of Morris Chang](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)

**Significance:** The contrast between TI's engineering-driven, manufacturing-focused culture and General Instrument's deal-making orientation reinforced Chang's conviction that great semiconductor companies are built on process excellence and technology depth, not financial engineering. This experience directly informed the culture he would later build at TSMC.

---

### Chronological Summary of Chang's Pre-TSMC Career

| Year | Role | Key Insight Gained |
|------|------|--------------------|
| 1949 | Immigrates to U.S. | —  |
| 1952 | MIT (BS), Stanford (MS); Harvard (1 semester) | —  |
| 1955 | Sylvania Semiconductor | Recognized early-stage organizational stagnation |
| 1958 | Joins Texas Instruments | Immersed in IC revolution; semiconductor manufacturing economics |
| 1983 | Leaves TI (VP, worldwide semiconductor ops) | Scale, learning curves, manufacturing as competitive moat |
| 1983 | President, General Instrument | Rejected M&A-driven model; reinforced manufacturing values |
| 1985 | Director General, ITRI (Taiwan) | Understood Taiwan's industrial development ecosystem |
| 1987 | Founds TSMC | Pure-play foundry model — the synthesis of 32 years of learning |

**Significance of the arc:** Chang's career trajectory was unusual in that he accumulated world-class manufacturing expertise for 25+ years before founding his defining company at age 55. The TSMC founding was not a young person's leap of faith — it was a precisely timed, deeply informed strategic bet on a business model gap that Chang had observed from the inside of the IDM (integrated device manufacturer) world.
