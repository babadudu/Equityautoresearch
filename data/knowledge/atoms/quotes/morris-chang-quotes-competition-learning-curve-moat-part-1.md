## Morris Chang on Learning Curve Moat, Competitive Barriers, and Taiwan's Structural Advantages

### The Learning Curve as an Insurmountable Moat

In his January 2025 Acquired Podcast interview, Chang traced TSMC's competitive moat directly to insights he developed during his career at Texas Instruments: learning curve pricing, where every doubling of cumulative production volume yields a fixed percentage cost reduction. Applied to semiconductor manufacturing, decades of volume accumulation produce cost advantages that cannot be replicated by new entrants regardless of capital:

> "Building a world-class foundry requires decades of learning, billions of dollars, and thousands of the best engineers. You cannot do it overnight."

([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

TSMC's cumulative production volume—spanning 35+ years of continuous leading-edge manufacturing—creates a cost structure that new entrants (including Intel Foundry Services and Samsung Foundry) cannot match on a capital-equivalent basis. The learning curve advantage compounds over time: each generation of process node builds on manufacturing knowledge from prior generations.

### Taiwan's Geographic Compactness as Engineering Leverage

Chang explained why Taiwan's manufacturing advantages are structurally rooted in geography and cannot be transplanted to the United States:

> "Taiwan's advantages that I just talked about are America's weaknesses."

> "TSMC's three manufacturing centers often have thousands of engineers whose location assignment changes but who do not have to move their family."

([Source: Interconnected Blog, 2023-04](https://interconnected.blog/morris-changs-last-speech/))

This is a precise operational point: Taiwan's geographic compactness (the entire island is roughly the size of a US state) means that TSMC engineers can rotate between fab locations without family relocation. In the US, TSMC's Arizona, potential New York, and any future sites would require engineers to uproot their lives to change assignments—reducing flexibility, increasing costs, and slowing knowledge transfer between facilities.

### US Semiconductor Revival: "Expensive Futility"

Chang offered perhaps his most quoted assessment of American semiconductor industrial policy:

> "a very expensive exercise in futility"

And the policy implication:

> "Short-term subsidies cannot make up for long-term competitive disadvantages."

([Source: Interconnected Blog, 2023-04](https://interconnected.blog/morris-changs-last-speech/); [Brookings Institution, 2022-04](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/Vying-for-Talent-Morris-Chang-20220414.pdf))

Chang's argument is structural, not financial: the CHIPS Act's $52.7B cannot create the talent density, engineering mobility, supply chain proximity, and institutional knowledge that Taiwan built over four decades. Subsidies address capital costs but not the deeper competitive substrate.

### Context and Significance

These quotes come primarily from two sources:

1. **Acquired Podcast (January 2025)** — a ~4-hour interview with Chang conducted shortly before his 94th birthday, widely regarded as the most comprehensive public account of TSMC's founding and strategy ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

2. **Chang's "Last Public Speech" (April 2023)** — delivered at an AIT event in Taipei, translated and published by Interconnected Blog; Chang argued that the US semiconductor revival effort fundamentally misunderstands the structural sources of Taiwan's competitive advantage ([Source: Interconnected Blog, 2023-04](https://interconnected.blog/morris-changs-last-speech/))

The Brookings Institution transcript (April 2022, "Vying for Talent" event) provides additional sourcing for the talent-centered arguments ([Source: Brookings Institution, 2022-04](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/Vying-for-Talent-Morris-Chang-20220414.pdf)).

### Investment Relevance

Chang's framing has direct implications for evaluating TSMC's competitive durability:

- **Samsung Foundry** reported ~20% yields on its SF3 (3nm) process in Q2 2024, improving to ~50% by mid-2025—still far below TSMC's ~90% yield on its 3nm process. The yield gap directly reflects the learning curve differential Chang described. ([Source: SamMobile, 2025](https://www.sammobile.com/news/heres-how-shockingly-bad-samsungs-3nm-yields-currently-are/); [Design Reuse, 2025-06](https://www.design-reuse-embedded.com/news/202506089/samsung-foundry-struggles-with-3nm-yields-at-50-as-tsmc-climbs-past-90/))

- **Intel Foundry Services**: despite billions in US government support and decades of IDM history, Intel has struggled to achieve competitive yields and customer traction on leading-edge nodes.

The structural moat Chang identified—accumulated learning, engineering density, geographic compactness—continues to manifest in TSMC's yield leadership and customer adoption metrics.
