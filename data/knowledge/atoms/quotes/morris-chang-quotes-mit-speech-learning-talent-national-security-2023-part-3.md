## Morris Chang at MIT — October 2023: "Learning Is Local"

**Source:** [MIT News, 2023-10-25](https://news.mit.edu/2023/morris-chang-describes-secrets-semiconductor-success-1025) | Transcript: [transcripts/Morris_Chang_2023_MIT_Speech.md](transcripts/Morris_Chang_2023_MIT_Speech.md)

In one of his final public appearances, Morris Chang delivered a lecture at MIT that synthesized his lifelong thinking on what makes semiconductor companies succeed. The speech was structured around several interlocking themes: learning curves, talent geography, and national security.

---

### "Learning Is Local"

The central conceptual claim of the MIT speech was that semiconductor manufacturing knowledge does not transfer easily across borders or organizations — it is embedded in proximity, repetition, and shared context:

> "Learning is local."

This phrase encapsulates Chang's theory of competitive moats in semiconductor manufacturing. Unlike software, where code can be replicated and shipped globally at zero marginal cost, advanced chip fabrication involves thousands of tacit process steps, equipment calibrations, and yield-improvement heuristics that live in the heads and hands of engineers who work together daily. Geographic and organizational clustering is not merely convenient — it is constitutive of the capability itself.

This principle underpins Chang's skepticism about US reshoring: you cannot simply move the *equipment* and expect the *learning* to follow. The learning is embedded in the people, and the people are embedded in Taiwan's ecosystem.

---

### Taiwan's Talent Advantage

Chang was explicit about why Taiwan became the center of global semiconductor manufacturing — and why replicating it elsewhere is harder than critics of Taiwan's dominance appreciate. He pointed to several compounding factors:

- **Workforce density**: Taiwan's engineering talent pool, concentrated in Hsinchu's Science Park, creates an unmatched cluster effect. Engineers move between TSMC, suppliers, and equipment vendors, accelerating knowledge diffusion within the ecosystem.
- **Work culture and dedication**: Chang repeatedly cited the willingness of Taiwanese engineers to work extended hours and remain deeply committed to continuous improvement as a structural advantage — not a cultural stereotype, but an observable operational reality reflected in yield rates and ramp speeds.
- **Cost-competitiveness of talent**: At the time TSMC was built, Taiwan offered world-class engineering talent at a fraction of US or European cost — a gap that has narrowed but not closed.

---

### National Security Framing

Chang was unusually direct in the MIT speech about the national security dimensions of semiconductor geography. He framed TSMC's role not merely as a commercial enterprise but as a strategic asset whose concentration in Taiwan creates both risk and leverage:

- Taiwan's dominance in advanced chips is a deterrent — a "silicon shield" — because any actor seeking to coerce Taiwan must weigh the global economic disruption that would follow.
- But the same concentration is a vulnerability if deterrence fails.
- Chang's implicit prescription: the answer is not to hollow out Taiwan's advantage via forced diversification, but to strengthen the political and military frameworks that make deterrence credible.

---

### Experience Curve Theory Applied to Semiconductors

Chang also revisited his foundational intellectual contribution: the experience curve (learning curve) as applied to semiconductor manufacturing and pricing strategy.

The core insight — developed during his years at Texas Instruments — is that each doubling of cumulative production volume reduces per-unit cost by a predictable percentage (typically 20–30%). This creates a powerful first-mover dynamic: the company that ramps volume fastest achieves the steepest cost decline, which enables lower pricing, which drives further volume — a self-reinforcing flywheel.

At TSMC, Chang institutionalized this thinking not just in pricing but in capacity investment philosophy: commit to building ahead of demand, absorb early losses, and capture the learning that makes you the lowest-cost and highest-yield producer at each new node.

This is why Chang viewed capital investment timing as a strategic weapon, not merely a financial decision.
