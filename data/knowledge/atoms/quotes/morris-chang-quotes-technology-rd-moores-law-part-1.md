## Morris Chang — Technology Vision, Moore's Law & R&D Discipline

These quotes capture Morris Chang's thinking on the nature of Moore's Law (contingent, not inevitable), the strategic timing of process node transitions, and the institutional R&D discipline he hardwired into TSMC to ensure it never lost its technology lead.

---

### On Moore's Law as contingent achievement, not physical law

> "It was at first only a prediction, which might not have been true. In fact, if MOS was not invented... Moore's Law would not have been accurate."

— Morris Chang, last public speech, April 2023. ([Source: Interconnected Blog — Morris Chang's Last Speech, 2023-04](https://interconnected.blog/morris-changs-last-speech/))

**Context:** Chang made this remark while reviewing the 60-year history of semiconductor scaling. His point is subtle and important: Moore's Law is often presented as an iron physical law, but it was originally a *prediction* (Gordon Moore's 1965 observation about transistor density doubling roughly every two years). Whether that prediction remained accurate depended on contingent inventions — particularly the transition from bipolar transistors to MOS (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor) transistors, which enabled lower power density and continued miniaturization. Chang is making the case that TSMC's existence is part of the human effort that *made* Moore's Law true, rather than simply riding an inevitable natural curve. This framing positions TSMC not as a commodity manufacturer but as an active co-creator of the industry's most fundamental growth driver.

---

### On the 28nm node as a strategic "sweet spot" — and Shakespeare

> "There's a tide in the affairs of man, which taken at its flood leads on to fortune."

> "28 is going to be the sweet spot. It's just like tennis racket — you hit the ball with a sweet spot."

— Morris Chang, Acquired Podcast, January 2025, recounting his 28nm strategy. ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang); [alternate transcript](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

**Context:** Chang quoted Shakespeare's Julius Caesar (Act IV) to explain TSMC's strategic timing on 28nm — the process node that dominated smartphone chip manufacturing from roughly 2011 through 2016 and remained in production for mobile and IoT applications for over a decade. Chang recognized that 28nm sat at the intersection of: (1) adequate density for smartphone-era logic, (2) acceptable yield at scale, (3) reasonable CapEx per wafer relative to revenue, and (4) a long product lifecycle that would generate sustained capacity utilization. The Shakespeare quote signals that he viewed the 28nm window not as something TSMC stumbled into, but as a deliberate strategic bet — timed to flood tide, taken boldly. TSMC's aggressive 28nm capacity build in 2010–2012 locked in Apple and Qualcomm as anchor customers at that node and established TSMC's foundry dominance through the entire smartphone supercycle.

**Later invocation:** Chang also used the Shakespeare quote to describe TSMC's EUV ramp timing (N5 in 2020) — suggesting "taken at the flood" is his personal mental model for identifying and committing to inflection-point opportunities.

### On R&D as a fixed institutional commitment

> "8%, regardless of whether there's a recession or not. And it's just 8% of revenue."

> "I wanted to make him at ease, you know what I mean? He doesn't have to argue, he doesn't have to request every year."

— Morris Chang, Acquired Podcast, January 2025, explaining why he set TSMC's R&D budget at a fixed 8% of revenue. ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

**Context:** Chang established early in TSMC's history that R&D spending would be a *fixed percentage* of revenue — 8% — regardless of economic conditions. The "him" in the second quote refers to the head of R&D, whom Chang wanted to free from annual budget battles so that long-cycle process development could proceed without interruption. This is institutionalized counter-cyclicality: when revenue falls (as in 2001, 2009, 2015), absolute R&D dollars shrink, but the *commitment* to research as a proportion of the business never wavers. Chang's insight was that semiconductor process technology is a multi-year, highly sequential investment — cutting R&D in a downturn doesn't just save money in the current year, it *delays* the next process node and creates a permanent technology gap vs. competitors who maintained investment. This 8% discipline is a primary reason TSMC has maintained a technology lead over Samsung Foundry and Intel Foundry for multiple consecutive process generations.

**Current status:** TSMC continues this discipline; 2024 R&D spending was approximately NT$240 billion, representing ~8% of revenue.

### On learning curve as a manufacturing epistemology

> "It works, the learning curve, the experience curve, it works only when you have a common location. Learning is local."

— Morris Chang, MIT speech, October 2023. ([Source: MIT News, 2023-10-25](https://news.mit.edu/2023/morris-chang-describes-secrets-semiconductor-success-1025))

**Context (technology framing):** From a technology development perspective, Chang's "learning is local" principle explains why TSMC maintains the world's densest concentration of process engineers in the Hsinchu-Tainan corridor. When a new process node is introduced, the learning curve — the empirical reduction in cost and improvement in yield as cumulative wafer volume doubles — only materializes when engineers, equipment, and process recipes are co-located. A new fab in Arizona runs the same equipment but starts at the bottom of the local learning curve; the process knowledge embedded in Taiwan's workforce cannot be simply copied into a document and transferred. This is a fundamental argument for why TSMC's Taiwan fabs will maintain cost and yield advantages over overseas fabs for many years regardless of capital investment parity.

### Synthesis

Chang's technology quotes reveal a mind that operates simultaneously at the level of physics (Moore's Law contingency), strategy (node sweet spots and timing), and institutional design (fixed R&D ratios). His deepest insight is perhaps that technology leadership is not primarily an engineering achievement — it is a *commitment architecture*. By making R&D non-negotiable, by betting boldly on inflection points (28nm, EUV), and by understanding that manufacturing knowledge is irreducibly local, Chang built a set of organizational rules that compound over time into a moat that money alone cannot replicate.
