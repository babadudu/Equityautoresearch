## Morris Chang — Customer Philosophy, Pricing & ASML Partnership

These quotes illuminate Morris Chang's understanding of what it means to be a truly customer-centric foundry, how TSMC prices its services, and how TSMC co-evolved with ASML to build the EUV supply chain that underpins modern advanced manufacturing.

---

### On the foundry compact with customers

> "The biggest discovery was who the customers were."

— Morris Chang, last public speech, April 2023. ([Source: Interconnected Blog — Morris Chang's Last Speech, 2023-04](https://interconnected.blog/morris-changs-last-speech/))

**Context:** Chang's reflection that the most important strategic insight of his foundry career was recognizing that TSMC's customers — fabless chip designers — are not merely buyers but partners whose commercial fate is completely intertwined with TSMC's manufacturing excellence. TSMC never designs its own chips, never competes with customers, and treats customer IP with strict confidentiality. This non-compete covenant is the foundational trust mechanism that allows Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, and Qualcomm to share their most sensitive next-generation chip designs with TSMC years before product launch.

---

### On pricing philosophy — value, not cost-plus

> "Unwilling to take the price that customers can afford means the same that they were not good at doing dedicated foundry service."

> "What TSMC is good at is making reasonable profits out of the price that is acceptable to the customer."

— Morris Chang, autobiography (Volume II). ([Source: TechSoda, Morris Chang autobiography analysis](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography))

**Context:** These two sentences together define TSMC's pricing compact: the foundry must be willing to accept the price the customer can afford (i.e., the price that makes the customer's business viable), and in return TSMC's job is to drive costs down via the learning curve until that customer-acceptable price generates a reasonable margin. Chang was criticizing IDMs who attempted foundry services but priced to cover high internal cost structures — effectively pricing themselves out of the foundry market. TSMC's model inverts this: start from what the customer can pay, then engineer costs down to earn margin. This value-based pricing (rather than cost-plus) is what enables TSMC to maintain deep customer relationships even as it raises prices for leading-edge nodes.

### On Apple's migration to TSMC — pricing as covenant

> "Unwilling to take the price that customers can afford means the same that they were not good at doing dedicated foundry service."

— Morris Chang, autobiography (Volume II), in the context of Apple's decision to leave Samsung manufacturing for TSMC. ([Source: TechSoda, Morris Chang autobiography analysis](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography))

**Context:** Chang's autobiography reveals that in 2010, Jeff Williams (Apple's VP of Operations at the time) approached TSMC expressing Apple's desire for TSMC to manufacture iPhone logic chips. In 2011, Tim Cook personally told Chang he did not need to worry about Intel as a competitor for Apple's chip manufacturing. Chang interpreted Apple's migration as validation of the pure-play foundry compact: Apple trusted TSMC precisely because TSMC would never design a competing chip, would never leak IP to Samsung (which was simultaneously Apple's chip manufacturer and display supplier and smartphone competitor), and would commit manufacturing capacity to Apple's roadmap. The pricing philosophy was central to this trust — TSMC priced to make Apple's chip economics viable, not to maximize short-term margin.

### On TSMC and ASML as co-evolutionary partners

> "TSMC and ASML were established almost at the same period, and we grew up together and treated each other as partners."

— Morris Chang, autobiography (Volume II). ([Source: TechSoda, Morris Chang autobiography analysis](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography))

**Context:** ASML was founded in 1984 (as a spin-off from Philips), three years before TSMC's 1987 founding. Chang's framing of the two companies as having "grown up together" reflects a deep supply-chain co-evolution: TSMC became ASML's largest single customer (representing 30%+ of ASML's revenue) and provided critical feedback that shaped EUV tool development. ASML's EUV lithography machines — each priced above $350 million USD, requiring years of lead time — could not have been commercialized without TSMC's commitment to deploying them at high volume. Conversely, TSMC's N7+/N5/N3/N2 technology roadmap depends entirely on ASML's EUV tool generations.

This co-evolutionary relationship is strategically significant in two ways: (1) TSMC has *purchasing priority* over Samsung and Intel for ASML's newest tools due to its largest-customer status; (2) TSMC and ASML's shared institutional history creates a level of process-development collaboration that is difficult for newer foundry entrants to replicate.

### On the proof of customer loyalty — NVIDIA's near-bankruptcy and rescue

> "His prediction came true. Not only did it solve NVIDIA's financial problems, it prevented bankruptcy."

— Morris Chang, Acquired Podcast, January 2025, referring to TSMC's decision to support NVIDIA's early graphics chip manufacturing in the late 1990s. ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

**Context:** In NVIDIA's early years (circa 1998–2000), the company faced severe cash constraints and yield challenges with its first-generation GPU designs. TSMC committed manufacturing support that stabilized NVIDIA's production economics. Chang noted that NVIDIA — like many fabless companies in the late 1990s — had initially tried a multi-source manufacturing strategy, but the yield and cost gap between TSMC and alternative foundries was too large to sustain. The consolidation of leading fabless companies to a single primary foundry (TSMC) was not contractually mandated but emerged organically from quality and cost outcomes. This revealed-preference dynamic — customers choosing TSMC not because they must but because alternatives underperform — is the operational definition of TSMC's moat.

---

### Synthesis

Chang's customer and partnership philosophy rests on three interlocking principles: (1) the foundry must never compete with customers (non-compete covenant as trust infrastructure); (2) pricing must be anchored to customer value creation, not TSMC's cost structure; and (3) supplier relationships (particularly ASML) must be treated as strategic partnerships, not transactional vendor arrangements. These principles are not abstract values — they are the specific mechanisms through which TSMC converted a 1987 startup into the world's most critical manufacturer, trusted with the designs of Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, and dozens of other companies simultaneously.
