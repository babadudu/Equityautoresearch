## David Ricks — GLP-1 Market, Pricing, Competition & Compounders

**David Ricks** is Chairman and CEO of Eli Lilly and Company. The following quotes are drawn from multiple interviews (2023–2026) covering GLP-1 market sizing, competitive dynamics, compounding pharmacy threats, Medicare pricing negotiations, and Lilly's global access strategy.

---

### On the Global Obesity TAM

> **"Almost one billion people could benefit from obesity medications."**
> — David Ricks, *CNBC Mad Money*, August 8, 2023 ([source](https://www.cnbc.com/video/2023/08/08/eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks-almost-one-billion-people-could-benefit-from-obesity-medications.html))

Context: Made in August 2023, before the full commercial launch of Zepbound, this was Ricks's first major public statement anchoring the obesity TAM at near-1 billion people globally. He was laying the groundwork for investor acceptance of Lilly's massive capex program ($50B capacity expansion).

> **"There's a billion people globally with obesity within five years."**
> — David Ricks, *Chief Executive*, 2025 ([source](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/))

Context: By 2025, Ricks refined the same thesis with a time horizon — the addressable population will reach 1 billion within five years due to demographic trends and rising obesity prevalence in developing markets. This is distinct from current diagnosed patients; it reflects the structural demand ceiling.

---

### On Welcoming Competition

> **"Someone has to do this work."**
> — David Ricks, *T. Rowe Price*, November 2025 ([source](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly))

Context: When asked about the 193 obesity drug candidates in development globally as of late 2025, Ricks expressed an unusually collaborative stance. His argument: the obesity market is so vastly underserved (only ~6–7M Americans treated out of 110M obese) that new entrants expand the market rather than take share. Market creation, not market defense, is his frame.

---

### On the Compounding Pharmacy Threat

> **"There's just too much opaque pricing and profit taking."**
> — David Ricks, *Washington Reporter*, March 2025 ([source](https://washingtonreporter.news/p/interview-eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks))

Context: Referring to the pharmacy benefit manager (PBM) and specialty pharmacy ecosystem that captures significant margin between manufacturer net price and patient out-of-pocket cost.

> **"Shadow generic industry undermining patents itself... You've created basically a shadow generic industry."**
> — David Ricks, *Cheeky Pint / All-In Summit*, 2024 ([source](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp))

Context: Ricks's sharpest language on compounding pharmacies. During the 2023–2025 period when FDA listed tirzepatide and semaglutide as "in shortage," compounding pharmacies legally produced copies. Ricks argues this constitutes patent infringement at scale — a "shadow generic industry" that undermines the IP framework that funds drug innovation.

> **"There may be up to one or two million Americans using these so-called drugs. They're not actually proven."**
> — David Ricks, *Washington Reporter*, March 2025 ([source](https://washingtonreporter.news/p/interview-eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks))

> **"Most of them are sourced out of Chinese labs that aren't even approved in China, let alone in the U.S."**
> — David Ricks, *Washington Reporter*, March 2025 ([source](https://washingtonreporter.news/p/interview-eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks))

Context: Ricks raises a patient safety argument against compounders: unregulated API sourcing from unapproved Chinese facilities, unknown impurity profiles, and no clinical validation. He is simultaneously lobbying the FDA to remove GLP-1 drugs from the shortage list (achieved April 2025) to eliminate the legal basis for compounding.

### On Medicare Pricing Negotiations

> **"We're excited to get going on that; that'll start in the spring, and we lowered our prices to get that done."**
> — David Ricks, *T. Rowe Price*, November 2025 ([source](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly))

Context: Following the November 2025 Medicare Part D negotiation agreement that set Zepbound's monthly cost at $245 (patient co-pay $50), down from ~$1,000/month list price — a ~75% reduction. Ricks frames this as a strategic win: volume gain (20–30M new Medicare-eligible patients) outweighs the price concession.

> **"20 to 30 million new patients... Just on Medicare."**
> — David Ricks, *T. Rowe Price*, November 2025 ([source](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly))

Context: Quantifying the upside from Medicare GLP-1 coverage expansion. Prior to this agreement, Medicare Part D did not cover obesity drugs (only diabetes). The volume uplift from Medicare access is Lilly's core "price-for-volume" trade-off argument.

---

### On Insurance Coverage as the Structural Bottleneck

> **"There's a blurry picture here of insurance and health care schemes that cover the comorbidities of obesity."**
> — David Ricks, *T. Rowe Price*, November 2025 ([source](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly))

Context: Ricks identifies the core access paradox: insurers cover downstream consequences of obesity (heart disease, diabetes, sleep apnea) but historically refused to cover the obesity treatment itself. This creates a perverse incentive structure where the chronic disease is treated but the root cause is not.

---

### On International Pricing Strategy

> **"A company to government discussion we're never going to win, but a government to government discussion, we could win."**
> — David Ricks, *Washington Reporter*, March 2025 ([source](https://washingtonreporter.news/p/interview-eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks))

Context: On global market access for GLP-1s in countries where government health systems are the dominant payer. Ricks acknowledges Lilly has no leverage in bilateral pharma-to-government price negotiations in countries like the UK or Germany, but believes US government diplomatic pressure can unlock better terms for American pharmaceutical companies in foreign markets.

---

### On Tirzepatide's Clinical Breakthrough

> **"20% of body weight."**
> — David Ricks, *Fortune*, November 2024 ([source](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/))

Context: Citing tirzepatide's average weight loss in SURMOUNT-1 trials — approximately 20% of body weight over 72 weeks, compared to ~15% for semaglutide. This difference is clinically and commercially significant.

> **"People lose the weight and they don't feel miserable."**
> — David Ricks, *Cheeky Pint / All-In Summit*, 2024 ([source](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp))

Context: On tolerability differentiation. Ricks argues that tirzepatide's dual GIP+GLP-1 mechanism produces less nausea than single-receptor semaglutide, improving adherence. The 50% first-year dropout rate for GLP-1s broadly is partly attributable to GI side effects; tirzepatide's profile is incrementally better.

---

### On the SURMOUNT-1 Three-Year Results

> **"This is a profound result."**
> — David Ricks, *CNBC*, August 20, 2024 ([source](https://www.cnbc.com/video/2024/08/20/eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks-on-3-year-obesity-drug-study-this-is-a-profound-result.html))

Context: Responding to the SURMOUNT-1 three-year extension data showing tirzepatide reduces the risk of developing type 2 diabetes by 94% in people with obesity and prediabetes. This repositions Zepbound from a weight-loss drug to a chronic disease prevention drug — with major implications for insurance coverage arguments and regulatory labeling.

### On GLP-1 Pipeline Breadth

> **"60+ cancer studies"** and exploration of GLP-1 in **"addiction and behavioral health"**
> — David Ricks, *Chief Executive*, 2025 ([source](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/))

Context: Ricks disclosed that Lilly is running over 60 cancer studies and actively exploring GLP-1's application in addiction medicine. This signals that GLP-1's addressable market is substantially larger than current obesity/diabetes estimates suggest.
