## Morris Chang — Globalization, Identity, Integrity Culture, and TSMC's Core Values

Morris Chang (張忠謀), TSMC's founder, has been a consistent voice on the intersection of geopolitics, cultural identity, and corporate integrity. The following quotes span 2005–2025 and reflect themes that directly shaped TSMC's strategic and cultural DNA.

---

### On the Death of Globalization

**Context:** April 2022, Brookings Institution event. Chang made this statement as the U.S.-China technology decoupling was accelerating following COVID supply chain disruptions, the CHIPS Act debate, and rising geopolitical tension over Taiwan.

> "Globalization is almost dead. Free trade is almost dead."

**Source:** [Brookings Institution, 2022-04](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/Vying-for-Talent-Morris-Chang-20220414.pdf)

**Significance:** This was not a rhetorical flourish — Chang made the statement as a strategic fact that TSMC had already internalized. The judgment directly drove TSMC's decision to accelerate overseas fab construction: if globalization was dead, a single-location manufacturing strategy was a political and business liability. Chang's candor on this point was notable given that most multinational CEOs were still publicly defending globalization at the time.

---

### On His Personal Identity

**Context:** A 2024 essay and interview discussing Chang's complex relationship with Taiwan, the United States, and China. Chang, who immigrated to the U.S. in 1949, lived in the U.S. for decades before returning to Taiwan to found TSMC at the government's invitation.

> "My identity has always been an American. There is nothing else."

But simultaneously:

> "I am from the United States, and my dream is to build a wafer fab in the United States."

**Source:** [Andy Lin's Blog / Granite Firm, 2024-12-28](https://www.granitefirm.com/blog/us/2024/12/28/morris-changs-controversial/)

**Significance:** The juxtaposition reveals the genuine complexity of Chang's position. He is an American citizen who built Taiwan's defining company, which now sits at the center of a geopolitical contest between the U.S. and China. His American identity is sincere — but it does not simplify the political calculus for TSMC or Taiwan. These statements became controversial in Taiwan, where some interpreted them as evidence that Chang prioritized U.S. interests over Taiwan's.

---

### On What Made TSMC Successful

**Context:** December 2024, CommonWealth Publishing interview, reflecting on TSMC's nearly four decades.

> "A pioneering business model executed to perfection and relentless innovation through proprietary technologies."

**Source:** [CommonWealth Publishing, 2024-12-02](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3849)

**Significance:** Chang identifies two non-negotiable factors: (1) the foundry model itself — the insight that chip designers would rather outsource manufacturing than build their own fabs, and (2) execution quality — the manufacturing precision, yield rates, and process leadership that made TSMC the only credible choice for leading-edge logic. Either without the other would have been insufficient.

---

### On Layoffs During the 2008–2009 Financial Crisis

**Context:** Acquired Podcast (January 2025), Chang reflecting on the Global Financial Crisis when TSMC — like many manufacturers — faced significant demand destruction. He describes the unconventional decision not to use performance ratings as the basis for layoffs.

> "People will not respect us if we lay off by performance ratings. Because it's very subjective."

> "If you need the people back within a year, you shouldn't lay off."

**Source:** [Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang)

**Significance:** This personnel philosophy is counterintuitive. Most companies use recessions to cull underperformers; Chang rejected this on two grounds: (1) performance ratings are too subjective to be a fair basis for life-altering decisions, and (2) if demand will recover within a year, the transaction costs of layoff-and-rehire (severance, recruiting, training) exceed the payroll savings. The practical outcome was that TSMC retained its core engineering talent through the 2008–2009 downturn, entering the 28nm and then FinFET era with an intact, experienced workforce.

### On Enforcing Integrity — The Ultimate Sanction

**Context:** Global Views Monthly, 2005. Chang was articulating TSMC's integrity culture to a Taiwanese business audience, explaining what happens when the principle is violated at the executive level.

> "If there is any executive violating this very principle, with a very heavy heart I must personally handle the situation and ask him/her to resign."

**Source:** [Global Views Monthly, 2005](https://exampleof2015.wordpress.com/2016/03/03/integrity-we-should-carry-on-throughout-all-of-our-life/)

**Significance:** The phrase "with a very heavy heart" is crucial — it signals that the enforcement is not punitive pleasure but a painful obligation. And "personally handle" indicates that integrity enforcement cannot be delegated to HR or legal. The combination of genuine reluctance and firm action is the structure of effective deterrence: it signals that violations will be caught and will have real consequences, even when the violator is someone the CEO values.

---

### On TSMC as a "Pure Land" of Social Values

**Context:** Global Views Monthly, 2005. Chang articulating the ultimate purpose of TSMC beyond commercial success.

> "It is my goal to make TSMC the pure land of maintaining the core value of the society."

**Source:** [Global Views Monthly, 2005](https://exampleof2015.wordpress.com/2016/03/03/integrity-we-should-carry-on-throughout-all-of-our-life/)

**Significance:** The Chinese concept of "pure land" (淨土) has Buddhist connotations — a space free of corruption and moral pollution. Chang is invoking a standard far above typical corporate governance: not "we comply with the law" but "we are a refuge of ethical behavior in a world where such behavior is rare." This framing positioned TSMC's integrity culture not as a compliance obligation but as a founding purpose.

**Institutional outcomes of this culture (as of 2025):**
- 11 consecutive years in the top 5% of Taiwan Stock Exchange Corporate Governance Evaluations
- 77,293 employees completed annual integrity and compliance training in 2025, with a 100% pass rate
- Ombudsman system (whistleblower channel) with direct reporting to the Board of Directors, bypassing management

**Sources:** [TSMC IR Corporate Governance](https://investor.tsmc.com/english/corporate-governance-officer); [TSMC 2024 Annual Report Ch.7](https://investor.tsmc.com/static/annualReports/2024/english/pdf/2024_tsmc_ar_e_ch7.pdf)
