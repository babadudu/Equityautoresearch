## David Ricks — Diversity, Leadership Philosophy, Succession & Insulin Pricing Candor

### On Diversity and Innovation

In a 2019 Posse Foundation interview, Ricks reflected on the relationship between diversity and leadership:

> "The status quo will always be the status quo unless some force moves it."

> "New ideas only come from different ideas, so we need more diversity."

**Source:** [Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)

These quotes reflect Ricks's conviction that organizational inertia is the default and that genuine diversity — of background, perspective, and experience — is the primary mechanism for disrupting it. He frames diversity not as a compliance or reputational goal, but as a prerequisite for innovation.

---

### On Leadership by Example

In the same Posse Foundation interview, Ricks emphasized that leadership influence flows from modeling behavior rather than instruction:

> "What you model doing — the shadow you cast — matters."

**Source:** [Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)

This principle aligns with Ricks's broader management philosophy at Lilly — a culture where the CEO sets visible examples on R&D commitment, patient focus, and intellectual honesty — rather than relying on top-down mandates.

---

### On Career Advice for Young Professionals

Also from the Posse Foundation interview, Ricks offered advice reflecting his own career trajectory across multiple geographies and functions within Lilly:

> "Have a sense of adventure! Move around, and don't be afraid to change."

**Source:** [Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)

Ricks joined Lilly fresh out of Indiana University's Kelley School of Business and spent over two decades rotating through commercial, international, and general management roles before becoming CEO in 2017. His advice mirrors a career built on geographic mobility and functional breadth.

### On CEO Succession

In the same 2019 Posse Foundation interview, Ricks articulated his personal benchmark for a successful tenure:

> "I'll know I did a good job because we found a successor who can do it better."

**Source:** [Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)

**Context:** As of early 2026, Lilly has not publicly disclosed a formal succession plan. The departure of Anat Ashkenazi (former CFO, now at Alphabet) was considered a significant bench loss. Remaining internal candidates include Patrik Jonsson (EVP, President of Lilly USA) and Daniel Skovronsky, M.D., Ph.D. (EVP, Chief Scientific & Medical Officer), though neither is considered a clear default successor. Analysts rate Lilly's bench depth as **mid-to-low** — Ricks's personalized leadership style (direct approval of all senior hires across a 138-person executive team) creates above-average key-person risk. That said, Lilly has operated as a 150-year institution with 11 CEO transitions, suggesting institutional succession mechanisms exist even without public disclosure.

### On Insulin Pricing — Rare Industry Candor

In a 2024 Cheeky Pint / All-In interview, Ricks made an unusually candid admission about how the pharmaceutical industry — including Lilly — contributed to the insulin pricing crisis:

> "Our actual net on insulin really hasn't changed. It's like $30–$40, but the list price got up to $275."

> "We competed on this spread... We kept raising the list price."

**Source:** [Cheeky Pint/All-In, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)

**Context:** The "spread" referred to is the gap between the list (sticker) price and the net price after rebates paid to pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs). The industry practice was to raise list prices to offer larger rebates in exchange for preferred formulary placement — a dynamic that harmed uninsured patients paying out-of-pocket. Following this acknowledged dysfunction, Lilly in 2023 proactively cut the list price of Humalog to $35, a decision Ricks framed as both ethically correct and strategically necessary for rebuilding public trust. This move preceded broader legislative pressure on insulin pricing.
