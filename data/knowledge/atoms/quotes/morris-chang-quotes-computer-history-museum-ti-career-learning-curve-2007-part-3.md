## Morris Chang — Computer History Museum Oral History (2007)

**Source:** [Computer History Museum Oral History, 2007](https://www.computerhistory.org/collections/catalog/102658129) | Transcript: [transcripts/Morris_Chang_2007_Computer_History_Museum.md](transcripts/Morris_Chang_2007_Computer_History_Museum.md)

In 2007, the Computer History Museum conducted a detailed oral history with Morris Chang covering his 25-year career at Texas Instruments, the intellectual origins of learning-curve pricing, and the early history of the global semiconductor industry. This interview is a foundational primary source for understanding how Chang's business philosophy developed before he founded TSMC.

---

### The 25-Year Texas Instruments Career

Chang spent 1958–1983 at Texas Instruments — a formative period that encompassed the birth of the integrated circuit industry. During this time, TI was one of the defining competitive battlegrounds of early semiconductors, competing fiercely with Fairchild, Intel (later), and Japanese manufacturers.

Chang rose through TI's engineering and general management ranks, eventually running the semiconductor business. His time there gave him:

- Deep hands-on experience with high-volume manufacturing processes
- Exposure to the economics of capital-intensive scale businesses
- Direct observation of how cost structure, pricing strategy, and volume interact to determine market outcomes
- Early encounters with the talent and culture factors that separate high-yield from low-yield operations

The TI years were also where Chang developed his most influential intellectual framework: the experience curve applied to semiconductor pricing.

---

### The Learning Curve Pricing Origins

In the Computer History Museum interview, Chang described how he arrived at the strategic insight that defined TI's — and later TSMC's — competitive philosophy:

The standard industry practice in early semiconductors was to price based on *current* cost plus margin. Chang observed that this approach systematically underinvested in volume growth: it set prices high (because current costs were high), which limited demand, which slowed learning, which kept costs high — a vicious cycle.

Chang's counter-insight was to price based on *anticipated future cost* — i.e., to project forward along the learning curve and price aggressively enough to capture the volume that would drive you to that future cost point. This meant:

- Accepting losses or thin margins early in a product cycle
- Committing to cost reduction roadmaps before they were proven
- Using pricing as a strategic weapon to crowd out competitors who couldn't match the investment discipline

This approach became TI's competitive weapon in memory chips and calculators during the 1970s — and later became the foundational pricing philosophy Chang embedded in TSMC's capacity-investment strategy.

---

### Early Semiconductor Industry History

The oral history also covers Chang's observations on the formative dynamics of the global semiconductor industry:

- **The Fairchild diaspora**: Chang witnessed firsthand how the exodus of engineers from Fairchild Semiconductor seeded Silicon Valley's entrepreneurial ecosystem — an early example of how talent clustering and mobility creates industry-wide compounding.
- **Japanese competitive threat**: Chang's era at TI overlapped with Japan's aggressive push into memory semiconductors in the 1970s–80s, which he observed up close. Japan's strategy — government-coordinated investment, patient capital, relentless cost reduction — prefigured the debates about industrial policy that would later surround TSMC and US CHIPS Act.
- **The importance of general management in technical industries**: Chang noted that one of the structural weaknesses he observed in many semiconductor companies was the separation of technical leadership from business strategy — engineers who didn't understand economics, or managers who didn't understand process. His own career was defined by bridging this gap.

---

### Legacy of the TI Years for TSMC

The Computer History Museum interview makes clear that TSMC was not a sudden inspiration but a logical synthesis of 25 years of accumulated learning at TI. Specifically:

1. **The pure-play foundry model** was conceivable to Chang because he had seen how fabless design companies were being constrained by the lack of manufacturing capacity — a gap he identified while still at TI.
2. **Learning-curve pricing** translated directly into TSMC's early pricing discipline: set prices to capture volume, build learning, and achieve cost leadership before competitors.
3. **The talent philosophy** — Chang's conviction that the quality and dedication of the engineering workforce is the primary determinant of yield and process capability — was forged at TI and operationalized at TSMC through compensation, culture, and selective hiring.

The 2007 interview is a rare document where Chang speaks at length about the *intellectual genealogy* of his ideas, making it essential context for understanding why TSMC is built the way it is.
