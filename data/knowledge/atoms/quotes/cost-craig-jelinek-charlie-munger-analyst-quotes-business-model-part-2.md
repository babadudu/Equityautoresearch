## Craig Jelinek & Charlie Munger — Costco Business Model & Membership Philosophy

Craig Jelinek served as Costco CEO from 2012 to January 2024, having joined the company in 1984 as a warehouse manager. Charlie Munger, the late investor and Berkshire Hathaway Vice Chairman, served on Costco's board for decades and was one of its most prominent external advocates. The following quotes span 2018–2022.

---

## Craig Jelinek

### On the Treasure Hunt Experience

**Quote:** "When members walk in, they never know exactly what they'll find. That's intentional."

**Source:** Costco FY2018 Annual Meeting

**Context:** Jelinek articulated the deliberate curation logic behind Costco's non-food SKU rotation strategy. Approximately 25% of non-food items are rotated regularly, so members cannot rely on finding the same products each visit. This creates a "treasure hunt" dynamic—an incentive to visit frequently and purchase on impulse rather than out of planned need. The urgency is real: items that sell out typically are not restocked. This mechanic drives average transaction values and visit frequency above what a purely replenishment-driven retail format would generate.

---

### On the Membership Revenue Model

**Quote:** "We've always said, if we can sell something for less, we should. We don't need to make money on the merchandise. We need to make money on the membership."

**Source:** Costco FY2022 Annual Meeting

**Context:** Jelinek articulated Costco's fundamental economic model with unusual directness. Merchandise operates near break-even (gross margins capped at ~14%); the profit center is membership fees. In FY2025, membership fee revenue was approximately $4.8B at near-100% flow-through to operating income, representing roughly 60–70% of Costco's total operating profit. This model inverts conventional retail logic: instead of treating customers as revenue sources, Costco treats them as fee-paying subscribers whose satisfaction determines renewal rates—the company's most watched KPI.

---

### On the Value Proposition in Economic Downturns

**Quote:** "It's a good time to be in the warehouse club business. When times are tougher, consumers look for value, and that's exactly what we offer."

**Source:** [CNBC, 2022/07/11](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/07/11/4-things-costco-ceo-craig-jelinek-said-in-a-rare-interview-on-the-economy-retail-trends-and-more.html)

**Context:** Jelinek made this remark during a rare CNBC interview in July 2022, amid rising inflation and consumer concern about discretionary spending. He was describing the countercyclical quality of Costco's appeal: when household budgets tighten, the value of Costco membership—bulk savings on essentials—becomes more compelling, not less. This comment also reflects the structural resilience of Costco's member base, whose median household income (~$125,000) provides consumption durability even in downturns. FY2021 revenue had grown 17.5% to $195.9B and FY2022 a further 15.8% to $227.0B—not purely COVID-driven but a permanent acceleration of bulk-buying habits.

---

### On Asia-Pacific as a Growth Engine

**Context:** Jelinek confirmed in a December 2022 CNBC interview that Asia-Pacific, and China specifically, represents a significant long-term growth opportunity for Costco. ([CNBC, 2022/12/19](https://www.cnbc.com/video/2022/12/19/watch-cnbcs-full-interview-with-costco-ceo-craig-jelinek.html)) The milestone event was Costco's first China warehouse opening in August 2019 in Shanghai's Minhang district—the store had to close early on opening day due to traffic gridlock and overwhelming crowd demand, a viral moment that illustrated Chinese consumer appetite for the Costco format. Under Jelinek, Costco expanded its Asia-Pacific footprint significantly as a proving ground for the warehouse club model outside North America.

## Charlie Munger

### On Costco as an Institution

**Quote:** "Costco is one of the most admirable capitalistic institutions in the world."

**Source:** Daily Journal Annual Meeting, 2020

**Context:** Munger made this statement at his annual Daily Journal Corporation shareholder meeting, where he held his most extended public conversations. He singled out Costco—one of only three individual stocks he held personally alongside Berkshire Hathaway and Daily Journal—as a exemplary institution. Munger served on Costco's board for many years, giving him direct visibility into the company's governance and culture. His endorsement carries particular weight given his general skepticism of most consumer businesses and his documented criteria for admirable companies: honest management, durable competitive advantages, and genuine benefit to customers.

**Investment significance:** Munger's Costco position was maintained for decades, through multiple price cycles, including periods when the stock appeared expensive by conventional valuation metrics. His willingness to hold at elevated P/E multiples reflected his view that Costco's institutional qualities—management integrity, membership model durability, pricing discipline—justified a sustained premium above DCF-implied intrinsic value.

### Munger's Historical Influence on Costco's Relationship with Amazon

**Context (attributed via Acquired Podcast):** A historically notable connection: Jeff Bezos met with Jim Sinegal in 2001 and was influenced by Costco's philosophy to reverse Amazon's then-current practice of charging customers higher prices based on purchasing history. Bezos adopted the principle that Amazon should not exploit customer data to maximize prices—a value directly imported from the Costco model. This episode illustrates Munger's broader point: that Costco's principles have influenced the design of competing business models, not merely Costco's own operations. Source: [Acquired Podcast — Costco, 2023/08/20](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/costco).

## Synthesis: External Validation of the Membership Flywheel

The Jelinek and Munger quotes together validate Costco's membership-first model from two distinct vantage points:

- **Jelinek (operator):** Confirmed that Costco's economic logic is deliberately backward relative to conventional retail—merchandise margins are sacrificed to deepen member value, and profit flows from the renewal fee.
- **Munger (institutional observer):** Treated Costco as a reference standard for ethical capitalism—a business that succeeds financially precisely because it does not maximize short-term extraction from customers or employees.

The Acquired Podcast analysis ([2023/08/20](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/costco)) further contextualizes this: Kirkland Signature, generating ~$69B/year (~25% of Costco revenue), would rank as the largest CPG brand in the United States if measured independently—a scale achievement that validates Jelinek's assertion that Costco can build genuine product equity without relying on national-brand suppliers.
