## Morris Chang — Geopolitics, Globalization & American Identity

These quotes document Morris Chang's views on the unraveling of globalization, the semiconductor industry's role in U.S.-China rivalry, and his own complex identity as a Chinese-born naturalized American who built the world's most critical technology company in Taiwan.

---

### On the death of globalization

> "Globalization is almost dead. Free trade is almost dead."

— Morris Chang, Brookings Institution, April 2022. ([Source: Brookings Institution, 2022-04](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/Vying-for-Talent-Morris-Chang-20220414.pdf))

**Context:** Chang made this stark declaration at a Brookings Institution event focused on semiconductor talent and supply chain resilience — among the first senior industry figures to publicly frame the geopolitical shift so bluntly. The speech came after Russia's invasion of Ukraine and amid accelerating U.S.-China decoupling. Chang was not lamenting this development, but rather stating it as a strategic fact that the semiconductor industry must plan around. The remark came two years before the CHIPS Act fully took shape and before TSMC's Arizona fab broke ground — making it prescient context for why TSMC's geographic diversification is geopolitically driven, not operationally optimal.

---

### On American identity

> "My identity has always been an American."

— Morris Chang, Brookings Institution, April 2022. ([Source: Brookings Institution, 2022-04](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/Vying-for-Talent-Morris-Chang-20220414.pdf))

**Context:** Chang's declaration of American identity at the Brookings speech is notable given his life trajectory: born in mainland China (1931), educated at Harvard and MIT and Stanford, spent most of his career at Texas Instruments in the United States, then relocated to Taiwan at the Taiwanese government's invitation to lead ITRI before founding TSMC. He holds U.S. citizenship. This self-identification matters geopolitically: it signals that Chang — and by extension TSMC — does not position the company as a purely Taiwanese or Chinese-origin enterprise, but as a global institution with deep American roots. This identity complexity is part of why TSMC has navigated U.S.-Taiwan-China triangulation more skillfully than a purely Taiwan-nationalist framing would allow.

---

### On the dream of American manufacturing

> "I am from the United States, and my dream is to build a wafer fab in the United States."

— Morris Chang. ([Source: Andy Lin's Blog, 2024-12-28](https://www.granitefirm.com/blog/us/2024/12/28/morris-changs-controversial/))

**Context:** This quote captures the emotional underpinning of TSMC's Arizona investment — beyond geopolitical compliance with CHIPS Act incentives, Chang personally wanted to build in America. He invested his reputation in Arizona as a statement of belonging and commitment to the country where he was educated and where his intellectual formation in semiconductor manufacturing took place (Texas Instruments, Dallas). The quote is particularly significant because Chang was simultaneously skeptical about the economics of overseas manufacturing (he warned that Arizona fabs would be significantly more expensive than Taiwan fabs), creating a tension between his personal dream and his economic realism.

---

### Synthesis

Chang's geopolitical quotes reveal a man who anticipated the end of hyperglobalization before most policymakers did, who holds a genuine emotional connection to the United States, and who simultaneously understands that building advanced semiconductor manufacturing outside Taiwan incurs real cost penalties. His 2022 "globalization is almost dead" declaration is now an industry axiom. The tension he identified — between geopolitically driven geographic diversification and economically rational concentration in Taiwan — remains the central strategic challenge for TSMC under C.C. Wei.
