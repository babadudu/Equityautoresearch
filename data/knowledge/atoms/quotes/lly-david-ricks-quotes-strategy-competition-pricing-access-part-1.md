# David Ricks on GLP-1 Pricing, Competition, Compounders, and Medicare Access

**David Ricks** is the CEO of Eli Lilly and Company. The quotes below span 2024–2025 and address Lilly's competitive strategy, its stance on compounding pharmacies as a threat to intellectual property, the Medicare pricing deal, and Ricks' counter-intuitive welcome of new competitors.

---

## On Competition and Market Expansion

**Source: [T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)**

> "Someone has to do this work."

*Context: Ricks responding to questions about the 193 obesity drug candidates in development globally (as of October 2025). Rather than viewing new entrants as threats, he frames expanded competition as validation of the market size and a force that helps build the broader GLP-1 ecosystem. His view: with 1 billion people projected to have obesity within five years, the market is so large that multiple winners can coexist — new participants expand the total pie rather than taking share from Lilly.*

---

## On Opaque Drug Pricing and the Pharmacy Benefit Manager System

**Source: [Washington Reporter, 2025-03](https://washingtonreporter.news/p/interview-eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks)**

> "There's just too much opaque pricing and profit taking."

*Context: Ricks criticizing the U.S. pharmaceutical distribution system — particularly pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) and the layers of intermediaries that sit between Lilly's list price and the patient's out-of-pocket cost. This unusual candor from a pharmaceutical CEO reflects Ricks' view that the access problem for GLP-1 drugs is as much a distribution system failure as it is an affordability issue at the manufacturer level.*

> "A company to government discussion we're never going to win, but a government to government discussion, we could win."

*Context: Ricks on the strategy for expanding GLP-1 reimbursement in international markets where single-payer systems negotiate directly with governments. He acknowledges that bilateral government negotiations — where a country's health ministry negotiates drug access terms with the U.S. government — are more likely to succeed than Lilly negotiating independently with foreign payers.*

## On the Medicare Deal and Volume-for-Price Strategy

**Source: [T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)**

> "We're excited to get going on that; that'll start in the spring, and we lowered our prices to get that done."

*Context: Ricks commenting on the November 2025 agreement between Lilly, Novo Nordisk, and the U.S. government to include GLP-1 anti-obesity drugs in Medicare Part D coverage. The negotiated price dropped from approximately $1,000/month to $245/month (patient co-pay: $50), a ~75% reduction. Ricks frames the price concession as a deliberate volume-for-price trade — Lilly willingly accepted lower margins in exchange for access to a dramatically expanded patient pool.*

> "20 to 30 million new patients... Just on Medicare."

*Context: Ricks' estimate of the incremental patient population unlocked by Medicare Part D coverage. Prior to the deal, Medicare was prohibited from covering obesity drugs under Part D. At 20–30 million newly eligible patients, even at the reduced negotiated price, the revenue opportunity is substantial — Ricks views this as margin-dilutive but volume-accretive.*

## On Compounding Pharmacies as Intellectual Property Threat

**Source: [Cheeky Pint / All-In, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)**

> "Shadow generic industry undermining patents itself... You've created basically a shadow generic industry."

*Context: Ricks on the phenomenon of compounding pharmacies producing semaglutide and tirzepatide during the 2023–2025 drug shortage period. Under FDA rules, compounders can produce copies of drugs listed on the shortage list — but Ricks argues this created a de facto generic market that bypassed Lilly's patent protections years before the patents expire. The FDA removed GLP-1 drugs from the shortage list in April 2025, ending the legal basis for most compounding, but the market impact persisted.*

> "There may be up to one or two million Americans using these so-called drugs. They're not actually proven."

*Context: Ricks estimating the scale of the compounded GLP-1 market — 1–2 million Americans potentially using unregulated versions of semaglutide or tirzepatide that have not undergone FDA clinical trials, stability testing, or manufacturing quality review. He emphasizes the patient safety dimension, not just the business threat.*

**Source: [Washington Reporter, 2025-03](https://washingtonreporter.news/p/interview-eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks)**

> "Most of them are sourced out of Chinese labs that aren't even approved in China, let alone in the U.S."

*Context: Ricks revealing the supply chain origin of much of the compounded GLP-1 raw material — active pharmaceutical ingredients (API) sourced from Chinese manufacturers that have not received approval from China's NMPA. The irony he highlights: these products are too unsafe to sell in China yet are being injected into American patients. This is Ricks' most pointed criticism of the compounding pharmacy system.*

## On the LillyDirect Direct-to-Consumer Model

**Source: [Cheeky Pint / All-In, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)**

> "People skip all this other morass and getting a 'this is not a bill' piece of paper. They're just like, 'Here's my Visa card number.'"

*Context: Ricks describing consumer enthusiasm for LillyDirect, the company's direct-to-consumer telehealth and pharmacy platform that sells GLP-1 medications at approximately $549/month — bypassing traditional insurance networks and PBMs entirely. The quote captures the consumer frustration with opaque insurance billing and their preference for transparent, direct pricing even at a premium.*

## On Alignment with MAHA (Make America Healthy Again)

**Source: [Washington Reporter, 2025-03](https://washingtonreporter.news/p/interview-eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks)**

> "At the top level, we have a huge amount of alignment. Everyone in our company wakes up every day trying to make people healthy."

*Context: Ricks expressing strategic alignment with the Robert F. Kennedy Jr.-led health policy agenda, which initially appeared threatening to pharmaceutical companies. Rather than opposing the MAHA movement, Ricks embraced its anti-obesity and pro-preventive-health dimensions as consistent with Lilly's mission.*

> "Medicine isn't always the answer. Science tells us that already, so that's easy to embrace."

*Context: Ricks signaling openness to the MAHA movement's skepticism of over-medicalization — a diplomatically shrewd position that avoids conflict with a politically influential health movement while Lilly's GLP-1 drugs are simultaneously positioned as the medical answer to the obesity crisis.*

**Source: [T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)**

> "There's a blurry picture here of insurance and health care schemes that cover the comorbidities of obesity."

*Context: Ricks describing the insurance coverage paradox — insurers will pay for treating heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and sleep apnea (all obesity comorbidities) but resist covering obesity itself as a primary diagnosis. He views this as an irrational distinction that Medicare Part D coverage will help resolve by establishing the precedent that obesity is a chronic disease warranting pharmacological treatment.*
