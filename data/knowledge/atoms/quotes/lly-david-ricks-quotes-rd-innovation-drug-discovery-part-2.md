## David Ricks — R&D Investment, Drug Discovery Philosophy, and Manufacturing Scale-Up

**Source context:** David Ricks is CEO of Eli Lilly (since January 2017). The following quotes are drawn from interviews conducted 2019–2025, primarily [All-In/Cheeky Pint (2024)](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp), [Chief Executive (2025)](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/), [T. Rowe Price (2025)](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly), [Fortune (2024)](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/), and [S&P Global (2025)](https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/research/2025/08/eli-lilly-set-to-leapfrog-novo-nordisk-in-weight-loss-drug-race).

### On the Uniqueness of Drug Discovery

> "Our business is one of the few businesses that you do actual discovery... Something never happened before—and now it's happened."

> "It's literally like the card flipping over and you win the hand or you don't."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks uses this framing to explain why pharmaceutical R&D carries fundamentally different risk than most industries. Drug discovery is binary: molecules either work in humans or they don't, and no amount of incremental investment can guarantee a positive outcome. This shapes Lilly's portfolio strategy — the company runs broad, parallel bets rather than concentrating on one molecule.

---

### On the Cost and Structure of Drug Development

> "The average drug costs three and a half, 4 billion to make."

> "Most of that is the last step."

— [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)

**Context:** Ricks was explaining why pharmaceutical pricing is difficult for the public to understand. The "last step" he refers to is the large-scale Phase 3 clinical trial, which often costs $500M–$2B on its own. The $3.5–4B figure includes all failed candidates that funded the eventual approved drug — a reminder that the economics of drug development are portfolio economics, not per-molecule economics.

### On R&D Scale and Public Responsibility

> "We spend almost 25% of sales this year on R&D. We'll spend $14 billion this year total."

> "[That's] above Germany and Japan as a nation and headed closer to the NIH."

— [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp); [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** With 2025 revenues of approximately $45.0B, Lilly's ~$14B R&D commitment represents one of the largest private R&D investments in the world — comparable in scale to the research budgets of entire nation-states. Ricks uses this comparison not to boast but to underscore what Lilly could do for human health if its capital allocation decisions are correct.

> "If we're good, we can direct that at problems that are both big and solvable in a way governments never could."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Made in the context of potential cuts to U.S. federal health research funding (NIH budget under political pressure in 2025). Ricks sees the contraction of government research as creating both a responsibility and an opportunity for large pharmaceutical companies to step into the breach — provided they remain focused on high-unmet-need therapeutic areas rather than commercial optimization.

### On the tirzepatide Discovery Moment

> "We had to stop the study because people were losing too much weight to stay in it."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks describes receiving the early clinical data for tirzepatide (then in development as a GIP+GLP-1 dual agonist) shortly before he became CEO in 2017. Trial participants were dropping out because their weight loss was so dramatic it exceeded protocol limits. This was the inflection point that defined his entire tenure: "It's been all execution" — the science was already settled; the challenge became manufacturing, regulatory approval, and global commercialization at speed.

### On the Off-Strategy Origin of the Biggest Innovation

> "Someone was off strategy, and it may be the biggest drug in the history of our industry."

> "[Middle management tends to] squash deviations, but the deviations are the people doing stuff off strategy."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks is referring to tirzepatide's origin — a molecule that was being studied for diabetes but showed unexpectedly dramatic weight loss results, which was not a primary strategic priority at the time. This insight shapes his management philosophy: he deliberately maintains close contact between senior leadership and bench scientists precisely to catch these "off-strategy" discoveries before middle management filters them out. The implication for investors is that Lilly's pipeline may contain similar surprises not yet visible to the market.

### On Manufacturing Scale-Up and the CapEx Commitment

> "$50 billion CapEx agenda... Over five years."

— [T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)

**Context:** Lilly announced a $50B capital expenditure program spanning 2024–2029 to build four new manufacturing facilities capable of producing incretin (GLP-1 and GIP) therapies at scale. Ricks characterizes this as likely the largest manufacturing expansion in pharmaceutical history. The investment reflects his conviction that the demand for GLP-1 class drugs will far exceed current global production capacity for the foreseeable future.

> "[We] produced more than 1.6 times the amount of salable incretin doses during the first half of 2025 when compared to the first half of 2024."

— [S&P Global / CNBC, 2025](https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/research/2025/08/eli-lilly-set-to-leapfrog-novo-nordisk-in-weight-loss-drug-race)

**Context:** This 60%+ year-over-year production increase validates that the CapEx is translating into real output. The supply shortage that constrained Mounjaro and Zepbound revenues in 2023–2024 is being systematically resolved ahead of schedule.

### On Insulin Pricing — A Candid Admission

> "Our actual net on insulin really hasn't changed. It's like $30-$40, but the list price got up to 275."

> "We competed on this spread... We kept raising the list price."

— [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)

**Context:** Ricks is providing an unusually frank explanation of how the U.S. pharmaceutical pricing system created perverse incentives. PBMs and insurance companies extracted rebates based on the spread between list price and net price — so manufacturers had an incentive to raise list prices in order to offer larger rebates and win formulary placement. The net price to Lilly barely changed, but list prices became politically untenable. Lilly responded in 2023 by unilaterally capping Humalog's list price at $35/month, restoring public trust ahead of IRA-mandated Medicare price negotiations.

### On LillyDirect and Bypassing Intermediaries

> "People skip all this other morass and getting a 'this is not a bill' piece of paper. They're just like, 'Here's my Visa card number.'"

— [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)

**Context:** Describing the success of LillyDirect, Lilly's direct-to-consumer pharmacy platform that allows patients to purchase Mounjaro and Zepbound directly with a credit card, bypassing insurance, PBMs, and the confusing U.S. prior-authorization process. LillyDirect both reduces friction for patients and captures a higher margin per dose by eliminating intermediary fees and rebate obligations.

### On the Compounding Pharmacy Threat

> "You've created basically a shadow generic industry."

— [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)

**Context:** Ricks was describing the explosion of compounding pharmacies producing semaglutide and tirzepatide during the FDA shortage period. While technically legal under shortage exemptions, compounded GLP-1 drugs exist outside Lilly's and Novo Nordisk's quality control and IP frameworks. Ricks' tone implies he expects — and welcomes — regulatory intervention to shut down this market once supply catches up with demand, which FDA action in 2025 began to deliver.

### On Obesity as a Disease, Not a Lifestyle Failure

> "[Obesity is] pathologic... difficult to very difficult to use traditional tools like diet and exercise."

— [Fortune, 2024](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks consistently frames obesity as a chronic medical condition requiring pharmacological intervention, not a personal failing addressable by willpower. This framing is central to Lilly's market-building strategy: normalizing pharmaceutical treatment increases the TAM and reduces stigma-driven resistance to prescribing.

### On Alzheimer's and the Coming Decade of Brain Health

> "We've been studying... Alzheimer's for basically 30 years at Lilly."

> "More than 80% reduction in [amyloid plaques] in like a year. Even to undetectable levels."

> "The decade of the brain... brain health is probably the biggest category."

— [T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly); [Fortune, 2024](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Kisunla (donanemab) received FDA approval in 2024 for early Alzheimer's disease, representing the culmination of Lilly's three-decade investment in amyloid hypothesis research. Ricks is positioning neuroscience — not obesity — as Lilly's next multi-decade growth platform. Brain health encompasses Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, depression, and other neurological conditions representing an enormous unmet medical need that Ricks believes will define the next ten years of pharmaceutical innovation.
