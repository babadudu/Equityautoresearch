## Jeff Bezos on Ethics, Values, and Failure

---

### On the Harder Choice Between Cleverness and Kindness

**Source:** [Jeff Bezos 2010 Princeton Commencement Speech](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vBmavMmz9WM)

In his 2010 Princeton commencement address, Bezos shared a childhood story that shaped his moral philosophy. At age 10, he calculated how many minutes his grandmother's smoking habit had shortened her life, then announced the result to her, expecting praise for his mathematical cleverness. Instead, she began to cry. His grandfather took him aside and said:

> "Jeff, one day, you'll understand that it's harder to be kind than clever."

Bezos built his commencement address around this lesson, culminating in:

> "Cleverness is a gift. Kindness is a choice. Gifts are easy — they're given, after all. Choices can be hard."

And his final summation of a life's philosophy:

> "In the end, we are our choices. Build yourself a great story."

**Significance:** The "we are our choices" framework explains Bezos's repeated willingness to choose long-term customer trust over short-term revenue — choices that are structurally harder than defaulting to cleverness or optimization. The Princeton speech is one of the rare public windows into the personal value system behind Amazon's institutional culture.

### On Accepting Scrutiny and Defending Amazon's Scale

**Source:** [Congressional Antitrust Testimony, July 2020](https://www.congress.gov/116/meeting/house/110883/witnesses/HHRG-116-JU05-Wstate-BezosJ-20200729.pdf)

At the 2020 House Judiciary antitrust hearing, Bezos took an unusual posture for a CEO under regulatory pressure — he affirmatively embraced the principle of scrutiny:

> "I believe Amazon should be scrutinized. We should scrutinize all large institutions, whether they're companies, government agencies, or non-profits."

He simultaneously contextualized Amazon's market position:

> "Amazon accounts for less than 1% of the $25 trillion global retail market and less than 4% of retail in the U.S."

**Significance:** The willingness to affirm the legitimacy of scrutiny — rather than resist it — reflects the same "choices over cleverness" ethic from the Princeton speech. Bezos framed Amazon not as an exception to institutional accountability but as subject to the same standards he would apply to any large organization.

### On Failure as a Prerequisite for Meaningful Invention

**Source:** Various; see also [HBR IdeaCast, January 2013](https://hbr.org/podcast/2013/01/jeff-bezos-on-leading-for-the)

> "If the size of your failures isn't growing, you're not going to be inventing at a size that can actually move the needle."

**Context:** Bezos offered this quote in reference to Amazon's failed Fire Phone (2014), which generated over $170M in losses. Rather than framing the failure as a mistake to be avoided, he presented it as necessary evidence of sufficient ambition. If Amazon's failures were small, it would mean Amazon's bets were small.

**Notable failures cited in the AMZN research:**
- **Fire Phone** (2014): $170M+ loss, discontinued.
- **Amazon Destinations**: Travel booking service, shuttered.
- **Haven Healthcare**: Joint venture with JPMorgan and Berkshire Hathaway for employer healthcare, dissolved.

Bezos's framework positions these not as management errors but as the expected output of a company betting at sufficient scale.
