## Morris Chang on His Journey from War to Founding TSMC

Morris Chang's path from wartime China to creating the world's most valuable semiconductor company spans half a century of deliberate choices. The following quotes, drawn from biographical materials, the Computer History Museum oral history, the Synergy Startup Substack, and Commonwealth Publishing, trace the logic of his career decisions.

---

### On His Early Life and Formative Experience

**Quote:** "The early part of my life between birth and 18, the background was war, poverty, injustice."

**Context:** Chang was born in 1931 in Ningbo, Zhejiang, China. He spent his childhood and adolescence under Japanese occupation, civil war, and social upheaval. He emigrated to the United States in 1949 at age 18. This background is significant because it explains the tenacity and long-horizon thinking that characterized his career — someone who has survived genuine scarcity does not panic during cyclical downturns.

**Source:** [Synergy Startup Substack](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang); [transcripts/Morris_Chang_Early_Career_Untold_Story.md](transcripts/Morris_Chang_Early_Career_Untold_Story.md)

---

### On Leaving Sylvania for Texas Instruments

**Quote:** "Sylvania wasn't going anywhere in semiconductors."

**Context:** Chang joined Sylvania Semiconductor in 1955 after completing his mechanical engineering degrees at MIT. Within three years, he recognized that Sylvania lacked the strategic commitment to build a meaningful semiconductor business and transferred to Texas Instruments (TI) in 1958.

**Source:** [Synergy Startup Substack](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)

---

### On Leaving Texas Instruments After 25 Years

**Quote:** "I left TI without any job offer, and I left because I felt that essentially I had been put out to pasture at TI."

**Context:** After 25 years at TI — rising to Vice President of the worldwide semiconductor business — Chang departed in 1983 without another position lined up. He described feeling sidelined within TI's leadership despite his contributions. He briefly joined General Instrument:

**Quote:** "General Instrument was mainly about acquisitions, mergers... it was just very different from what I was used to at Texas Instruments."

**Source:** [Synergy Startup Substack](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)

**Investment significance:** Chang's 25 years at TI gave him unparalleled expertise in semiconductor manufacturing economics, learning curve pricing, and the scale dynamics of the industry. His departure was not a failure — it was the accumulation point of experience that made TSMC's founding possible.

---

### On the Decision to Found TSMC

**Quote:** "I wanted to establish a company that would be the foundry for the world's semiconductor industry. At that time, there was no such company."

**Context:** After joining Taiwan's Industrial Technology Research Institute (ITRI) as Director General in 1985 at the invitation of the Taiwan government, Chang identified the structural opportunity: IC design was becoming cheaper (with the rise of EDA tools and fabless architecture) while manufacturing was becoming more expensive. A dedicated, neutral foundry could serve the emerging wave of fabless chip designers who could not afford their own fabs. In 1987 he founded TSMC with initial backing from the Taiwan government (48.3% stake) and Philips (27.5%).

**Quote:** "When I founded a semiconductor company, there was only one path: to make it a world-class one."

**Source:** [CommonWealth Publishing, 2024-12-02](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3849)

**Investment significance:** The insight that "at that time, there was no such company" captures TSMC's original strategic logic: it was not competing with existing IDMs (Integrated Device Manufacturers) but creating a new category. This founding logic remains the core of TSMC's identity — it does not compete with its customers, it enables them. The purity of this model (no proprietary products, no equity stakes in customers, no IP licensing) is what gave fabless companies the confidence to share their most sensitive designs with TSMC.

---

### On What "World-Class" Required

**Background — Education:** After one year at Harvard, Chang transferred to MIT, earning a B.S. and M.S. in Mechanical Engineering. He later received his Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering from Stanford.

**Quote (on founding ambition):** "When I founded a semiconductor company, there was only one path: to make it a world-class one."

This statement, from the December 2024 CommonWealth publication accompanying his autobiography, reflects Chang's belief that in capital-intensive industries with steep learning curves, there is no sustainable middle ground between world leadership and irrelevance. A foundry that is second-best loses customers to the leader; the leader gets more volume, descends the cost curve faster, and widens its lead. Chang understood this flywheel from his TI years and designed TSMC to be the singular global leader — not one of several comparable options.
