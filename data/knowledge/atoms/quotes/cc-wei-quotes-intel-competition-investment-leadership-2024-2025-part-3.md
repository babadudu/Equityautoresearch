## CC Wei — Competition, Investment, and Leadership Quotes (2024–2026)

### On Intel Foundry: Investment Cannot Buy Competitiveness (January 2026)

**Source:** [WCCFTech, 2026-01](https://wccftech.com/tsmcs-ceo-says-intel-foundry-wont-be-competitive-by-just-throwing-money/)

In TSMC's January 2026 earnings call, CC Wei addressed the question of Intel Foundry's competitive threat with unusual directness:

> "In the development of semiconductor technology to this point, receiving investment has not helped to improve competitiveness, right?"

This statement — framed rhetorically but delivered as a verdict — encapsulates Wei's view that semiconductor process leadership is not a capital problem. It is a time, talent, and culture problem. The implication: Intel Foundry's government subsidies and massive investment commitments do not address the structural disadvantages in process know-how, yield management culture, and customer trust that separate TSMC from its aspiring competitors.

Wei followed this by stating he was "not worried" about Intel Foundry as a near-term competitive threat — not from complacency, but from a clear-eyed assessment of what competitive gaps actually consist of and how long they take to close.

The broader strategic message: TSMC's moat is not a capital barrier (which can in principle be matched) but a *learning and culture barrier* that compounds over decades and cannot be replicated by funding alone.

### On Talent and Arizona Site Selection (October 2023)

**Source:** [Yale Daily News, 2023-10-05](https://yaledailynews.com/blog/2023/10/05/taiwan-semiconductor-ceo-c-c-wei-speaks-at-the-yale-science-building/) | Transcript: [transcripts/CC_Wei_2023_Yale_Speech.md](transcripts/CC_Wei_2023_Yale_Speech.md)

Speaking at Yale — his alma mater — CC Wei offered insight into the talent philosophy behind TSMC's Arizona expansion decision:

> "Most talented and innovative people."

Wei used this phrase to characterize what TSMC sought from the Arizona location — and what drove the site-selection logic. The Chandler/Phoenix metropolitan area was chosen in part for its proximity to Arizona State University and the broader Southwest US technology talent pipeline, offering access to engineers who would not necessarily relocate to Taiwan but could staff a US flagship fab.

Wei's Yale speech also reinforced the message that TSMC's US expansion was not a geopolitical concession but a talent strategy: accessing the best engineers where they already live, rather than requiring all global talent to cluster in Hsinchu.

This framing — expansion as talent diversification, not manufacturing diversification — is consistent with Morris Chang's "learning is local" thesis: you build where the learning can happen, and learning happens where the people are.
