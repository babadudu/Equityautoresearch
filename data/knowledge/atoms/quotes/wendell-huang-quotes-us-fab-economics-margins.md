## Wendell Huang — U.S. Fab Cost Structure

When TSMC's CFO Wendell Huang was asked on the Q4 2024 earnings call to explain why U.S. fab costs are structurally higher than Taiwan fabs, he gave a direct three-part answer:

> "The U.S. fab cost, it is more expensive in the U.S., mainly because of several reasons. Number one, the smaller scale, right? Now number two, the higher price in the supply chain; and number three, the very early stage of the ecosystem."

([Source: TSMC Q4 2024 Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/01/16/taiwan-semiconductor-manufacturing-tsm-q4-2024-ear/))

This comment was made in the context of CEO C.C. Wei simultaneously noting that TSMC does charge a premium for U.S.-made wafers — framing "Made in U.S.A." as a value-add rather than a handicap. Huang's breakdown highlights that the cost disadvantage is structural and not easily overcome: scale takes years to build, the U.S. supply chain charges higher prices for raw materials and components, and the semiconductor ecosystem (trained workforce, specialized vendors, chemical suppliers) is still nascent. This trio of headwinds explains why even TSMC — the world's most efficient foundry — faces meaningful cost inflation in Arizona, and why any new entrant trying to build U.S. fabs from scratch would face an even steeper climb.

## Wendell Huang — Pricing Philosophy: Strategic, Not Opportunistic

During the 2022 cycle peak — when global chip shortages gave foundries unusual pricing leverage — Huang articulated TSMC's disciplined approach to wafer pricing:

> "Our pricing will remain strategic, not opportunistic to earn our value."

([Source: The Next Platform, 2026-01-16](https://www.nextplatform.com/compute/2026/01/16/tsmc-has-no-choice-but-to-trust-the-sunny-ai-forecasts-of-its-customers/4092173))

This quote captures TSMC's pricing doctrine: the company raised prices (reportedly 10–20% on advanced nodes in 2022, with gross margins reaching a then-record 59.6% and ROIC hitting 37.3%), but it did so by asserting that it was capturing fair value — not exploiting a temporary shortage. The distinction matters because TSMC's customers (Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, Qualcomm) are long-term partners. Opportunistic pricing would damage trust and accelerate diversification efforts. Strategic pricing, by contrast, is tied to the genuine value TSMC delivers: technology leadership, yield reliability, and packaging integration.

## Wendell Huang — Profitability Drivers: Utilization Rate Over Pricing

When speculation emerged in late 2025 that TSMC was planning a ~20% wafer ASP increase, Huang pushed back in the Q4 2025 earnings call to clarify what actually drives TSMC's profitability:

> "Pricing is only one element of our broader profitability... the more decisive driver of profitability is a high utilization rate supported by strong demand and disciplined capacity planning."

([Source: TrendForce, 2026-01-15](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2026/01/15/news-tsmc-addresses-speculations-of-a-20-wafer-asp-increase-says-price-is-not-the-main-profit-driver/))

This is a revealing statement about TSMC's operating model. The company's fabs have very high fixed costs; the marginal cost of producing an additional wafer is low once fixed costs are covered. Therefore, keeping utilization high (minimizing idle capacity) is far more impactful to gross margin than squeezing an extra percentage point of price from customers. This is why TSMC has historically been reluctant to overbuild capacity — it would dilute utilization and hurt margins — and why the company's capacity allocation process is so deliberate.

## Wendell Huang — Long-Term Gross Margin Floor: 53%+

Huang has publicly anchored TSMC's long-term structural gross margin floor on multiple occasions. In the Q2 2025 earnings call, he stated:

> "Thus, even with the unfavorable foreign exchange rate, we believe a long-term gross margin of 53% and higher remains well achievable."

([Source: TSMC Q2 2025 Transcript](https://investor.tsmc.com/chinese/encrypt/files/encrypt_file/reports/2025-07/1f4f86c935f1de837672a6973154e64b26bdae57/TSMC%202Q25%20Transcript.pdf))

The 53% floor is significant in several ways. First, it was stated explicitly in the context of FX headwinds (a strong NTD reduces USD-reported margins), signaling that even in adverse macro conditions, TSMC's structural cost advantages protect the floor. Second, the company's actual gross margins in 2025–2026 have run in the 62%+ range — meaning the AI supercycle is generating roughly 9 percentage points of excess margin above the structural baseline. Third, the 53% floor is itself substantially above what most semiconductor manufacturers can achieve, reflecting TSMC's unique combination of technology leadership, scale, and customer concentration in high-value AI workloads.

**Context on wafer pricing trajectory**: Industry analysts estimate 3nm (N3) wafer prices at approximately $18,000–$20,000 — more than 3× the price a decade ago — while 2nm (N2) wafers are expected to exceed $30,000, representing a 50%+ premium over N3. ([Source: Tom's Hardware, 2025](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/tsmcs-wafer-pricing-now-usd18-000-for-a-3nm-wafer-increased-by-over-3x-in-10-years-analyst)). TSMC has also announced 5–10% price increases on sub-5nm nodes in 2026, with CoWoS advanced packaging prices rising as much as 20%. ([Source: TrendForce, 2025-10-08](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2025/10/08/news-tsmc-2nm-reportedly-up-10-20-far-below-rumored-50-3-7nm-to-rise-single-digit-in-2026/)). Huang's messaging consistently frames these price moves as earned through technology advancement rather than as opportunistic rent extraction.
