## C.C. Wei on the Intel Paradox, Customer Success as TSMC's Metric, and Turning TSMC Global

As Chairman & CEO since June 2024, C.C. Wei has articulated a distinctive management philosophy that simultaneously reflects Morris Chang's foundational values and addresses a new set of geopolitical and competitive pressures.

---

### On Intel as Simultaneous Competitor and Customer

**Quote:** "That competitor happened to be our customer, very good customer. So in fact, we are working with them..."

**Context:** Wei made this statement during TSMC's Q3 2025 earnings call (October 17, 2025) when analysts pressed him on Intel Foundry as a competitive threat. The remark captures a structural paradox: Intel is simultaneously attempting to reclaim advanced foundry market share with its 18A process node while its own most advanced products — Panther Lake (18A) and Arrow Lake — continue to require TSMC manufacturing for their leading-edge components.

**Source:** [TSMC Q3 2025 Earnings Call Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/10/17/tsmc-tsm-q3-2025-earnings-call-transcript/); [transcripts/CC_Wei_2025_Q3_Earnings_Call.md](transcripts/CC_Wei_2025_Q3_Earnings_Call.md)

**Investment significance:** The Intel competitor-customer paradox illustrates the depth of TSMC's moat more vividly than any market share statistic. Intel — with its own world-class fabs, its own process engineering teams, and $20B+ in CHIPS Act funding — still cannot fully manufacture its own products without TSMC. This dependency reflects accumulated yield, reliability, and tool ecosystem advantages that cannot be closed in 2–3 years regardless of capital investment. Wei's calm, almost bemused tone in framing Intel as "a very good customer" reflects TSMC's institutional confidence in its technological lead.

### On Measuring TSMC's Success Through Customer Success

**Quote:** "It's a great encouragement to go up on the Fortune Global 500, but the best sign of our success is if our customers are on the list — or even higher than TSMC."

**Context:** Wei made this statement in a July 2025 Fortune Asia interview discussing TSMC's climb up the Fortune Global 500. His framing inverts the conventional competitive logic: most companies measure success by rising above peers; Wei measures TSMC's success by whether its customers rise above TSMC.

**Source:** [Fortune Asia, 2025-07-30](https://fortune.com/asia/2025/07/30/tsmc-ai-boom-cc-wei-wendell-huang-global-500/); [transcripts/CC_Wei_2025_Fortune_Asia.md](transcripts/CC_Wei_2025_Fortune_Asia.md)

**Investment significance:** This customer-success orientation echoes Morris Chang's core 1987 insight that "the greatest discovery was who the customer is." The remark is not merely philosophical — it explains TSMC's reluctance to move into system-level integration or chip design (areas where it would compete with customers). TSMC's entire business model is predicated on genuine alignment of interest with customers: the more capable TSMC's process technology, the more competitive its customers' products, the more wafer volume flows back to TSMC. When NVIDIA's GPUs dominate AI training, NVIDIA buys more N3/N2 wafers from TSMC. The virtuous cycle is real.

### On the Globalization Vision

**Quote:** "Turning Taiwan's TSMC into the World's TSMC."

**Context:** Wei articulated this vision in a July 2025 CommonWealth Magazine interview as the strategic frame for TSMC's global fab buildout — Arizona (Fab 21, phases 1–3), Kumamoto Japan (JASM), and Dresden Germany (ESMC). The formulation acknowledges both the opportunity and the challenge: TSMC must replicate its operational excellence across different cultural, regulatory, and labor environments while managing costs that are structurally 20–50% higher than Taiwan.

**Source:** [CommonWealth Magazine, 2025-07-10](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=4215)

**Investment significance:** The tension embedded in this vision is the central risk to TSMC's margin profile. Taiwan operations run at gross margins of ~55–60% (N2/N3 expected to improve further as yields mature). Arizona Phase 1 (N4) is estimated to carry a 10–20% structural cost premium. Wei's challenge is proving that TSMC's organizational model — the "TSMC Way" of manufacturing discipline and continuous improvement — can be exported. The Arizona fab's early cultural adaptation challenges (reported in 2023–2024) were a preview of this test.

### On Management Philosophy and Background

**Quote:** "When looking for a job, you have to choose your boss first, and I had a great boss."

**Context:** Wei — who grew up in the rural township of Lugu (鹿谷), Nantou County — was asked why he chose to build his entire career at TSMC rather than pursuing opportunities elsewhere. His answer pointed entirely to Morris Chang's mentorship and the organizational environment Chang created, not to compensation or prestige.

**Source:** [CommonWealth Magazine, 2024-06-03](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3710); [SemiWiki — CC Wei Wiki](https://semiwiki.com/wikis/industry-wikis/dr-c-c-wei-wiki/)

**Investment significance:** Wei's career trajectory — 35+ years at a single company, rotating through technology, manufacturing, and business development at Chang's deliberate direction — produced a leader with no gaps in domain knowledge. CommonWealth Magazine characterized his leadership style as "unorthodox" compared to predecessors: direct, self-deprecating, and grounded. This cultural authenticity matters for organizational trust in a company where retention is itself a competitive advantage.

### On CapEx Discipline (CFO Wendell Huang)

**Quote (Wendell Huang):** "A higher level of CapEx is always correlated with higher growth opportunities. If we do our job right, the growth of our business should outpace the growth of the CapEx."

**Context:** TSMC CFO Wendell Huang made this statement during the Q3 2025 earnings call, framing the company's elevated 2026 CapEx guidance ($52–56B) as a function of growth visibility rather than infrastructure maintenance. The implicit target is that ROIC remains at 30%+ even as absolute capital spending reaches record levels.
