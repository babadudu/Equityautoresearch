## Morris Chang — Founding Vision & Pure-Play Foundry Philosophy

Morris Chang (張忠謀) founded TSMC in 1987 and pioneered the pure-play foundry model — separating chip design from manufacturing. The quotes below capture his core convictions about why TSMC was built, what it stands for, and the pricing philosophy that underpins its moat.

---

### On the founding moment and the unmet need

> "I wanted to establish a company that would be the foundry for the world's semiconductor industry. At that time, there was no such company."

— Morris Chang, TSMC founding story. ([Source: TSMC PR](https://pr.tsmc.com/english/news/3210))

**Context:** Chang made this remark recounting the insight behind TSMC's 1987 founding. Before TSMC, the semiconductor industry operated under the IDM (Integrated Device Manufacturer) model — companies both designed and manufactured their own chips. Chang saw that separating these functions would unlock a new category of fabless design companies (later exemplified by Qualcomm, NVIDIA, MediaTek) that could innovate on design without owning fabs.

> "When I founded a semiconductor company, there was only one path: to make it a world-class one."

— Morris Chang. ([Source: CommonWealth Publishing, 2024-12-02](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3849))

**Context:** Chang's reflection on his founding ambition. This single-mindedness about world-class execution — not merely being profitable, but being the best — characterizes TSMC's relentless investment in process technology even during downturns.

---

### On the foundry model's historical significance

> "The foundry model has proven to be one of the most successful business model innovations in the history of the semiconductor industry."

— Morris Chang, reviewing industry evolution in 2024. ([Source: TSMC PR](https://pr.tsmc.com/english/news/3210))

**Context:** Spoken decades after founding, this is Chang's retrospective verdict on the model he invented. The pure-play foundry enabled the horizontal disaggregation of the semiconductor value chain — design, IP, EDA tools, manufacturing, and packaging became separate specialist industries, dramatically accelerating innovation pace.

---

### On the pivotal discovery about customers

> "The biggest discovery was who the customers were."

— Morris Chang, last public speech, April 2023. ([Source: Interconnected Blog — Morris Chang's Last Speech, 2023-04](https://interconnected.blog/morris-changs-last-speech/))

**Context:** Chang described realizing that TSMC's true customers were not the end consumers of electronics, but the fabless chip designers whose commercial fate was completely intertwined with TSMC's manufacturing excellence. This customer-centric insight drove TSMC's deep, decades-long relationships with Apple, NVIDIA, and AMD — relationships built on trust, confidentiality (TSMC never competes with customers), and shared success.

---

### On pricing philosophy and the foundry compact

> "Unwilling to take the price that customers can afford means the same that they were not good at doing dedicated foundry service. What TSMC is good at is making reasonable profits out of the price that is acceptable to the customer."

— Morris Chang, autobiography (Volume II). ([Source: TechSoda, Morris Chang autobiography analysis](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography))

**Context:** This quote appears in Chang's discussion of how IDMs — who also offered foundry services — consistently failed as foundry suppliers because they priced opportunistically or were unwilling to accept customer-dictated price points. TSMC's formula was different: price based on the value delivered to the customer, ensure the customer's business success, and earn sustainable margins through volume and continuous cost improvement. This value-based pricing philosophy (rather than cost-plus) is what allows TSMC to maintain 55%+ gross margins at leading-edge nodes while retaining customer loyalty.

---

### On capital allocation conviction and founder authority

> "I am still the guy responsible for the company operation. You need to let me go ahead."

— Morris Chang, to TSMC's board of directors, recounted in Acquired Podcast, January 2025. ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

**Context:** Chang recalled this exchange when persuading a skeptical board to approve large increases in capital expenditure during earlier industry cycles. His argument: the CEO is accountable for results, so the CEO must have the authority to commit capital when the opportunity is clear. This founder-grade decisiveness set TSMC's culture of bold, counter-cyclical CapEx investment — a tradition continued by C.C. Wei, who committed $380–420 billion NT in 2025 CapEx even amid macro uncertainty.

---

### Synthesis

These quotes collectively reveal the architecture of Chang's foundry philosophy: (1) identify an unmet structural need in the industry; (2) build for world-class quality as the only acceptable standard; (3) define customers as partners whose success is TSMC's success; (4) price based on value delivered, not cost-plus; and (5) invest boldly because capital creates compounding moats. This philosophy has not changed materially under C.C. Wei's leadership, making these quotes as relevant to understanding TSMC today as they were at founding.
