## David Ricks — Leadership Philosophy, Organizational Design, and Culture

**Source context:** David Ricks has been CEO of Eli Lilly since January 2017. He is the company's 11th CEO in its 150-year history, joining from IBM and Indiana University (MBA). The following quotes are drawn primarily from [Chief Executive (2025)](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/), [T. Rowe Price (2025)](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly), [Fortune (2024)](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/), and [Posse Foundation (2019)](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks).

### On Lean Executive Team Design

> "When I started, there were 137 of them. This week, there were 138. We've grown by one every 10 years."

> "It's subtraction by addition. You're making the company more complicated and more siloed."

> "If we all know each other, it solves so many problems that no org chart can."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks is describing his deliberate policy of keeping Lilly's senior executive headcount extremely small — approximately 138 people at the top tier of a 43,000-person company. He personally approves every hire at that level. The philosophy is that personal familiarity at the top eliminates the coordination costs, political maneuvering, and information-filtering problems that grow exponentially with headcount. At a company where a single drug approval can add $5–10B in annual revenue, Ricks believes organizational clarity is as much a competitive advantage as scientific excellence.

### On Performance Standards Without Compromise

> "If you're not playing your position and you're not passing the ball, you don't deserve to be on the team."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks is addressing the tension between Lilly's warm "family company" heritage (founded 1876 by Colonel Eli Lilly, with a long history of employee loyalty and Midwestern values) and the uncompromising performance standards he has imposed. He sees no contradiction: the mission of curing disease is too important to protect underperformers. The sports metaphor is one he uses repeatedly — Lilly is a team with specific positions, and team success requires every individual to execute their role precisely.

### On Implicit vs. Explicit Culture Change

> "Almost everything I tried to do explicitly didn't work. But everything I did implicitly stuck."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks discovered early in his tenure that formal cultural initiatives — values statements, culture workshops, declared priorities — had minimal lasting impact. What worked instead was modeling the behavior himself and creating visible wins: "doing the work and then leading the work in a way people can see that is successful." This insight leads him to spend approximately 20% of his time in direct field visits to labs, factories, and commercial offices — writing a two-paragraph report after each visit noting what was working and what needed improvement. The culture follows the leader's calendar, not the culture deck.

### On Mission as the Primary Talent Retention Tool

> "People will really work for that sort of emotional connection, a sense that we're doing something beyond ourselves."

— [T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)

**Context:** Ricks is explaining why Lilly's talent retention — particularly among scientists and clinical researchers — relies less on compensation than on purpose. In pharmaceutical R&D, the most motivated employees are often not optimizing for salary but for the chance to create a drug that cures a disease they care about personally or that affects people they know. Ricks sees this as a durable competitive advantage: mission-driven employees work harder and stay longer than mercenary ones, and the mission at Lilly (addressing diseases with high unmet need) is unusually compelling compared to most industries.

### On Protecting Off-Strategy Innovation

> "Someone was off strategy, and it may be the biggest drug in the history of our industry."

> "[Middle managers tend to] squash deviations, but the deviations are the people doing stuff off strategy."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks is describing how tirzepatide — the active ingredient in Mounjaro and Zepbound — originated in a research program that was not part of Lilly's declared strategic priorities. The molecule showed unexpectedly dramatic weight loss results in diabetic patients, which was initially outside the study's scope. In most large organizations, middle management would have redirected resources away from this "distraction" and back to the plan. Ricks uses this case to argue that CEOs must maintain direct, frequent access to scientific teams precisely to catch the exceptions that organizational gravity would otherwise eliminate. He has structured Lilly's governance to preserve this access — one reason he keeps the executive team so small and personally knows every senior scientist.

### On the Nature of Pharma as Both Technical and Human

> "It's a hugely technical industry. It's also a hugely important human industry... integrity, about truthfulness."

— [Fortune, 2024](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks is framing what makes pharmaceutical leadership different from other industries. The technical dimension — molecular biology, clinical statistics, regulatory science — is table stakes. But the human dimension — the integrity required to report negative trial results accurately, the truthfulness required to communicate risks to patients and regulators, the empathy required to understand what patients actually need — is equally foundational. This framing informs Lilly's public communications strategy: Ricks has been notably direct about the company's historical insulin pricing failures, for example, rather than deflecting.

### On the Science-First Strategic Framework

> "We start with: What's human suffering? And work backwards."

**Context:** This is Ricks' encapsulation of Lilly's strategic prioritization methodology. Rather than starting from market size, competitive dynamics, or reimbursement probability, Lilly's pipeline decisions begin with identifying the highest-burden diseases where patients currently have no adequate treatment — then asking whether Lilly has or could develop the science to address them. This has led the company to maintain costly, long-duration programs in Alzheimer's (30+ years) and other high-failure areas that pure-commercial logic might have abandoned.

### On the Competitive Moment and Lilly's Position

> "We're probably having our best moment in 150 years."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Said in 2025 with reference to Lilly's position at the center of the GLP-1 revolution, the Kisunla Alzheimer's approval, and a robust oncology pipeline. The statement is historically grounded — Lilly has existed since 1876, weathered patent cliffs, pipeline droughts, and competitive disruptions. Ricks is positioning this as a generational opportunity, not a cyclical upturn.

### On Time Management and CEO Prioritization

> "This is where I'm going to spend my time. Any questions?"

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Ricks sets 90-day priority blocks and shares them with the Board in advance. He acknowledges that even in his best quarters, only about 50% of his time actually goes to planned priorities — the rest is consumed by the reactive demands of running a global enterprise. He spends ~20% of his time in field visits. The practice of sharing his intended schedule with the Board is a transparency mechanism that keeps priorities visible and creates accountability for time allocation rather than just financial results.

### On Legacy and Long-Term Stewardship

> "It feels like the kind of question where someone with a good answer would have a foot out the door."

> "How can we make sure to continue that invention, that usefulness to solving healthcare problems?"

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** When asked about his personal legacy, Ricks deflects from ego-driven answers and redirects to institutional continuity. With Lilly's 150th anniversary approaching in May 2026, his stated concern is not what people will say about him but whether the organization he is stewarding will remain capable of discovery and impact for the next generation. He identifies as the 11th CEO in 150 years — a framing that emphasizes his role as a temporary custodian of something much larger and longer-lived than his own tenure: "I'm the 11th CEO of the company in 150 years." ([T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly))

### On Diversity as Structural Necessity for Innovation

> "The status quo will always be the status quo unless some force moves it."

> "New ideas only come from different ideas, so we need more diversity."

> "What you model doing—the shadow you cast—matters."

> "Have a sense of adventure! Move around, and don't be afraid to change."

— [Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)

**Context:** These quotes come from an interview Ricks gave to the Posse Foundation, a college access and leadership program. Ricks frames diversity not primarily in ethical terms but in innovation terms: novel ideas require people with different cognitive backgrounds, experiences, and perspectives. The "shadow you cast" framing is consistent with his broader leadership philosophy that behavior is more powerful than stated values — leaders who want diverse teams must model diverse thinking themselves. His advice to "have a sense of adventure" reflects his own career arc: moving from IBM to graduate school in Indiana for personal reasons, then building a career across Lilly's Canada, China, and global business units before reaching the CEO role.

### On Lilly's Mission and Scale

> "That's what Lilly's for. Doing things at scale, but also focusing on where the most suffering is."

— [Fortune, 2024](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** This is perhaps the most concise summary of Ricks' thesis for Lilly's competitive advantage. The combination of organizational scale (capacity to run dozens of parallel Phase 3 trials, manufacture at hundreds of millions of doses per year, navigate complex global regulatory environments) with mission focus on high-unmet-need conditions is, in his view, the moat that cannot be quickly replicated by smaller biotechs or diversified pharmaceutical conglomerates whose resource allocation is pulled in too many directions.
