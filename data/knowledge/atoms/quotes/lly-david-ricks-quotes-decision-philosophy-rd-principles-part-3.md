## David Ricks — R&D Decision Philosophy: Human Suffering, Discovery Uncertainty, and Institutional Rigor

### Principle 1: Start with human suffering, work backwards

This is Ricks' most stable and frequently cited belief — that Lilly's scale obligates it to pursue the largest unmet medical needs rather than commercially convenient niches:

> "Lilly, with its massive scale, is not being built to make niche drugs for niche diseases. Lots of companies can do that — and do do that. We start with: What's human suffering? And work backwards."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

Importantly, Ricks does not frame this as altruism in tension with commercial interests. He is explicit about the alignment:

> "I'm a capitalist. I like customers."

— [IBJ, 2018](https://www.ibj.com/articles/60488-the-interview-issue-dave-ricks)

**Context:** In pharma, the largest diseases produce the largest markets — so mission and revenue are structurally aligned. This principle drove three pivotal decisions: (1) Spinning off Elanco (animal health) in 2017–2019 to concentrate resources on human disease; (2) Sustaining the Alzheimer's program through the catastrophic 2016 solanezumab Phase 3 failure at a total cost exceeding $8B, ultimately yielding donanemab (Kisunla) FDA approval in 2024; (3) Announcing GLP-1 trials in addiction (alcohol, nicotine, narcotics) in 2024.

On the Alzheimer's commitment after the 2016 failure, Ricks told chief scientist Daniel Skovronsky:

> "Right, get on with it. We don't need to talk about it anymore. It's a good strategy. Full speed ahead."

— [Endpoints News, 2024](https://endpoints.news/failure-faith-and-8-billion-how-lillys-amyloid-conviction-brought-it-to-the-brink-of-alzheimers-approval/)

On the addiction trials announcement:

> "These medicines can be useful for other things we don't think about connected to weight... They're reducing that desire cycle. So next year, you'll see Lilly start large studies in alcohol abuse and nicotine use, even in drug abuse."

— [Economic Club DC, Dec 10 2024](https://www.economicclub.org/events/david-ricks)

On competitive urgency when a major discovery is in hand:

> "If it's going to be a great drug, let's make sure it says Lilly on it."

— [TIME100 Health, 2025](https://time.com/collections/time100-health-2025/7279659/david-ricks/)

### Principle 2: Accept discovery uncertainty; reduce decision error rate through institutional design

Ricks frames pharma as distinct from all other industries in its fundamental epistemology — real discovery, not pure development:

> "Our business is one of the few businesses that you do actual discovery. It's not development purely. You really don't know the answer. That's about the risk we take, but it's also about the utility when it works."

> "It's literally like the card flipping over and you win the hand or you don't."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

His institutional response to irreducible uncertainty is a three-layer decision architecture:

**Layer 1 — Moneyball-style quantitative rigor:**

> "We've actually put together a decision process that's quite a bit more rigorous than it used to be and that leads to fewer bad decisions."

— [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)

In the Bell Pioneer interview, Ricks explicitly invoked Billy Beane's sabermetrics-based baseball methodology as his model — replacing intuition and hierarchy with structured evidence evaluation:

> "More rigorous decision processes than previously, leading to fewer bad decisions."

— [Bell Pioneer, 2025](https://www.bellpioneer.com/insights/the-better-version-of-nice-inside-ceo-david-ricks-leadership-philosophy-at-eli-lilly)

**Layer 2 — Mandatory cooling-off periods:**

> "We actually have a rule to never decide in one meeting."

— [All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)

**Context:** All major R&D decisions require at least two separate meetings, preventing anchoring bias, groupthink, and emotionally-driven decisions during high-pressure clinical data readouts.

### On the tirzepatide discovery moment

Ricks was about to assume the CEO role in late 2016 when the data that would define his tenure arrived:

> "We had to stop the study because people were losing too much weight to stay in it."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

He described his subsequent nine-year tenure as primarily an execution challenge:

> "It's been all execution."

— [Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)

**Context:** Tirzepatide was first synthesized in 2011 when obesity was not in Lilly's strategic plan. The discovery was protected through Lilly's "allowable variation" in research scope — had the company been more rigidly strategy-bound, the compound may have been deprioritized before its clinical potential was recognized. The CEO inherited an execution problem, not a discovery problem: manufacturing scale, regulatory approval, global launches, and DTC infrastructure all had to be built in parallel at unprecedented speed.
