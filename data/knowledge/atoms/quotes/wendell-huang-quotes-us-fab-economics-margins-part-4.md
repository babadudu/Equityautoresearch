## Wendell Huang on US Fab Cost Disadvantage: Structural, Not Temporary

**Context:** At the Q4 2024 earnings call, CFO Wendell Huang was asked to quantify the cost impact of TSMC's US expansion. Analysts wanted to understand whether the higher US manufacturing costs were a startup-phase issue that would normalize over time, or a structural disadvantage relative to Taiwan operations.

> "The U.S. fab cost, it is more expensive in the U.S., mainly because of several reasons."
— Wendell Huang, CFO, TSMC; [TSMC Q4 2024 Earnings Call Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/01/16/taiwan-semiconductor-manufacturing-tsm-q4-2024-ear/)

**Context:** Huang identified three structural cost drivers for US fabs: (1) **Scale disadvantage** — Arizona's Phase 1 and Phase 2 fabs are significantly smaller than TSMC's Gigafab clusters in Hsinchu and Tainan, which benefit from co-located tool maintenance, chemical supply, and yield engineering at massive scale; (2) **Supply chain immaturity** — Taiwan's semiconductor supply chain ecosystem (chemicals, gases, equipment service networks, outsourced assembly) took 30+ years to develop and cannot be replicated in Arizona on a 5-year timeline; (3) **Ecosystem gaps** — workforce training pipelines, university research collaboration, and local vendor networks are nascent in Arizona vs. deeply embedded in Taiwan. Huang acknowledged these factors would result in gross margin dilution of 2–3 percentage points in early phases, with potential further dilution of 3–4pp in later phases before scale and maturity improvements take effect. The company's approach is to charge premium pricing for "Made in USA" chips — confirmed by customer acceptance — to offset these structural cost disadvantages, though whether the premium fully covers the gap remains contested among analysts.

## Wendell Huang on N2 Node Economics: Structural Profitability Improvement

**Context:** During earnings discussions around N2 (2nm) ramp planning in 2025, Huang addressed investor concern that leading-edge nodes would be margin-dilutive given the massive EUV equipment investment required. His response challenged the assumption that new nodes necessarily compress margins.

> "N2's structural profitability is better than N3."
— Wendell Huang, CFO, TSMC; [TSMC Q4 2024 Earnings Call Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/01/16/taiwan-semiconductor-manufacturing-tsm-q4-2024-ear/)

**Context:** This statement is significant because it breaks from the historical pattern where new nodes require margin sacrifice during ramp before maturing to full profitability. Huang's assertion that N2 is structurally more profitable than N3 — not just at mature yields, but inherently — reflects several factors: (1) pricing power at the leading edge has increased as TSMC's competitive moat widened (Samsung's 3nm GAA yield struggles at ~50% vs. TSMC's ~90% left TSMC with even greater pricing leverage at N2); (2) N2's gate-all-around (GAA) architecture delivers higher performance-per-watt, making it differentiated enough from N3 that customers accept steeper ASP premiums; (3) TSMC's tool amortization model and manufacturing learning curves have improved, reducing the upfront cost burden of new node introductions. If N2's structural profitability is indeed superior to N3 — and if subsequent nodes follow the same pattern — it would suggest TSMC's long-term gross margin trajectory is upward, not flat or declining as bears have argued.
