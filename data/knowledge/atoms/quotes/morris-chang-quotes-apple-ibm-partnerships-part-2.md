## Morris Chang on Key Partnership Decisions: Apple and IBM

Morris Chang's autobiography (Volume 2, published December 2024) revealed previously undisclosed details about two pivotal business decisions: winning the Apple relationship and rejecting IBM's partnership overtures. These quotes illuminate TSMC's foundry pricing philosophy and Chang's instinct for protecting organizational independence.

---

### On Winning the Apple Relationship

**Background:** On November 9, 2010, Foxconn founder Terry Gou introduced Jeff Williams — then Apple's Chief Operating Officer — to Morris Chang. Williams immediately expressed Apple's desire for TSMC to manufacture iPhone logic chips. In April 2011, Chang traveled to Silicon Valley to meet Tim Cook.

**Quote (Tim Cook's implication about Intel):** "Intel is not good at doing dedicated foundry service."

**Chang's gloss on this:** "Unwilling to take the price that customers can afford means the same that they were not good at doing dedicated foundry service. What TSMC is good at is making reasonable profits out of the price that is acceptable to the customer."

**Source:** [TechSoda, Morris Chang autobiography analysis](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography); Book: Morris Chang Autobiography Volume 2 (CommonWealth Publishing, 2024-12-02); [CommonWealth Publishing, 2024-12-02](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3849)

**Investment significance:** This quote encapsulates TSMC's foundry pricing philosophy: not maximizing margin on each transaction, but finding a price the customer can absorb while TSMC earns a reasonable return. This customer-centric pricing created the trust that made Apple exclusively dependent on TSMC for its most strategic chips (A-series, M-series). Intel's failure in foundry, by contrast, stemmed partly from pricing wafers at margins that fabless customers found unacceptable.

---

### On Capacity Discipline with Apple

**Quote:** "I decided that I would take half of what Apple asked for... He said, well, I think you can eliminate your dividend. Your shareholders will understand that. I said, well, no."

**Context:** When Apple requested a large production commitment, Chang deliberately accepted only half the volume, despite Apple's suggestion that TSMC could eliminate its dividend to fund additional capacity for Apple's orders. Chang refused to sacrifice shareholder returns even for its largest strategic customer.

**Source:** [Acquired Briefing, 2025-01](https://www.acquiredbriefing.com/p/the-morris-chang-interview); [transcripts/Morris_Chang_2025_Acquired_Briefing.md](transcripts/Morris_Chang_2025_Acquired_Briefing.md)

**Investment significance:** TSMC has maintained dividend payments through every semiconductor downcycle. Chang's discipline in the Apple negotiation established the precedent that TSMC would not subordinate shareholder capital allocation to any single customer relationship, regardless of strategic importance. This discipline is reflected in TSMC's current commitment to progressive dividend growth alongside record CapEx ($52–56B guided for 2026).

---

### On Rejecting IBM's Partnership

**Quote:** "IBM still consider themselves to be the senior partner in any partnership they established... We declined without having to think about it at all."

**Context:** IBM approached TSMC with a joint R&D partnership proposal. Chang rejected it without hesitation, citing IBM's institutional assumption of seniority in any collaborative arrangement. Accepting would have compromised TSMC's independent research direction and potentially shared its process know-how with a partner that viewed itself as superior.

**Source:** [Acquired Briefing, 2025-01](https://www.acquiredbriefing.com/p/the-morris-chang-interview); [transcripts/Morris_Chang_2025_Acquired_Briefing.md](transcripts/Morris_Chang_2025_Acquired_Briefing.md)

**Investment significance:** This decision preserved TSMC's full intellectual property ownership over its process technologies. Foundry moats are built on accumulated proprietary process know-how — sharing it with a joint venture partner, even one as technically sophisticated as IBM, would have eroded the uniqueness of TSMC's manufacturing stack. Samsung's parallel participation in IBM's Albany consortium (alongside Intel and GlobalFoundries) illustrates the alternative path Chang rejected.
