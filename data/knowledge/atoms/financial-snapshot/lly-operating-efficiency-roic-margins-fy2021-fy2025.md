### ROIC Analysis

Lilly's ROIC troughed in 2023 at 10.21%, primarily due to accelerated R&D investment (tirzepatide multiple Phase 3 trials plus early-stage $50B CapEx outlays) at a time when revenue had not yet fully reflected GLP-1 volume ramp. ROIC rebounded to 16.97% in 2024 and surged to **25.18%** in 2025.

In the context of large-cap pharmaceuticals, a 25%+ ROIC is top-tier. Peer comparison:
- **Novo Nordisk**: ~30%
- **Merck**: ~18%
- **Pfizer**: ~5%

ROE exceeding 100% reflects Lilly's levered capital structure (D/E ~1.6x). Because ROIC strips out the leverage effect, the 25%+ figure confirms that the core business is generating exceptional returns on capital deployed — independently of financial engineering.

### Operating Leverage Analysis

The most striking efficiency story in Lilly's 2025 results is the operating leverage inflection. Revenue grew approximately 45% year-over-year in 2025, while operating income grew more than 100%. This is the classic pharmaceutical leverage dynamic: once a drug is approved and in commercial production, incremental production carries near-zero marginal cost, meaning almost all incremental revenue flows through to profit.

The operating margin expansion from 23.4% (2023 trough) to 40.3% (2025) — a ~1,700 basis point improvement in two years — is among the most dramatic margin expansions seen in large-cap pharmaceutical history over a comparable period. The 2026 Performance Margin guidance of 46–47.5% implies continued acceleration.

CEO Dave Ricks acknowledged the R&D cost structure explicitly: *"We spend almost 25% of sales this year on R&D... We'll spend $14 billion this year total"* ([All-In / Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)). However, Ricks frames high R&D intensity as a competitive moat rather than a burden: *"If we're good, we can direct that at problems that are both big and solvable in a way governments never could"* ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)).

As the denominator (revenue) grows faster than R&D and SG&A spend in absolute dollar terms, the ratio-based efficiency metrics compress naturally — R&D/revenue has declined from ~27% (2023) to ~22% (2025), and SG&A/revenue has dropped from ~25% to ~18% over the same period.

### CapEx Investment vs. Efficiency Trade-off

The $50B CapEx plan (announced across multiple capital allocation disclosures) has temporarily suppressed free cash flow generation during the 2024–2027 build-out phase. This is a deliberate strategic choice: Lilly identified manufacturing capacity — not demand or pricing — as the binding constraint on GLP-1 revenue realization.

Capacity expansion is converting rapidly into incremental revenue. In H1 2025, Ricks reported that incretin sellable doses "produced more than 1.6 times" the prior year comparable period ([S&P Global, 2025](https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/research/2025/08/eli-lilly-set-to-leapfrog-novo-nordisk-in-weight-loss-drug-race)).

This establishes a classic **"invest now, harvest later"** capital cycle:
- **CapEx peak phase (2024–2027)**: ROIC temporarily suppressed by heavy capital deployment
- **Harvest phase (2027+)**: As new facilities reach full utilization, incremental revenue carries near-zero incremental CapEx, driving ROIC toward 30%+ (Ricks has characterized this as the largest capacity expansion in pharmaceutical history)

The gross margin trajectory to 83.0% in 2025 already reflects the favorable unit economics of GLP-1 products — high ASPs, relatively stable COGS per dose as scale increases, and minimal incremental selling costs for a drug with near-universal awareness. Operating margin at 40.3% and Performance Margin guidance of 46–47.5% for 2026 suggest the harvest phase is beginning to materialize ahead of the full CapEx build-out completion.
