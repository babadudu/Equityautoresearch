### Key Inflection Points

#### 1. FY2021–FY2022: Pandemic-Driven Demand Surge (Structural, Not Transitory)

COVID-19 triggered a structural shift in bulk-purchasing behavior. Revenue growth accelerated to 17.5% in FY2021 and 15.8% in FY2022 — the two fastest growth years in the decade-long dataset. Diluted EPS jumped from $9.02 (FY2020) to $13.14 (FY2022), a 46% increase in two years.

Critically, this was not a one-time shock that later reversed. Post-pandemic revenue did not normalize downward. FY2023–FY2025 maintained steady 5–8% annual growth from the elevated FY2022 base, confirming that consumer habits shifted permanently toward value-oriented bulk purchasing. This validates the Costco thesis that a crisis can act as a permanent customer acquisition event.

#### 2. FY2024–FY2025: Operating Leverage and Margin Expansion

Operating margin expanded from 3.1% in FY2019 to 3.8% in FY2025 — a 70 basis point improvement. Net margin rose from 2.4% to 2.9% over the same period. These appear modest in percentage-point terms but represent significant absolute dollar expansion on a $275B revenue base.

To quantify the leverage: if FY2025 net margin had remained at FY2019 levels (2.4%), net income would have been approximately $6.6B instead of $8.1B. The additional $1.5B in profit (roughly 23% upside) is attributable entirely to margin expansion — without any additional revenue growth assumption.

Three structural drivers of margin improvement:
- **(a) Membership fee revenue growing faster than operating costs:** Membership fee income is essentially pure profit (near-100% margin), and fee base growth from new warehouses plus 2017 and 2024 price increases compounds over time.
- **(b) Kirkland Signature mix shift:** Costco's private-label brand carries structurally higher gross margins than comparable national brands. As Kirkland's share of total sales increases, blended gross margin improves.
- **(c) Fixed cost dilution from warehouse growth:** Each new warehouse adds incremental fixed overhead, but mature warehouses run at higher utilization, spreading corporate and distribution costs across a larger revenue base.

#### 3. FY2017: Membership Fee Increase — A Test of Pricing Power

In June 2017, Costco raised its Gold Star membership fee from $55 to $60 and its Executive membership from $110 to $120. This was the first fee increase since 2012, a five-year gap that had generated market concern about Costco's ability to sustain pricing power.

The market initially worried about member attrition. The outcome disproved that concern: renewal rates remained above 90% following the increase, demonstrating that Costco's value proposition is strong enough that members absorb fee increases without churning. This is a high-quality test of brand loyalty — members paying more and staying — rather than a theoretical moat claim.

Costco repeated this in September 2024, raising Gold Star fees to $65 and Executive fees to $130. Renewal rates remained robust, reinforcing that the membership fee mechanism is a repeatable, durable source of incremental income.

Historical fee increase timeline:
- 2012: $55 / $110
- 2017: $60 / $120 (+9% Gold Star)
- 2024: $65 / $130 (+8% Gold Star)

Note: Membership fee revenue is treated as deferred income and recognized over the 12-month membership period, providing a revenue smoothing effect.

#### 4. FY2019: Shanghai Opening — Asian Market Validation

In 2019, Costco opened its first China location in Shanghai. The opening-day response was so overwhelming — with crowds causing traffic gridlock around the warehouse — that the store temporarily closed on its first day of operations. This real-world stress test validated that the Costco membership warehouse model translates to Asian consumer preferences, specifically in a market where the combination of perceived Western-brand authenticity and bulk-value pricing had not been systematically tested at this scale.

The Shanghai opening established the precedent and operational playbook for China expansion and demonstrated that international growth — particularly in high-income urban Asian markets — represents a credible long-term growth vector.

### Decade-Level Trend Summary

**Revenue CAGR FY2015–FY2025:** From $116.2B to $275.2B = approximately **9.0% CAGR** over 10 years.

**EPS CAGR FY2015–FY2025:** From $5.37 to $18.21 = approximately **12.9% CAGR** — meaningfully faster than revenue growth, reflecting both margin expansion and share buybacks.

**Gross margin trend:** Relatively stable at 12–13% throughout the decade, consistent with Costco's pricing philosophy of passing savings to members. The FY2022 dip to 12.2% (likely reflecting supply chain cost inflation) has since recovered to 12.8% in FY2025.

**Operating margin trend:** A slow but consistent upward drift from 3.1% (FY2015) to 3.8% (FY2025), driven primarily by membership fee leverage rather than product margin expansion — this is the structural signature of the Costco business model.
