## Costco DCF Valuation & Multi-Metric Valuation Analysis (FY2026–2035)

### Multi-Metric Valuation Comparison

As of the analysis date, Costco (COST) trades at significant premiums to both its own historical averages and peer comparables across all major valuation metrics:

| Metric | Current | 5-Year Average | Peer Comparison | Implication |
|--------|---------|---------------|-----------------|-------------|
| P/E (TTM) | 54.6x | ~38x | WMT 32x / BJ's 22x | Significant premium reflecting quality scarcity |
| EV/EBITDA | ~37x | ~27x | WMT 17x / BJ's 13x | Near historical highs |
| P/FCF | ~55x | ~35x | WMT 28x / BJ's 18x | Above historical average |

Costco's current P/E of 54.6x compares to Walmart at 32x and BJ's Wholesale at 22x, reflecting the market's willingness to pay a persistent quality premium for the warehouse club leader's membership model and predictable cash flows.

---

### WACC Decomposition

The discount rate used in the DCF model is derived as follows:

- **Risk-free rate (Rf):** 4.3% (10-year U.S. Treasury yield)
- **Equity Risk Premium (ERP):** 5.5%
- **Beta (β):** 0.75 (low beta reflecting defensive consumer staples profile)
- **Company-specific premium:** 0% (market leader, no material idiosyncratic risk)
- **WACC = 4.3% + 5.5% × 0.75 = 8.4%**

The low beta of 0.75 is consistent with Costco's membership-driven revenue model, which provides high recurring visibility and resilience across economic cycles. The absence of a company-specific risk premium reflects Costco's dominant competitive position and balance sheet strength.

### DCF Model Assumptions

- **Forecast period:** 10 years (FY2026–FY2035)
- **Revenue growth:** 8% per annum for years 1–5 (FY2026–FY2030), stepping down to 6% per annum for years 6–10 (FY2031–FY2035)
- **Net margin:** Gradual improvement from 3.0% to 3.2% over the forecast period
- **Terminal growth rate:** 3.0%
- **WACC:** 8.4%
- **Shares outstanding:** 445 million (assumed stable; minimal buyback activity given Costco's capital return policy)

### Three-Scenario IRR Analysis (5-Year Holding Period from ~$1,050)

| Scenario | Probability | 5Y Target Price | IRR | IRR Decomposition |
|----------|------------|----------------|-----|-------------------|
| Bull (10% growth + 40x P/E) | 20% | $1,280 | 4.0% | Growth +10% / Multiple −5.5% / Dividend +0.5% |
| Base (8% growth + 35x P/E) | 55% | $1,010 | −0.8% | Growth +8% / Multiple −8.5% / Dividend +0.7% |
| Bear (6% growth + 30x P/E) | 25% | $790 | −5.5% | Growth +6% / Multiple −11% / Dividend +0.5% |
| **Probability-Weighted** | | **~$1,010** | **−0.7%** | |

Key observations from the scenario analysis:

1. **Multiple compression is the dominant risk.** Even in the bull case — where Costco sustains 10% top-line growth and the market holds a 40x P/E multiple — the IRR is only 4.0%. This is because multiple compression from the current ~55x drags returns by 5.5 percentage points.

2. **In the base case, COST is a value destroyer.** At 8% revenue growth (above historical averages) with a still-elevated 35x terminal multiple, the 5-year IRR is −0.8%. The stock would essentially be flat at ~$1,010 in five years despite strong underlying business execution.

3. **The bear case implies material capital loss.** At 6% growth and 30x terminal P/E (still above Sam's Club parent Walmart's current multiple), the stock declines to ~$790, generating a −5.5% annual return.

4. **The probability-weighted IRR is −0.7%,** implying that at current prices, investors are paying for a business that will compound but not generate equity returns above the risk-free rate of 4.3%.

### Valuation Conclusion

The DCF model indicates Costco's intrinsic value is approximately **$433/share** at an 8.4% WACC — representing a ~59% discount to the current market price of $1,050. The market is implicitly assigning a perpetuity-like growth premium that is difficult to justify on a risk-adjusted return basis.

**At current prices, Costco has virtually no margin of safety.** The stock fully prices in every structural advantage — membership model durability, pricing power, international expansion, and execution excellence — leaving forward investors exposed to multiple compression without a commensurate earnings acceleration.

Three structural reasons why the market has historically accepted this premium:

1. **Quality scarcity premium:** Institutional investors seeking defensive, high-quality compounders have few alternatives in the retail sector; COST serves as a low-volatility anchor position.
2. **Membership revenue predictability premium:** The subscription-like fee income (~$4.8B annually) warrants a DCF premium over traditional retail, as it resembles recurring SaaS revenue.
3. **Hybrid 'bond + growth' positioning:** Costco is perceived as offering bond-like downside protection (low beta, predictable cash flows) with equity-like upside optionality — a rare combination that commands structural multiple expansion from certain allocator mandates.

However, even granting these structural premiums, the math is challenging: the 5-year probability-weighted IRR of −0.7% compares unfavorably to a 4.3% risk-free rate, suggesting the risk/reward is unattractive for new positions at $1,050.

> **Analyst note:** Costco's valuation has persistently exceeded 'fair' levels — the 5-year average P/E of ~38x was itself elevated — yet the market has continuously re-rated the stock higher. Investors should distinguish between (1) Costco being a great business and (2) COST being a great stock at current prices. The two propositions are currently in tension.
