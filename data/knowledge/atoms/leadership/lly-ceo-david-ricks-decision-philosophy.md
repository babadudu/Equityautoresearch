## David A. Ricks — Seven First-Principles of Leadership

The following seven principles are not post-hoc rationalizations. Each is corroborated by cross-year decisions, repeated verbatim across multiple interview contexts, and directly traceable to specific capital allocation or strategic choices during Ricks' 2017–2026 CEO tenure.

---

### Principle 1: Start with Human Suffering, Work Backwards

Ricks' most stable and central conviction: Lilly's scale obliges it to attack the largest health problems, not niche markets. He stated explicitly: "Lilly, with its massive scale, is not being built to make niche drugs for niche diseases. Lots of companies can do that — and do do that. We start with: What's human suffering? And work backwards" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)).

Noteworthy: he does not frame this as idealism. He is a self-described capitalist — "I'm a capitalist. I like customers" ([IBJ, 2018](https://www.ibj.com/articles/60488-the-interview-issue-dave-ricks)) — who believes large disease markets and large human suffering are the same thing.

This principle drove three landmark decisions: (1) Elanco spin-off (2017–2019), concentrating all resources on human pharmaceutical needs; (2) Continued Alzheimer's investment after the 2016 solanezumab Phase 3 failure — Ricks told Chief Scientific Officer Skovronsky: "Right, get on with it. We don't need to talk about it anymore. It's a good strategy. Full speed ahead" ([Endpoints News, 2024](https://endpoints.news/failure-faith-and-8-billion-how-lillys-amyloid-conviction-brought-it-to-the-brink-of-alzheimers-approval/)), ultimately spending $8B+ before Kisunla was approved in 2024; (3) Announcing large GLP-1 trials in addiction (alcohol, tobacco, drug abuse) in 2024: "These medicines can be useful for other things we don't think about connected to weight... They're reducing that desire cycle. So next year, you'll see Lilly start large studies in alcohol abuse and nicotine use, even in drug abuse" ([Economic Club DC, 2024-12-10](https://www.economicclub.org/events/david-ricks)).

### Principle 2: Accept Discovery's Fundamental Uncertainty; Use Institutions to Reduce Decision Error Rate

Ricks positions pharma as a genuine discovery industry: "Our business is one of the few businesses that you do actual discovery. It's not development purely. You really don't know the answer... It's literally like the card flipping over and you win the hand or you don't" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)).

To manage this uncertainty, he introduced three institutional decision mechanisms:

**Layer 1 — Moneyball-style quantitative process**: "We've actually put together a decision process that's quite a bit more rigorous than it used to be and that leads to fewer bad decisions" ([All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)), modeled on Billy Beane's baseball analytics approach ([Bell Pioneer, 2025](https://www.bellpioneer.com/insights/the-better-version-of-nice-inside-ceo-david-ricks-leadership-philosophy-at-eli-lilly)).

**Layer 2 — Mandatory cooling period**: "We actually have a rule to never decide in one meeting" ([All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)) — all major R&D decisions require at least two meetings to prevent groupthink and anchoring.

**Layer 3 — CEO direct science engagement**: Ricks makes only "three or four important decisions... and they're all science" per year ([All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)), while spending "25, 30% of my time working with the scientific team to point that capability in the right direction" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)). He uses AI in real-time during meetings: "I have at least one or two AIs running every minute of every meeting I'm in, and I just am asking it science questions. Either Claude or the xAI one. I find it more terse and the references actually check out more often" ([All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)).

Key corollary: tirzepatide originated from an "off strategy" project. Ricks reflected: "I guarantee you that obesity was not on our corporate strategy document 10 years ago. But tirzepatide was first formulated in 2011. So, someone was off strategy, and it may be the biggest drug in the history of our industry" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)). He deliberately preserves "allowable variation" — "skunk... you just turn away and see what happens" ([All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)).

### Principle 3: Actions Define Culture; Words Do Not

Ricks discovered that explicit culture programs are largely ineffective; what changes organizations is what leaders actually do. This insight appears consistently across 2019–2025 interviews with nearly identical phrasing, indicating a deeply held and validated belief.

2019: "What you model doing — the shadow you cast — matters, because that's what people think you're doing" ([Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)).

2025: "The words matter, but not that much. What matters is what you do. What and who you pay attention to" ([Bell Pioneer, 2025](https://www.bellpioneer.com/insights/the-better-version-of-nice-inside-ceo-david-ricks-leadership-philosophy-at-eli-lilly)).

2025: "Almost everything I tried to do explicitly didn't work. But everything I did implicitly stuck" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)).

His cultural north star: "Tough on problems, soft on people" — with the caveat that "soft on people" does not mean low standards: "If you're not playing your position, and you're not passing the ball, then you don't deserve to be on the team. It is an honor to have a Lilly badge. You've got to uphold that" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)). His performance bar is explicit: "The company grew 45 percent in Q1. Did you grow 45 percent in Q1? As a leader, the implications are that if not, you're actually slowing us down" (same source).

### Principle 4: R&D Is a Moat, Not a Cost — Scale Itself Is a Barrier

Ricks' capital allocation hierarchy is unambiguous: R&D first, manufacturing capacity second, buybacks third. He repeatedly quantifies Lilly's R&D scale in national terms: "This year, Lilly will spend $14.5 billion. That's above Germany and Japan as a nation and headed closer to the NIH" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)). "We have more than 4,000 PhDs doing research. That's more than MIT or Harvard. We do real science. We do it at scale" ([Bell Pioneer, 2025](https://www.bellpioneer.com/insights/the-better-version-of-nice-inside-ceo-david-ricks-leadership-philosophy-at-eli-lilly)).

He claims industry-leading R&D ROI: "We have probably the highest return on investment in R&D in the industry because... the process by which we run it" ([All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)).

Critical institutional change: "R&D is now a corporate decision where segment heads can no longer cut R&D to protect margins" ([Bell Pioneer, 2025](https://www.bellpioneer.com/insights/the-better-version-of-nice-inside-ceo-david-ricks-leadership-philosophy-at-eli-lilly)) — elevating research spend from a divisional cost line to a company-level inviolable investment.

### Principle 5: Organic Growth Over Acquisition — Anti-M&A Heterodoxy

In an industry defined by mega-mergers, Ricks holds a contrarian view: "I haven't a shred of evidence that any of those big mergers have created value for shareholders or other constituents. They've probably destroyed value" ([IBJ, 2018](https://www.ibj.com/articles/60488-the-interview-issue-dave-ricks)). His preferred alternative: "The key is launching products and growing, not acquiring things and cutting costs" (same source).

Lilly under Ricks completed roughly two dozen acquisitions totaling approximately $3B — many small bets rather than one transformative deal ([Bell Pioneer, 2025](https://www.bellpioneer.com/insights/the-better-version-of-nice-inside-ceo-david-ricks-leadership-philosophy-at-eli-lilly)). He acknowledges biotech's importance but prefers partnership over integration: "The origin of... more than half of medicines approved in the last 10 years have come from biotech... I don't think anyone's really pursuing just fully integrated anymore. I think your head's in the sand, if you're doing that" ([All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)). The 2025 $2.5B Scorpion Therapeutics acquisition (oncology pipeline) remains his largest single deal — still far below peer-company acquisitions.

On market capitalization itself: "I think of [market cap] as more like an outcome that is derived from focusing on the things that create the value, not a goal by itself" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)) — throughout Lilly's growth from $70B to $770B+ he never cited market cap as a target.

### Principle 6: Fearlessness About Breaking and Rebuilding

Ricks draws from a Chinese plant manager's aphorism: "To be a great plant manager you have to love the plant enough to break it and put it back together." Applied to Lilly's CEO role: "When I became CEO I really loved the company. I still very much do. But you have to be not afraid to push it to be a lot better" ([Bell Pioneer, 2025](https://www.bellpioneer.com/insights/the-better-version-of-nice-inside-ceo-david-ricks-leadership-philosophy-at-eli-lilly)).

On the GLP-1 revolution: "Why didn't these things happen earlier? I don't know, but I think people feel constrained by norms, and we just pushed beyond them" ([TIME100 Health, 2025](https://time.com/collections/time100-health-2025/7279659/david-ricks/)).

The insulin pricing crisis demonstrates this principle in action. Ricks publicly acknowledged systemic industry failure: "Our actual net on insulin really hasn't changed. It's like $30-$40, but the list price got up to 275. We competed on this spread... We kept raising the list price" ([All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)). In 2023 Lilly proactively cut Humalog list price to $35 — the first major insulin maker to do so — and launched LillyDirect, a direct-to-consumer platform that directly challenges the PBM intermediary system.

---

### Principle 7: Speed as Competitive Advantage — "Failing Faster" Is a Moat

Ricks inverts the pharmaceutical industry's reputation for deliberateness: "We can go faster and we've cut our times in roughly half. So we're failing a lot faster than others and that's an advantage" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)).

He distinguishes two categories of failure: "Sometimes you fail and you learn, and sometimes you fail, and it just wasn't a good idea" ([T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)). The Alzheimer's program belongs to the former — five or six Phase 3 failures over 30 years, yet persistence yielded Kisunla. The lesson from incretin history: "It's rarely the case in our sector that the first thing is the best thing" (same source). Byetta → Trulicity → tirzepatide → retatrutide and orforglipron represent iterative compounding.

The tirzepatide execution is the clearest speed case. When Phase 2 data emerged in 2016: "The team breathlessly called and said, 'Okay, we've got the phase-two results and this is going to change diabetes and probably beyond.' Since that day, it's been all execution. We need to make more, we need to do more trials, we need to make this an important global product. It wasn't difficult to see that... What was difficult was doing it" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)).

### Internal Tensions & How They Resolve

These seven principles are not independent — they exist in productive tension:

- **P2 (institutional rigor) vs P6 (break and rebuild)**: Institutions prevent random errors; structural transformation requires overriding them. Ricks resolves this by maintaining the cooling-period rule for deliberation but acting decisively once clarity emerges — "Full speed ahead."
- **P3 (actions define culture) vs P1 (mission-driven)**: Mission sets direction; only action transmits it through the organization. Ricks avoids culture campaigns and instead ensures scientists see the company genuinely investing in the hardest problems.
- **P5 (anti-M&A) vs P4 (scale is a moat)**: He opposes *acquiring* scale; he endorses *building* scale organically through R&D and manufacturing. This explains why he simultaneously rejects large M&A and commits $55B in CapEx expansion.
- **P7 (speed) vs P2 (deliberateness)**: Deliberate under uncertainty (cooling-period rule, quantitative process); full-speed once results are clear (tirzepatide post-Phase 2).

The unifying logic: pharmaceutical innovation requires simultaneously scientific rigor and entrepreneurial decisiveness — "There won't be innovation if people don't have an incentive to invest at risk" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)).
