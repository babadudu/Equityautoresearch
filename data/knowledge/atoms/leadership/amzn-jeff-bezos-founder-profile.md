## Jeff Bezos — Founder & Executive Chairman (CEO 1994–2021)

### Background and Education

Jeffrey Preston Bezos was born January 12, 1964 in Albuquerque, New Mexico. He demonstrated an early passion for technology and space exploration. In 1986 he graduated summa cum laude from Princeton University with a degree in Electrical Engineering and Computer Science. He then entered finance, working at Fitel and Bankers Trust before joining hedge fund D.E. Shaw in 1990, where by age 30 he became the firm's youngest Senior Vice President.

### The Founding Decision (1994)

In 1994, Bezos identified that internet usage was growing at 2,300% per year. He applied what he later called the "Regret Minimization Framework" to decide whether to leave his Wall Street career: "I knew that when I was 80, I was not going to regret having tried this. I was going to regret not having tried. I knew that that would haunt me every day." ([2010 Princeton Commencement Speech](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vBmavMmz9WM))

In July 1994 Bezos founded Amazon in a Seattle garage, starting with books. The rationale was explicit first-principles logic: books had more SKUs than any other retail category. In a 1997 Charlie Rose interview he explained: "There are more items in the book category than there are items in any other category, by far. Music is No. 2 — there are about 200,000 active music CDs at any given time. But in the book space, there are over 3 million different books worldwide active in print at any given time." He added: "When you have that many items, you can literally build a store online that couldn't exist any other way." ([CNBC, Jan 2024 — citing 1997 Charlie Rose interview](https://www.cnbc.com/amp/2024/01/13/in-1997-jeff-bezos-said-why-he-chose-books-to-sell-on-amazon.html)) The point of entry was not chosen for margin; it was chosen because the long-tail SKU breadth of books made an online store permanently superior to any physical store.

### Early Fundraising

Bezos recalled the difficulty of Amazon's seed round: "The riskiest moment for Amazon was at the very, very beginning. I needed to raise $1 million at a certain point, and I ended up giving away 20 percent of the company for a million dollars." He approached 60 potential investors before securing that first round. ([Charlie Rose — cited by GeekWire, 2013](https://www.geekwire.com/2013/jeff-bezos-60-meetings-raise-1m-amazoncom-giving-20-early-investors/))

### Early Vision: The "Kittyhawk Stage" (1997–1999)

At the May 15, 1997 IPO Bezos had already framed Amazon as a Day 1 story. In the same 1997 Charlie Rose interview he invoked aviation history: "This is Day 1. This is the very beginning. This is the Kittyhawk stage of electronic commerce." — Kittyhawk being the site of the Wright Brothers' first flight. He simultaneously maintained a pragmatic caution: "Basically, right now, if you can do things using a more traditional method, you probably should do them using the traditional method." ([CNBC, Jan 2024](https://www.cnbc.com/amp/2024/01/13/in-1997-jeff-bezos-said-why-he-chose-books-to-sell-on-amazon.html))

By 1999, with the stock above $100, Bezos told CNBC: "There's no guarantee that Amazon.com can be a successful company. What we're trying to do is very complicated." Yet he held to scale-first strategy: "Scale is important to us and we're going to go after that kind of scale." ([CEO Today, Feb 2019 — citing 1999 CNBC interview](https://www.ceotodaymagazine.com/2019/02/jeff-bezos-in-1999-amazons-plans-before-the-dotcom-crash/)) This combination of acknowledging uncertainty while committing to long-term investment is considered central to Amazon surviving the 2000–2001 dot-com crash.

### Garage Era and Customer-First Culture Origin (1995–1997)

In the earliest days, Bezos and the founding team hand-packed books in a garage. He later described the discomfort — "This packing is killing me... my back hurts, this is killing my knees" — and how a colleague's simple suggestion to get packing tables was "the most brilliant idea I'd ever heard," instantly doubling efficiency. He reflected: "[That early unprepared stage was] probably [one of the] luckiest things that ever happened to us." ([Bezos "Amazing Amazon Story" Speech, ~2001](https://singjupost.com/amazing-amazon-story-jeff-bezos-full-speech-transcript/)) That period of resource scarcity is credited with forging Amazon's customer-back problem-solving culture.

### Key Milestones

- **1997 IPO** at $18/share, market cap $438M. First shareholder letter established the long-term mantra: "We will continue to make investment decisions in light of long-term market leadership considerations rather than short-term profitability considerations." ([1997 Letter to Shareholders](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1018724/000119312516530910/d168744dex991.htm))
- **2000–2001 Dot-Com Crash**: Stock fell from $113 to $6; Amazon cut 1,300 employees. Bezos persisted, launching Marketplace. He later reflected: "The stock is not the company. And the company is not the stock. When the stock went down 80%, it was very liberating. We knew we were right." ([Economic Club of Washington, Sep 2018](https://www.economicclub.org/events/jeff-bezos))
- **2005**: Launched Amazon Prime at $79/year for free two-day shipping.
- **2006**: Launched AWS (S3 and EC2), creating the public cloud market — an outcome of Bezos's internal Service-Oriented Architecture mandate requiring all Amazon teams to expose their capabilities as APIs.
- **2013**: Acquired Washington Post for $250M.
- **2017**: Acquired Whole Foods for $13.7B, entering physical retail.
- **2021**: Handed CEO role to Andy Jassy; became Executive Chairman to focus on Blue Origin and Day 1 Fund.

### Core Management Philosophy

Bezos identified three principles as the root cause of Amazon's 18-year record of success — principles he believed most companies had not implemented even one of: (1) **Customer Obsession**, (2) **Long-Term Thinking**, (3) **Willingness to Invent**.

On competition: "We don't focus on the competition, we focus on the customer. When people ask me what keeps you up at night, I say, 'Our customers.' Not our competitors." ([2016 Economic Club of Washington](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zN1YiCViXLM))

On the alignment of long-term thinking with shareholders: "If you're long term oriented, customer interests and shareholder interests are aligned. In the short term, that's not always correct." He cited Benjamin Graham: "In the short term, the stock market is a voting machine. In the long term, it's a weighing machine." — and stated Amazon's goal was "to be weighed and not voted upon." ([HBR IdeaCast, Jan 2013](https://hbr.org/podcast/2013/01/jeff-bezos-on-leading-for-the))

On margins vs. cash flow: "Percentage margins are not one of the things we are seeking to optimize. It's the absolute dollar-free cash flow per share that you want to maximize, and if you can do that by lowering margins, we would do that." ([HBR IdeaCast, Jan 2013](https://hbr.org/podcast/2013/01/jeff-bezos-on-leading-for-the)) This explains why Amazon operated retail at low margins even at hundreds of billions in revenue: the optimization target was never margin percentage but total cash generated.

On investment horizons: Some of the most important things Amazon built "could not be done in two to three years. If we needed to see meaningful financial results in two to three years, some of the most meaningful things we've done we would never have even started." Kindle, AWS, and Prime all fall in this category.

### Ethics and Values

At his 2010 Princeton commencement address, Bezos shared a formative childhood memory. At age 10 he calculated how many years his grandmother's smoking was shortening her life, expecting praise, but instead made her cry. His grandfather took him aside: "Jeff, one day, you'll understand that it's harder to be kind than clever." Bezos drew the distinction: "Cleverness is a gift. Kindness is a choice. Gifts are easy — they're given, after all. Choices can be hard." He concluded: "In the end, we are our choices. Build yourself a great story." ([2010 Princeton Commencement](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vBmavMmz9WM)) This belief that character is constituted by choices — not talent — is the philosophical foundation for Bezos's repeated decisions to sacrifice short-term results for long-term value.

At the July 2020 Congressional antitrust hearing, Bezos stated: "I believe Amazon should be scrutinized. We should scrutinize all large institutions, whether they're companies, government agencies, or non-profits." He also argued for context: "Amazon accounts for less than 1% of the $25 trillion global retail market and less than 4% of retail in the U.S." ([Congressional Testimony, Jul 2020](https://www.congress.gov/116/meeting/house/110883/witnesses/HHRG-116-JU05-Wstate-BezosJ-20200729.pdf))

### Failure Philosophy

- **Fire Phone (2014)**: Amazon's smartphone attempt resulted in $170M+ write-down. Bezos's view: "If the size of your failures isn't growing, you're not going to be inventing at a size that can actually move the needle."
- On willingness to be misunderstood: "Long term orientation is essential for invention because you're going to have a lot of failures along the way." He added that pioneers need "a willingness to be misunderstood for long periods of time" — when Amazon introduced customer reviews, publishers objected, but Bezos held firm because "we're not going to make money selling things. We're going to make money helping people make purchase decisions." ([HBR IdeaCast, Jan 2013](https://hbr.org/podcast/2013/01/jeff-bezos-on-leading-for-the))
- Other notable failures: Amazon Destinations (travel booking, shut down), Haven Healthcare (healthcare JV with JPMorgan and Berkshire Hathaway, shut down).
