## Jim Sinegal: Costco Co-Founder — From FedMart Stock Boy to 29-Year CEO

### Origins: The Sol Price Apprenticeship

James D. Sinegal was born in 1936 in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, to an Italian-American working-class family. In 1954, at age 18, he took a part-time bagger's job at FedMart — the discount retail store Sol Price had founded that same year in San Diego, which reached $3M in revenue in its first year ([Acquired Podcast - Costco, 2023/08/20](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/costco)).

From that moment, Sinegal worked under Sol Price for 29 consecutive years. When asked what he learned from Price, his answer has become one of the most-quoted lines in retail history: *"No, that's inaccurate. I didn't learn a lot, I learned everything. Absolutely everything I know, I learned from Sol."* ([Acquired Podcast - Costco, 2023/08/20](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/costco)). He elaborated elsewhere: *"I was like a fly on the wall watching Sol. I watched everything he did and I learned everything from him."* ([Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal))

### The Price Club Inheritance

In 1976, Sol Price founded Price Club — the world's first membership warehouse club. Price Club's core innovations, which Sinegal absorbed firsthand during his years there, included:
- Member-selection model (initially restricted to business owners)
- Extreme SKU discipline (~3,000 items)
- Negative cash conversion cycle (merchandise sold before suppliers paid)
- The $1.50 hot dog + drink combo (originally vendor-initiated, later selling 130 million annually)

Sinegal witnessed and participated in every stage of these innovations, rising from bagger to management over his 29-year tenure.

### 1983: Co-founding Costco

At age 47, Sinegal partnered with attorney Jeff Brotman to found Costco in Seattle. The model was a direct replication of Sol Price's Price Club, executed more aggressively:
- Sub-3-year path to $1B revenue; $3B by year 6
- IPO in 1985, just two years after founding

On skeptics of the model: *"Everyone is always going to question what business model you have. They're going to say you probably won't be in business six months from now."* ([MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success))

### 1993: The Student Absorbs the Teacher

In June 1993, Costco and Price Club merged. The combined entity had ~200 warehouses and ~$16B in revenue. Sinegal's company — founded a decade after Price Club — had effectively absorbed its template. Sol Price, characteristically self-aware, noted: *"We're good at creating businesses, we're not as good at running businesses."* ([Acquired Podcast - Costco, 2023/08/20](https://www.acquired.fm/episodes/costco))

### 29-Year CEO Tenure: 1983–2012

Over his tenure, Sinegal grew Costco from one location to 600+, from $0 to $99B in revenue, from zero to 60M+ members.

**The Four Ethical Commandments**

Sinegal distilled his management philosophy into four non-negotiable obligations, in order: *"We think we have four things we have to do. We have to obey the law, take care of our customers, take care of our employees and respect our suppliers. Pretty much in that order."* ([Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal))

**The High-Wage Doctrine**

Against sustained Wall Street pressure, Sinegal insisted on wages materially above retail industry norms: *"No one was going to be able to say we're making money off the backs of our employees, because we were going to pay the highest wages in all of retail."* ([MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success)). The economic logic: *"If I could stock every shelf, ring every register, buy every product and push every cart, I would. But I can't, and you've got to hire people to do it. You want them to do it as well as if you were doing it, and you only do that by teaching."* ([MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success))

Sinegal's own compensation modeled this philosophy: his CEO salary remained at approximately $350K (plus ~$200K bonus) for years — a figure widely cited as anomalously low relative to his company's scale and peer CEOs.

**Pricing Discipline as a Non-Negotiable**

Sinegal used a memorable metaphor to describe the temptation to break pricing discipline: *"We pass the savings on to the customer, every time. Do you know how tempting it is to make another $7 on a pair? But once you do it, it's like taking heroin. You can't stop."* ([MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success))

**Growth Restraint**

Despite aggressive early expansion, Sinegal was explicit about avoiding the overextension trap: *"We're not kamikaze pilots. We want to do things in a sensible fashion."* And: *"We don't have any grand scheme that says, for example, that we have to be in Latin America by the year 2015 or have 1000 Costco's in ten years."* ([Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal))

**Delegation and Teaching**

Sinegal on management: *"The biggest mistake any boss can make is running around and doing everything yourself. If you haven't learned to delegate, you haven't learned a very valuable lesson."* ([MIT Sloan](https://mitsloan.mit.edu/ideas-made-to-matter/how-costcos-obsession-culture-drove-success)). He was also explicit about internal talent development: *"We home-grow all of our management."* ([Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal))

**Store Visit Approach**

Sinegal was known for hands-on warehouse visits: *"I try to approach the visits from the standpoint of a customer. Does the building have the right goods out? Is it well-stocked and clean and safe?"* ([Investment Masters Class](http://mastersinvest.com/newblog/2019/7/5/learning-from-costcos-jim-sinegal))

### Legacy

Sinegal retired in 2012 after 29 years as CEO. His influence continues to define Costco's operating culture under both his successors. The three-CEO succession (Sinegal → Jelinek → Vachris), each a long-tenured internal promotion, is itself a testament to the culture-building infrastructure he created. In 2012 he spoke at the Democratic National Convention, reflecting his stature beyond the business world.
