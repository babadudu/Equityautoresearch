## Morris Chang（張忠謀）— TSMC Founder

### Background & Education

Morris Chang was born in 1931 in Ningbo, Zhejiang, China. He emigrated to the United States in 1949, initially studying at Harvard University before transferring to MIT, where he earned both a bachelor's and master's degree in mechanical engineering. He subsequently earned a PhD in electrical engineering from Stanford University.

On his early life: "The early part of my life between birth and 18, the background was war, poverty, injustice." — the childhood forged in wartime shaped his later resilience. ([Source: Synergy Startup Substack](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang))

### Career Before TSMC

In 1955, Chang joined Sylvania Semiconductor, beginning his semiconductor career. Three years later he concluded: "Sylvania wasn't going anywhere in semiconductors." ([Source: Synergy Startup Substack](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)) In 1958 he joined Texas Instruments (TI), rising over 25 years to Vice President of Global Semiconductor Business. During his TI tenure he led multiple process innovations, developing deep insight into semiconductor manufacturing scale economics and learning curve effects.

In 1983 he left TI, saying: "I left TI without any job offer, and I left because I felt that essentially I had been put out to pasture at TI." ([Source: Synergy Startup Substack](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)) He briefly joined General Instrument — "General Instrument was mainly about acquisitions, mergers...it was just very different from what I was used to at Texas Instruments" — before accepting a Taiwan government invitation in 1985 to lead ITRI (工研院).

### Founding TSMC (1987): The Decision Logic

In 1987, after two years at ITRI, Chang founded TSMC and pioneered the "pure-play foundry" model. His thesis was that as IC design costs fell while manufacturing costs soared, a large population of fabless design companies would emerge requiring dedicated foundry services.

"I wanted to establish a company that would be the foundry for the world's semiconductor industry. At that time, there was no such company." — Morris Chang

"When I founded a semiconductor company, there was only one path: to make it a world-class one." ([Source: CommonWealth Publishing, 2024-12-02](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3849))

He attributed TSMC's success to: "a pioneering business model executed to perfection and relentless innovation through proprietary technologies." ([Source: CommonWealth Publishing, 2024-12-02](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3849))

### Winning Apple (2010–2011)

Chang's autobiography (volume 2, published December 2024) disclosed TSMC's path to winning Apple as a customer. On November 9, 2010, Foxconn founder Terry Gou introduced Apple COO Jeff Williams to Chang; Williams immediately expressed Apple's desire for TSMC to manufacture iPhone logic chips. In April 2011, Chang visited Tim Cook in Silicon Valley. Cook signaled that Intel was not a threat in dedicated foundry — Chang's annotation of this exchange articulated his foundry philosophy: "Unwilling to take the price that customers can afford means the same that they were not good at doing dedicated foundry service. What TSMC is good at is making reasonable profits out of the price that is acceptable to the customer." ([Source: TechSoda, Morris Chang autobiography analysis](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography))

In the capacity allocation negotiation with Apple, Chang displayed financial discipline: "I decided that I would take half of what Apple asked for... He said, well, I think you can eliminate your dividend. Your shareholders will understand that. I said, well, no." — even facing a massive order from the world's largest customer, he refused to sacrifice shareholder returns for short-term growth. He also firmly declined an IBM joint R&D proposal: "IBM still consider themselves to be the senior partner in any partnership they established... We declined without having to think about it at all." — protecting TSMC's independent R&D roadmap. ([Source: Acquired Briefing, 2025-01](https://www.acquiredbriefing.com/p/the-morris-chang-interview))

### The Jensen Huang / NVIDIA Relationship

Chang's relationship with Jensen Huang began in 1997 with a letter Huang sent to Hsinchu, introducing NVIDIA as a promising small company. Chang's principle: "We should never be negligent in talking to future customers, even if the customer seems very small." TSMC accepted the order and sent two production planning staff to be embedded at NVIDIA for a month.

Most remarkably, Chang's autobiography reveals he seriously considered inviting Jensen Huang to become TSMC's CEO successor: "Although I was quite familiar with the talent within the company, there was one person outside the company who, in terms of character, vision, professional experience, and knowledge in semiconductors—across all criteria—was worth considering as a successor for TSMC. I knew I should speak with him, and that person was Jensen Huang."

Huang declined: "I spent 10 minutes explaining my deep aspirations of TSMC and how TSMC's package for CEO would be much higher than his income at that time, but he had no question and did not refuse me directly, but simply said, 'I already have a job'." ([Source: TechSoda, Morris Chang autobiography analysis](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography))

### Return from Retirement (2009) & NVIDIA Settlement

Chang retired in 2005 but returned in 2009 due to the financial crisis. One pivotal post-return decision: resolving a legal dispute with NVIDIA. The previous CEO had only been willing to offer zero dollars in settlement; Chang returned and proactively proposed a settlement exceeding $100 million. Jensen Huang accepted "within two days." ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

On talent retention during the 2008–2009 financial crisis, Chang shared a counter-intuitive principle: "People will not respect us if we lay off by performance ratings. Because it's very subjective." And further: "If you need the people back within a year, you shouldn't lay off." — this philosophy preserved the core team through the downturn, building the talent base that powered subsequent 28nm/EUV technology breakthroughs. ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

### Key Milestones

- **1987**: Founded TSMC, invented the pure-play foundry model
- **2005**: First retirement
- **2009**: Returned as CEO amid global financial crisis
- **2018**: Final retirement, handing leadership to Mark Liu (Chairman) and C.C. Wei (CEO) under a co-leadership structure
- **2024 (December)**: Published autobiography volume 2, covering TSMC's history from 1964–2018
- TSMC grew under his leadership to become the world's largest semiconductor company (market cap exceeding $1 trillion)

### Integrity as Non-Negotiable Foundation

Chang spoke to Global Views Monthly (2005): "TSMC's executives are refrained from being anxious for quick success… I try my best to trickle down core company value — Integrity — from the top down." On enforcement: "If there is any executive violating this very principle, with a very heavy heart I must personally handle the situation and ask him/her to resign." His ultimate vision for TSMC: "It is my goal to make TSMC the pure land of maintaining the core value of the society." ([Source: Global Views Monthly / Culc Hsin Hsin Blog, 2005](https://exampleof2015.wordpress.com/2016/03/03/integrity-we-should-carry-on-throughout-all-of-our-life/))

### Geopolitical Worldview

Chang has warned in multiple forums: "Globalization is almost dead. Free trade is almost dead." ([Source: Brookings Institution, 2022-04](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/Vying-for-Talent-Morris-Chang-20220414.pdf)) — a judgment that directly drove TSMC's geographic diversification strategy. On identity: "My identity has always been an American. There is nothing else." and "I am from the United States, and my dream is to build a wafer fab in the United States." ([Source: Andy Lin's Blog, 2024-12-28](https://www.granitefirm.com/blog/us/2024/12/28/morris-changs-controversial/))
