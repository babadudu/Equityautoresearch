## David A. Ricks — Ethics, Moral Track Record & Reflections on Failure

### Framing

Ricks' moral operating system is not rhetorical. The four cases below represent concrete instances where Lilly incurred short-term costs — reputational, financial, or political — in exchange for long-term trust. They form the evidentiary base for evaluating his ethical track record as CEO.

---

### Case 1: Insulin Pricing — Public Admission of Systemic Industry Error

This is Ricks' most prominent demonstration of moral courage. The US insulin pricing crisis caused patients to ration or forgo doses, with reported deaths as a result.

Ricks did not deflect. In a rare public admission, he acknowledged the entire industry's (including Lilly's own) pricing absurdity on the All-In podcast: "Our actual net on insulin really hasn't changed. It's like $30-$40, but the list price got up to 275. We competed on this spread... We kept raising the list price" ([All-In/Cheeky Pint, 2024-10-08](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)).

He used a vivid analogy to expose the absurdity: "Can you imagine this? We go to a restaurant tonight and someone gives us the wine list and a bottle of like a hundred dollar Napa cabernet is like $14,000" (same source).

On patient rationing: "It shouldn't have happened... we're taking steps, we're learning, we're improving, and no one should have to ration their insulin" ([NPR, 2023-03-01](https://www.npr.org/2023/03/01/1160457074/eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks-comments-on-the-companys-new-insulin-price-cuts)).

In March 2023, Lilly became the first major insulin manufacturer to proactively slash its list price — Humalog cut to $35. Ricks explained the progression: "We've been working on improving affordability of insulin for some time, really since 2016, when we first launched a copy of the bestselling insulin from a competitor... Later, we noticed there wasn't anyone working on a competitor to our products that were off patent, so we launched our own generic against ourself" ([NPR, 2023-03-01](https://www.npr.org/2023/03/01/1160457074/eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks-comments-on-the-companys-new-insulin-price-cuts)) — a practice nearly unprecedented in branded pharma.

In May 2023, Ricks testified before Senator Bernie Sanders' Senate HELP Committee. He became the only major pharma CEO to explicitly commit to no further insulin price increases: "We'll leave our prices as they are for the insulins on the market today. In fact, we've been cutting them" ([CNBC, 2023-05-10](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/05/10/eli-lilly-novo-nordisk-sanofi-ceos-on-insulin-prices.html)). He simultaneously exposed PBM economics: "Last year, about 80 percent of our list prices went to pay ever-increasing fees and rebates to companies who don't invent, didn't develop nor manufacture the medicine" (same source). Novo Nordisk and Sanofi CEOs were notably more evasive in the same hearing, making Ricks' transparency a benchmark in the industry's pricing transparency movement.

### Case 2: Racial Justice Commitment — $25M and Institutional Action

Following George Floyd's death in 2020, Ricks positioned Lilly's response as personal rather than corporate strategy: "This is personal for me and for the whole company. We commit to bringing people and organizations together to acknowledge the trauma of racial injustice in its many forms and create a call to action for lasting change" ([Eli Lilly Press Release, 2020-06](https://investor.lilly.com/news-releases/news-release-details/indy-racial-equity-pledge-launches-drive-accountability-action)).

Beyond internal initiatives, Ricks launched the "Indy Racial Equity Pledge" — a multi-company coalition across Indianapolis: "We must confront these racial injustices now, and we must confront them head-on. We're proud to join this coalition and publicly commit to addressing systemic inequities that confront African Americans every day" ([PRNewswire, 2020-10](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/indy-racial-equity-pledge-launches-to-drive-accountability-action-and-progress-on-racial-equity-301148535.html)). He emphasized accountability over statements: "Now is the time for action. Let's hold ourselves accountable for progress" (same source).

Lilly committed $25M and 25,000 volunteer hours to this initiative. By 2022, $15.6M had been deployed, with ongoing progress tracking. Ricks' 2022 statement: "Let our progress serve as inspiration and fuel to stay the course as we live out our commitment to driving purposeful change" ([IBJ, 2022](https://www.ibj.com/articles/lilly-releases-report-card-on-racial-justice)).

His framing of diversity as an innovation driver — not merely a compliance obligation — is consistent with his management philosophy: "The status quo will always be the status quo unless some force moves it... New ideas only come from different ideas, so we need more diversity" ([Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)).

### Case 3: Institutional Transmission of Founder Values

Ricks treats Lilly's 150-year heritage not ceremonially but as a live decision framework. He references a 1975 document authored by founder's grandson Eli Lilly Jr., which articulates what makes the company special: "the pursuit of excellence and integrity, and honesty because the company makes a health product, but also how people are treated" ([Bell Pioneer, 2025](https://www.bellpioneer.com/insights/the-better-version-of-nice-inside-ceo-david-ricks-leadership-philosophy-at-eli-lilly)). This 50-year-old internal document remains a live reference for his organizational design.

He grounds the pharma-industry distinction in moral terms: "It's a hugely technical industry. It's also a hugely important human industry... integrity, about truthfulness" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)). People stay not for compensation but mission: "People will really work for that sort of emotional connection, a sense that we're doing something beyond ourselves" ([T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)).

### Case 4: COVID-19 Antibody Therapeutics — Mission Before Margin

In 2020, Lilly rapidly developed bamlanivimab antibody treatment, receiving FDA Emergency Use Authorization. Ricks described Lilly as a "science-based company" that achieved record-setting development speed ([CBS Face the Nation, 2020-12-20](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/transcript-david-ricks-on-face-the-nation-december-20-2020/)). Lilly supplied the treatment at or below cost — a direct expression of the "start with human suffering" principle.

### Reflections on Failure — Rare CEO-Level Candor

Ricks' public treatment of failure is atypical for a CEO of a $700B+ company. Three distinct dimensions:

**The Alzheimer's marathon**: Ricks personally witnessed five or six Phase 3 failures over decades: "I've personally sat in the readouts of like five or six failed studies" ([T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)). After solanezumab's 2016 failure shook confidence in the amyloid hypothesis, Ricks chose to accelerate rather than retreat: "Right, get on with it. Full speed ahead" ([Endpoints News, 2024](https://endpoints.news/failure-faith-and-8-billion-how-lillys-amyloid-conviction-brought-it-to-the-brink-of-alzheimers-approval/)). Reflection: "sometimes you fail forward, you learn something, and you can adapt, given the scourge of Alzheimer's" (same source). Deeper insight: "Often you change the standard of care forever. Because medicines don't go away... you just move the ratchet of what's possible" ([T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)) — even failed studies advance the field.

**GLP-1 supply crisis**: Rather than deflect, Ricks owned the shortage at the Q4 2024 earnings call: "There's turbulence. I'll own that... this is a market with hundreds of millions of people globally. When we run out, which we did last year, they get very angry with us" ([Q4 2024 Earnings Call, 2025-02-06](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/02/06/eli-lilly-lly-q4-2024-earnings-call-transcript/)). Paragon Intel independently flagged this as a risk area: whether Ricks used external narrative management as a substitute for internal accountability ([Paragon Intel, 2025](https://paragonintel.com/david-ricks-ceo-analysis-lly/)).

**Uncertainty about the future**: When asked to forecast the GLP-1 market trajectory in early 2026: "One thing as we look back at the obesity market, it's probably surprised us at every turn, so I'm reluctant to give out very specific numbers or anything like that" ([BioSpace/JPM26, 2026-01](https://www.biospace.com/business/jpm26-lilly-plots-path-to-dominance-as-obesity-market-enters-a-new-era)) — epistemic humility from a CEO whose company created the market.

**Personal limitations**: "When I turned 50, I started to learn I can't produce enough calorie output to manage this if I don't manage my food input" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)) — his own metabolic experience directly informs his conviction about obesity treatment's medical necessity.

**Control illusion**: "If I have to tell people how to organize and solve problems, we're losing" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)) — acknowledging that CEO micromanagement is itself an organizational failure signal.

### Unified Ethical Logic

Across all four cases, a consistent pattern: Ricks accepts short-term costs (regulatory scrutiny, foregone margin, political exposure) in exchange for long-term institutional trust. His self-description — "I'm a capitalist. I like customers" ([IBJ, 2018](https://www.ibj.com/articles/60488-the-interview-issue-dave-ricks)) — is not in tension with this; it is the same logic. He views ethical behavior and long-term commercial value as highly correlated in an industry where "integrity, about truthfulness" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)) is existentially linked to the product itself.

His view on innovation incentives encapsulates this pragmatic-ethical synthesis: "There won't be innovation if people don't have an incentive to invest at risk" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)) — profit is the fuel for innovation, but the direction of innovation must be determined by human need, not stock price.
