## David A. Ricks — Eli Lilly CEO Profile

### Background and Education

David Ricks is not a typical pharmaceutical industry insider by training. He earned his undergraduate degree at Purdue University, then worked at IBM before returning to Indiana University to pursue an MBA. His relocation to Indianapolis had a notably personal dimension: "his girlfriend (now wife) was in medical school" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)). He joined Eli Lilly in 1996 at an entry level and worked his way through marketing, sales, Canadian operations, and China operations over two decades before reaching the top.

---

### Career Trajectory and Key Inflection Points

**China Business Leadership (2000s):** Ricks was assigned to lead Lilly's China operations, giving him direct experience with the complexity and opportunity of emerging markets — an experience that shaped his understanding of global healthcare dynamics.

**2012 — President of Bio-Medicines Division:** This role placed Ricks at the center of Lilly's most advanced scientific R&D pipeline for the first time, setting the stage for his later engagement with transformative drug programs.

**2016 — The Tirzepatide "Eureka Moment":** Shortly before assuming the CEO role, Ricks received data from a research team that would define his entire tenure: "We had to stop the study because people were losing too much weight to stay in it" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)). He later described his CEO tenure in these terms: "It's been all execution."

**January 2017 — Assumes CEO Role:** Ricks became CEO when Lilly's market capitalization was approximately $70 billion. He is the 11th CEO in Lilly's 150-year history: "I'm the 11th CEO of the company in 150 years" ([T. Rowe Price, 2025](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)).

**2017–2022 — Strategic Transformation:** Ricks dramatically simplified Lilly's portfolio, divesting animal health (spinning off Elanco), exiting non-core assets, and concentrating the company's capital and talent on four therapeutic areas: diabetes, obesity, Alzheimer's disease, and oncology.

**2022–2025 — Explosive Growth Phase:**
- Mounjaro (tirzepatide for T2D) approved: 2022
- Zepbound (tirzepatide for obesity) approved: 2023
- Kisunla (donanemab for Alzheimer's) approved: 2024
- Tirzepatide becomes the world's best-selling drug: 2025
- Market capitalization grows from ~$70B to ~$770B — more than 10x under Ricks's tenure

### Leadership Philosophy

**Science-First, Problem-First Orientation:** Ricks's core belief is that pharmaceutical companies should attack the most significant sources of human suffering rather than pursuing commercial shortcuts. His framework is explicitly backward-looking from patient need: "We start with: What's human suffering? And work backwards."

**Embracing Radical Uncertainty in Drug Discovery:** Ricks approaches pharmaceutical R&D with an unusual philosophical equanimity about failure and serendipity: "Our business is one of the few businesses that you do actual discovery... It's literally like the card flipping over and you win the hand or you don't... Something never happened before—and now it's happened" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)).

**Tolerance for "Off-Strategy" Deviation:** Perhaps the most distinctive element of Ricks's management philosophy is his explicit protection of research programs that deviate from official strategy. Tirzepatide — which he describes as potentially "the biggest drug in the history of our industry" — originated as an "off strategy" project. Middle management tends to suppress such deviations, "but the deviations are the people doing stuff off strategy." This insight has led Ricks to maintain unusually close contact between senior executives and working scientists ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)).

**Balancing Technical Rigor with Human Mission:** Ricks is clear-eyed about the dual nature of his industry: "It's a hugely technical industry. It's also a hugely important human industry... integrity, about truthfulness" ([Fortune, 2024](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)).

**Time Management and Board Relations:** Ricks uses 90-day priority cycles, sharing his plan with the board each quarter: "This is where I'm going to spend my time. Any questions?" Even in strong quarters, he estimates only ~50% of his time lands on planned priorities. He dedicates roughly 20% of his time to site visits — laboratories, manufacturing facilities, and commercial operations — and writes two-paragraph summaries after each: what's working, what needs improvement ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)).

**On Legacy:** When asked about his legacy, Ricks deliberately deflects grand narratives: "It feels like the kind of question where someone with a good answer would have a foot out the door." His authentic concern is continuity of mission as Lilly approaches its 150th anniversary in May 2026: "How can we make sure to continue that invention, that usefulness to solving healthcare problems?" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/))

**On Public Mission in R&D:** As federal health research funding faces potential cuts, Ricks sees both opportunity and responsibility for private pharmaceutical companies: "If we're good, we can direct that at problems that are both big and solvable in a way governments never could" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)).

### Diversity and Leadership by Example

In a Posse Foundation interview, Ricks reflected on diversity and the mechanics of organizational change: "The status quo will always be the status quo unless some force moves it" and "New ideas only come from different ideas, so we need more diversity" ([Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)). He emphasized that leaders influence culture through behavior rather than words: "What you model doing—the shadow you cast—matters." His advice to younger professionals mirrors his own career arc: "Have a sense of adventure! Move around, and don't be afraid to change" ([Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)).

### Insulin Pricing: Rare Public Admission of Industry Failure

Ricks made an unusually candid admission about the insulin pricing dynamic that inflamed public opinion: "Our actual net on insulin really hasn't changed. It's like $30-$40, but the list price got up to 275" and "We competed on this spread... We kept raising the list price" ([Cheeky Pint/All-In, 2024](https://cheekypint.substack.com/p/dave-ricks-ceo-of-eli-lilly-on-glp)). Lilly subsequently took the initiative in 2023 to reduce Humalog's list price to $35, an act of proactive trust-rebuilding ahead of regulatory pressure.

### Succession Risk and Executive Bench Depth

**CEO Profile:** David Ricks was born in 1967 (~59 years old as of 2026) and has been CEO since January 2017 (~9 years). He also serves as Chairman of the Board. Average CEO tenure at large pharmaceutical companies runs 7–10 years, placing Ricks near the upper end of typical tenure.

**Key Departure — Anat Ashkenazi:** The former CFO, widely regarded as the strongest internal succession candidate, departed in 2024 to become CFO at Google. This was a material reduction in bench depth.

**Remaining Senior Leadership Candidates:**
- **Lucas Montarce** (EVP, President of Lilly International): Oversees all non-US markets and manages more than 60% of Lilly's global workforce. Deep international experience including European and emerging market expansion.
- **Patrik Jonsson** (EVP, President of Lilly USA): Responsible for the US — Lilly's largest market at ~67% of revenues — and played a central commercial role in the GLP-1 launch.
- **Daniel Skovronsky, M.D., Ph.D.** (EVP, Chief Scientific & Medical Officer): The scientific and strategic architect of both the Alzheimer's pipeline and GLP-1 programs. Primarily a scientist; commercial management experience is limited.

**Bench Depth Assessment: Medium-Low.** Ashkenazi's departure was a significant loss. The remaining candidates each have distinct strengths but none is a clear "default successor." Ricks's personal influence over company culture and strategy is substantial — he personally approves senior personnel across a ~138-person executive team — meaning his management style is deeply embedded rather than institutionalized. However, Lilly's 150-year history with 11 CEO transitions suggests institutional succession mechanisms exist.

**Succession Disclosure:** No formal public succession plan has been disclosed. In a 2019 Posse Foundation interview, Ricks offered a telling signal: "I'll know I did a good job because we found a successor who can do it better" ([Posse Foundation, 2019](https://www.possefoundation.org/news-and-events/ubben-posse-fellow-interviews-david-ricks)), suggesting deliberate succession thinking but no defined timeline.
