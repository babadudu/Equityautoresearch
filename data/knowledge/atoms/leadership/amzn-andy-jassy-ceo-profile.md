## Andy Jassy — Current CEO (July 2021–Present)

### Background and Education

Andrew R. Jassy was born in 1967 in Scarsdale, New York. He earned a B.A. from Harvard University in 1990 and an MBA from Harvard Business School in 1997. He joined Amazon in 1997 as a Technical Advisor (shadow) to Jeff Bezos — a role Amazon has used to cultivate future leaders — and has been at the company for nearly 28 years as of 2025.

### Creating and Leading AWS (2003–2021)

Jassy conceived of AWS in 2003 and led the division from its formal launch in 2006 through his appointment as Amazon CEO in 2021 — a 15-year tenure building it from zero to the world's largest cloud platform. He described the arc of AWS's early growth: "It took us about eight or nine years to build an AWS annual run rate of about $4 billion." ([SiliconANGLE, Dec 2024](https://siliconangle.com/2024/12/05/exclusive-amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-reveals-aws-strategy-building-enterprise-ai-platform/)) As of FY2024, AWS generates $128.7B in annual revenue.

### Key CEO-Era Decisions

1. **Cost Control and Organizational Restructuring (2022–2023)**: In response to post-pandemic cost pressures Jassy executed Amazon's largest-ever layoff (~27,000 employees) and drove regionalization of the fulfillment network to reduce logistics costs.

2. **Return-to-Office Policy (2023–2024)**: Required five-day in-office attendance, a decision that generated significant internal controversy. Jassy held firm: "When you're together that invention is stronger. People riff on top of each other's ideas better if they're together." ([HBR, May 2025](https://hbr.org/podcast/2025/05/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-on-agility-ai-strategy-and-the-changing-role-of-managers))

3. **Full Commitment to AI (2023–2025)**: Invested $4B in Anthropic, launched Trainium2 custom AI chips, introduced Amazon Bedrock (multi-model managed service) and Amazon Q (enterprise AI assistant). Positioned AI as the central growth engine of AWS.

### Leadership Philosophy: Three Pillars

Jassy's leadership philosophy is continuous with Bezos's but has its own distinct emphases. Three themes recur across his public statements.

#### 1. WhyQ — Institutionalizing Questioning

Jassy identified what he calls "WhyQ" as Amazon's most distinctive cultural trait and a key driver of its success. In a Fortune interview he defined it: "We ask why, and why not, constantly. It helps us deconstruct problems, get to root causes, understand blockers, and unlock doors that might have previously seemed impenetrable." He argued Amazon has "an unusually high quotient of this WhyQ" and that it "frames the way we think about everything that we do."

Critically, Jassy warned that this culture is fragile: it "needs to be built deeply into your culture and leadership team, and has to be fiercely protected over time if you're lucky enough to be successful." Success itself becomes a threat to the questioning instinct because it breeds complacency. ([Fortune, Apr 2025](https://fortune.com/2025/04/11/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-one-trait-guides-success-whyq-ability-to-ask-questions/))

On sustained learning as the compound of WhyQ: "The second you think there's a little left for you to learn is the second that you are unwinding as an individual and as a learning professional." ([About Amazon, 2024](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-leadership-principles-video-podcast)) "In the nearly 28 years I've been at Amazon, the biggest difference in the relative growth of companies and individuals has been their aptitude to learn." ([Fortune, Apr 2025](https://fortune.com/2025/04/11/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-one-trait-guides-success-whyq-ability-to-ask-questions/))

#### 2. Speed as a Leadership Decision

Jassy's most cited management thesis is that speed is not an output of organizational design but a deliberate leadership choice. He articulated this in an HBR long-form piece: "Speed disproportionately matters for every business, in every industry, at all times."

He rejected the common assumption that large organizations must move slowly: "The leadership team has to believe it's a priority, reinforce it constantly, organize and remove structural barriers, and build in modular ways that enable pace." He stated directly that "speed does not happen unless the entire company and culture embrace it." ([HBR, Jul 2025](https://hbr.org/2025/07/speed-is-a-leadership-decision); [About Amazon](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-speed-business))

To act on this belief, Jassy created a "bureaucracy mailbox" where employees could submit process complaints directly to him. He personally reviewed more than 1,000 submissions and drove 375 process reforms. His framing: "Builders hate bureaucracy. It slows them down, frustrates them, and keeps them from doing what they came here to do." ([HBR, Jul 2025](https://hbr.org/2025/07/speed-is-a-leadership-decision))

#### 3. Embracing Failure and Imperfect Process

Jassy's stance on failure continues and refines Bezos's: "You just can't be terrified of failure. An embarrassing amount of how successful you are is attitude." ([HBR, May 2025](https://hbr.org/podcast/2025/05/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-on-agility-ai-strategy-and-the-changing-role-of-managers))

On the invention process specifically: "When we're inventing, we accept the process being beautifully imperfect." ([Fortune, Apr 2025](https://fortune.com/2025/04/11/amazon-ceo-andy-jassy-one-trait-guides-success-whyq-ability-to-ask-questions/)) This formulation — "beautifully imperfect" — frames messiness not as a tolerated cost but as an inherent feature of innovation, a meaningful upgrade from Bezos's framing of failure as a necessary statistical byproduct of risk-taking.

### Leadership Continuity vs. Evolution

Jassy was Bezos's Technical Advisor for a year before building AWS — arguably the deepest immersion in Bezos's thinking of anyone at Amazon. The philosophical continuity is tight: customer obsession, long-term orientation, willingness to invent, and tolerance of failure all persist. The evolution is primarily in Jassy's emphasis on *speed as an organizational discipline*, the *institutionalization of questioning (WhyQ)* as a cultural protection mechanism, and a more explicit framing of learning rate as the primary predictor of individual and company growth.

From Bezos's "failures must grow" to Jassy's "beautifully imperfect process," the failure culture has been transmitted and given new language across the CEO transition.
