### The Patriarch: Morris Chang's Enduring Influence

Morris Chang (張忠謀), born in 1931 in Ningbo, China, lived through war and poverty before moving to the US in 1949 to study at Harvard, MIT, and Stanford. Reflecting on his youth, Chang noted, "The early part of my life between birth and 18, the background was war, poverty, injustice." ([來源：Synergy Startup Substack](https://synergystartup.substack.com/p/tsmc-the-untold-story-of-morris-chang)). 

After a 25-year career at Texas Instruments (TI), where he rose to group vice president and mastered the "learning curve pricing" strategy, Chang left in 1983 feeling he had been "put out to pasture." In 1987, at the behest of the Taiwanese government, he founded TSMC, creating the world's first pure-play semiconductor foundry. His vision was uncompromising: "When I founded a semiconductor company, there was only one path: to make it a world-class one." ([來源：CommonWealth Publishing, 2024-12-02](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3849)).

Chang's leadership was characterized by fierce independence, financial discipline, and a borderline-obsessive focus on integrity. In his autobiography, he revealed how he rejected an IBM partnership proposal because "IBM still consider themselves to be the senior partner... We declined without having to think about it at all." ([來源：Acquired Briefing, 2025-01](https://www.acquiredbriefing.com/p/the-morris-chang-interview)). Even when negotiating with Apple's Tim Cook and Jeff Williams for iPhone chip orders, Chang refused to cut TSMC's dividend to fund Apple's massive capacity demands, opting instead to take half the order to maintain financial discipline.

Remarkably, Chang once considered NVIDIA's Jensen Huang as a potential successor. "Although I was quite familiar with the talent within the company, there was one person outside the company who, in terms of character, vision, professional experience, and knowledge in semiconductors—across all criteria—was worth considering as a successor for TSMC... that person was Jensen Huang." Huang politely declined, stating, "I already have a job." ([來源：TechSoda, Morris Chang 自傳解析](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography)).

### The Pragmatist: C.C. Wei's Era of Global Expansion

Dr. C.C. Wei (魏哲家), born in 1953 in rural Taiwan, holds a Ph.D. in electrical engineering from Yale University. Joining TSMC in 1998, Wei is uniquely prepared for his current role. Morris Chang intentionally rotated him across technology, manufacturing, and business development, calling him "the best-prepared CEO." After sharing power with Mark Liu in a dual-leadership structure, Wei became sole Chairman and CEO in June 2024.

Wei's leadership style is unorthodox, grounded, and marked by self-deprecating humor, contrasting with Chang's patrician aura. Yet, Wei has successfully navigated TSMC's most turbulent era: the AI supercycle, brutal geopolitics, and global fab expansion. Under his tenure, TSMC's revenue grew from NT$1 trillion in 2018 to NT$3.8 trillion in 2025. 

Wei has spearheaded TSMC's $165 billion US expansion in Arizona, the JASM venture in Japan, and the ESMC fab in Germany. He views this not just as geographic diversification, but as a corporate transformation, stating his goal is "Turning Taiwan's TSMC into the World's TSMC." ([來源：CommonWealth Magazine, 2025-07-10](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=4215)). 

Wei is resolutely unfazed by competitors. When asked about Intel Foundry's massive spending to catch up, Wei replied with cool confidence: "In the development of semiconductor technology to this point, receiving investment has not helped to improve competitiveness, right?" When asked if he was worried about Intel, he simply answered, "Not worried." ([來源：WCCFTech, 2026-01](https://wccftech.com/tsmcs-ceo-says-intel-foundry-wont-be-competitive-by-just-throwing-money/)). However, he admits the sheer scale of the AI boom is daunting. Facing a $52-$56 billion CapEx budget for 2026, Wei confessed, "I'm also very nervous about it. You bet, because we have to invest about $52 billion to $56 billion... If we didn't do it carefully, that would be a big disaster to TSMC." To mitigate this, Wei personally interviewed the CEOs of all major cloud service providers to verify AI demand, concluding, "they are very rich" and the demand is real. ([來源：The Next Platform, 2026-01-16](https://www.nextplatform.com/compute/2026/01/16/tsmc-has-no-choice-but-to-trust-the-sunny-ai-forecasts-of-its-customers/4092173)).

### The Bench: Co-COO Structure and Succession Planning

As Wei enters his 70s, succession planning has become a critical focus for investors. In March 2024, TSMC revived a proven playbook by appointing two Executive Vice Presidents as Co-Chief Operating Officers (Co-COOs), mirroring the exact mechanism Morris Chang used to groom Wei and Mark Liu in 2012.

1.  **Dr. Y.J. Mii (米玉傑) - The Technologist**: Handling the R&D organization, Mii has been with TSMC for 31 years. He spearheaded the development of every advanced node from 16nm down to 3nm and the upcoming 2nm. He ensures TSMC maintains its process technology lead over Intel and Samsung.
2.  **Y.P. Chyn (秦永沛) - The Operator**: Overseeing all fab operations globally, Chyn is an original founding member of TSMC, boasting a 39-year tenure. He built TSMC's "Golden Spice Modeling" and Design for Manufacturing programs, and is the architect of the company's legendary yield curve ramps.

This Co-COO structure perfectly covers TSMC's two pillars: innovation (Mii) and execution (Chyn). Supported by SVPs like Dr. Kevin Zhang (Business Development) and Lora Ho (Corporate Strategy/ESG), the bench depth is rated "Medium-High." However, the recent retirements of several senior veterans in 2025 mark an accelerating generational shift that will test TSMC's ability to maintain its intense operational culture on a global scale.
