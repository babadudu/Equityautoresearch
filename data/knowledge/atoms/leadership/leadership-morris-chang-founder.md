### From War-Torn Youth to Semiconductor Pioneer

Morris Chang (張忠謀), born in 1931 in Ningbo, China, spent his early years navigating "war, poverty, injustice" before moving to the United States in 1949. He attended Harvard University for a year before transferring to MIT, ultimately earning a Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering from Stanford. His career in semiconductors began at Sylvania in 1955, but his formative professional era was a 25-year tenure at Texas Instruments (TI), where he rose to Group Vice President of global semiconductor operations. 

At TI, Chang mastered the economics of chip manufacturing, particularly the concept of "learning curve pricing"—the rule that cumulative production volume exponentially drives down unit costs. After feeling "put out to pasture" at TI in 1983, and following a brief stint at General Instrument, Chang relocated to Taiwan in 1985 to head the Industrial Technology Research Institute (ITRI). In 1987, at the age of 56, he founded Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC).

### The Foundry Vision and The 8% R&D Rule

Chang's founding premise was revolutionary: "I wanted to establish a company that would be the foundry for the world's semiconductor industry. At that time, there was no such company" ([CommonWealth Publishing, 2024](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3849)). 

To ensure TSMC never lost its technical edge, Chang instituted an unshakeable financial discipline: fixing R&D spending at 8% of revenue, regardless of macroeconomic cycles. Speaking on the *Acquired* podcast, Chang explained the rationale: "I wanted to make him [the head of R&D] at ease... He doesn't have to argue, he doesn't have to request every year." This relentless commitment allowed TSMC to capitalize on critical technological inflections, such as the 28nm node in 2011, which Chang described as the "sweet spot" that secured TSMC's dominance during the smartphone boom.

### Forging Legendary Partnerships: NVIDIA and Apple

Chang's ability to identify and nurture vital customer relationships defined TSMC's trajectory. In 1997, he received a letter from Jensen Huang, then the CEO of a small startup called NVIDIA. Chang's philosophy was that TSMC "should never be negligent in talking to future customers, even if the customer seems very small." TSMC not only accepted NVIDIA's orders but deployed engineers to assist them. The bond grew so profound that Chang later considered offering Jensen Huang the CEO position at TSMC prior to Chang's retirement, though Huang respectfully declined, stating "I already have a job" ([TechSoda, 2024](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography)).

Similarly, Chang personally navigated the onboarding of Apple. In 2010, Foxconn's Terry Gou introduced Chang to Apple COO Jeff Williams. By 2011, Chang was meeting with Tim Cook, who assured him not to worry about Intel competing for Apple's business, implying Intel lacked the DNA for dedicated foundry service. During capacity negotiations with Apple, Chang exhibited immense discipline, deciding to "take half of what Apple asked for" to avoid cannibalizing capacity for other clients and steadfastly refusing to cut TSMC's dividend to fund the expansion ([Acquired Briefing, 2025](https://www.acquiredbriefing.com/p/the-morris-chang-interview)).

### Integrity and the Geopolitical Realist

Chang established "Integrity" as TSMC's non-negotiable core value, once stating his goal was to make TSMC "the pure land of maintaining the core value of the society." He noted that any executive violating this principle would be immediately dismissed.

In his later years, Chang has emerged as a clear-eyed geopolitical realist. In a 2022 Brookings Institution speech, he famously declared, "Globalization is almost dead. Free trade is almost dead." While acknowledging his American identity, he bluntly criticized the US push to reshore semiconductor manufacturing as "a very expensive exercise in futility," pointing out that Taiwan's centralized, deeply integrated engineering ecosystem cannot be easily replicated by short-term subsidies. His foresight has proven highly prescient as TSMC navigates the complex realities of the US-China tech war and its own turbulent overseas expansion.
