### The Evaporation of TSMC's China Revenue

In 2020, mainland China represented 22% of TSMC's total revenue, serving as a vital growth engine driven by clients like Huawei's HiSilicon. By the end of 2024, following relentless U.S. export controls and the entity-listing of Chinese tech champions, that figure plummeted to just **9%**.

The U.S. Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) tightened the noose in late 2024, explicitly notifying TSMC to halt the shipment of 7nm and below AI/HPC chips to Chinese clients after TSMC silicon was found embedded in Huawei AI processors. Consequently, the remaining Chinese revenue (roughly $9.7B) is heavily skewed toward trailing-edge mature nodes (16nm and above) manufactured at TSMC's Fab 16 in Nanjing. 

While CEO C.C. Wei dismissed the financial impact, stating, "if the China market is not available, but I still think the AI's growth will be very dramatically," the strategic implications are profound. TSMC is structurally barred from serving the world's second-largest economy with its highest-margin products ([來源：TSMC Q3 2025 Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/10/17/tsmc-tsm-q3-2025-earnings-call-transcript/)).

### SMIC's State-Sponsored Catch-Up

Nature abhors a vacuum. Forced out of TSMC's foundries, Chinese domestic demand has funneled billions into Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation (SMIC). Despite being cut off from ASML's EUV lithography machines, SMIC has advanced faster than Western analysts predicted.

In December 2025, TechInsights confirmed that Huawei's Mate 80 Pro (powered by the Kirin 9030 SoC) was manufactured on SMIC's **N+3 (5nm-class) process**. SMIC achieved this using brute-force Deep Ultraviolet (DUV) multi-patterning. While this workaround results in abysmal yields (estimated at 30-40%) and manufacturing costs 50% higher than TSMC's N5, it proves China possesses sovereign 5nm capability ([來源：TechInsights, 2025-12](https://www.techinsights.com/blog/smic-n3-confirmed-kirin-9030-analysis-reveals-how-close-smic-5nm)). 

SMIC's capacity is also scaling. The foundry aims to double its 7nm capacity to roughly 60,000 to 80,000 wafers per month by 2026. This allows Huawei to produce the Ascend 910C and 920 AI accelerators, which offer roughly 60% of the performance of an NVIDIA H100, providing a "good enough" alternative for the sanctioned Chinese tech sector.

### The Structural Limits of China's Advancement

Despite this progress, the threat to TSMC's global dominance remains constrained. SMIC's reliance on DUV multi-patterning is a financial black hole. SMIC's gross margins dropped to a dismal 19.2% in late 2025 due to the severe cost drag of its 7nm/5nm yields. By contrast, TSMC operates at over 60% gross margins. 

Furthermore, without domestic EUV capability, scaling below 5nm becomes physically and economically impossible. Reports of Chinese breakthroughs in Laser-Produced Plasma (LDP) EUV prototypes indicate power outputs of only 50-100W, far short of the 250W+ required for commercial volume manufacturing. Transitioning a prototype to a commercial EUV machine took ASML two decades.

### Reverse CFIUS and the Parallel Ecosystem

The real risk to TSMC is not that SMIC steals Apple or NVIDIA's business in the West. The risk is the permanent bifurcation of the tech ecosystem. Under the U.S. "Reverse CFIUS" (Outbound Investment Screening Rule) activated in January 2025, U.S. capital is restricted from funding Chinese semiconductor advancement. Combined with CHIPS Act restrictions forbidding TSMC from expanding advanced fabs in China, the die is cast.

China's 15th Five-Year Plan (2026-2030) allocates $70 billion to build a completely parallel, self-sufficient semiconductor supply chain. While TSMC remains the undisputed king of the global market, it has permanently lost the ability to monetize the Chinese market's bleeding-edge growth.
