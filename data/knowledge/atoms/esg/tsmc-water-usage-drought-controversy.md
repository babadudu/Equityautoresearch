## TSMC Water Consumption, Recycling Systems & 2021 Taiwan Drought Controversy

Semiconductor manufacturing demands ultra-pure water (UPW) in extreme volumes — each wafer requires thousands of liters for cleaning, etching, and chemical processes. As the world's largest contract chipmaker, TSMC's water usage is a persistent governance and operational risk issue in Taiwan.

### Fab 20 Wastewater Innovation

In 2024, TSMC deployed an "ammonia-nitrogen wastewater diversion system" at Fab 20 Phase 1 (5nm/3nm), achieving a **40% reduction in effluent conductivity** and **30% reduction in chemical usage**. TSMC also developed PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) adsorption filtration technology achieving an average **95% PFAS removal rate** — an emerging environmental regulatory focus for the semiconductor industry. ([Source: Taiwan Insight, 2025-11](https://taiwaninsight.org/2025/11/05/water-nexus-can-semiconductors-and-sustainability-coexist-in-taiwan/); [TSMC ESG — Water Stewardship](https://esg.tsmc.com/file/public/e-APractitionerofGreenPower_2.pdf))

### 2021 Taiwan Drought — "Chips Before Crops" Controversy

**Background**: In 2021, Taiwan experienced its **worst drought in 56 years** — rainfall from June 2020 to May 2021 was only one-third of the annual average, with no typhoon landfall for the first time since the 1950s. Although Taiwan's average rainfall is 2.6× the global average, reservoir capacity is limited and seasonal distribution is extremely uneven. Tsengwen Reservoir (one of Taiwan's largest) fell to **11% capacity**, and the primary reservoir supplying Hsinchu Science Park reached historic lows. ([Source: Fortune, 2021-06-12](https://fortune.com/2021/06/12/chip-shortage-taiwan-drought-tsmc-water-usage/); [NPR, 2023-04-19](https://www.npr.org/sections/goatsandsoda/2023/04/19/1170425349/epic-drought-in-taiwan-pits-farmers-against-high-tech-factories-for-water))

**Government Priority Decision & Agricultural Impact**: According to NPR (2023), the Taiwan government effectively prioritized semiconductor manufacturing over agriculture during the drought — southern farmers were **banned from growing rice for three consecutive years**, with the government offering subsidies in exchange for farmers leaving fields fallow to preserve water for chip factories. Water resources management expert Gene You criticized the government's stance as essentially: "what you need we will give you, because the economy needs you" — prioritizing chip industry water supply on economic grounds rather than balanced resource management. ([Source: NPR, 2023-04-19](https://www.npr.org/sections/goatsandsoda/2023/04/19/1170425349/epic-drought-in-taiwan-pits-farmers-against-high-tech-factories-for-water))

According to The Diplomat (2024), the most severely affected southern areas implemented two-day-per-week water rationing during the drought, with nighttime residential water pressure reduced. Farmer Yang Kuan-wei stated that insufficient rainfall increased pest pressure, requiring more pesticide use that then killed pollinating bees, creating an ecological vicious cycle. Farmers' association leader Chang Mei-hsueh noted that rice cultivation "locks soil moisture and stabilizes ground temperature," and that forced fallow actually exacerbated land degradation effects of the drought. Farmer Yang further stated: "A country needs to rely on its own food supply. It is a form of security" — equating food security with chip security. ([Source: NPR, 2023-04-19](https://www.npr.org/sections/goatsandsoda/2023/04/19/1170425349/epic-drought-in-taiwan-pits-farmers-against-high-tech-factories-for-water); [The Diplomat, 2024-09](https://thediplomat.com/2024/09/how-water-scarcity-threatens-taiwans-semiconductor-industry/))

**TSMC's Response**: During the 2021 drought, TSMC deployed **water tanker trucks from wetter southern regions** to northern fab sites to maintain production. TSMC's official statement said "there is no impact on production, and we are monitoring the water supply situation closely." However, according to Fortune (2021), TSMC's daily water consumption at that time was approximately **20,000 tonnes** (equivalent to the daily water needs of a city of 58,000 people), and municipal water supply to TSMC had already increased **71% between 2015 and 2019** — reflecting rapid rising demand from capacity expansion. After the drought, TSMC began constructing its own water recycling plants in 2021 and committed to reducing water consumption by 10%. ([Source: Fortune, 2021-06-12](https://fortune.com/2021/06/12/chip-shortage-taiwan-drought-tsmc-water-usage/))

**Credit Risk Framing**: S&P Global Ratings (2024) positioned TSMC's water risk as a credit risk factor: "climate change is becoming a credit risk," noting that Taiwan's water resource vulnerability could affect TSMC's long-term operational stability and credit assessment. ([Source: S&P Global Ratings, 2024-02-26](https://www.spglobal.com/ratings/en/regulatory/article/240226-sustainability-insights-tsmc-and-water-a-case-study-of-how-climate-is-becoming-a-credit-risk-factor-s12992283))

### Forward-Looking Water Risk

According to PMC/Nature (2024), a study using TSMC as a case study for climate-induced water stress on semiconductor supply chains found that under the RCP 8.5 scenario, Taiwan's western plains (where TSMC's main fabs are located) will see rainfall variability increase 15–25% before 2050, with drought frequency potentially rising from 2 occurrences per decade to 3–4 per decade. ([Source: PMC — Climate change induced water stress and semiconductor supply chain risk, 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10826299/))
