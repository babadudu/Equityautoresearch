### The Thirst of Ultra-Pure Water

Manufacturing advanced semiconductors requires staggering volumes of Ultra-Pure Water (UPW) to rinse wafers between chemical treatments and photolithography steps. As TSMC scales its 5nm and 3nm production, its water demands have entered exponential territory. In 2021, TSMC's daily water usage was approximately 20,000 tons. By 2024, driven by advanced node capacity expansions, that figure skyrocketed 650% to roughly 150,000 tons per day—equivalent to the daily water needs of a city of 435,000 people. Annual consumption is now estimated at nearly 55 million tons.

### The 2021 Drought: A Case Study in Resource Allocation

TSMC's water vulnerability was starkly exposed in 2021 when Taiwan suffered its worst drought in 56 years, triggered by the absence of seasonal typhoons. With key reservoirs dropping below 15% capacity, the Taiwanese government intervened. Recognizing the systemic economic importance of the semiconductor industry (which accounts for ~15-18% of GDP), the state prioritized industrial water supply over agriculture.

Farmers across southern Taiwan were paid subsidies and explicitly forbidden from planting rice crops for three consecutive years to ensure TSMC and its peers did not have to halt fab operations. TSMC was reduced to deploying fleets of water tanker trucks to ferry water from wetter regions to its northern fabs. While production was not severely impacted, the social friction was immense. Water management experts criticized the government's stance as effectively saying, "what you need we will give you, because the economy needs you" ([NPR, 2023](https://www.npr.org/sections/goatsandsoda/2023/04/19/1170425349/epic-drought-in-taiwan-pits-farmers-against-high-tech-factories-for-water)).

### Credit Risk and Climate Modeling

The 2021 crisis fundamentally changed how financial institutions view semiconductor water usage. S&P Global Ratings took the unprecedented step of categorizing TSMC's water risk as a formal "credit risk factor." Climate models projecting RCP 8.5 scenarios indicate that by 2050, the frequency of severe droughts in Taiwan's western plains could double, shifting from twice a decade to three or four times per decade, alongside a 15-25% increase in rainfall variability.

### Mitigation, Recycling, and Global Controversies

TSMC is aggressively engineering its way out of this physical constraint. In 2024, the company achieved a 90.3% process water recycling rate, recovering over 140 million metric tons of wastewater. Facilities like Fab 20 have deployed advanced systems, such as ammonia-nitrogen diversion, reducing chemical usage by 30%. TSMC aims to reach a 95% recycling rate by 2030 and operate 100% circular water systems by 2040.

However, water controversies have followed TSMC's global expansion. In Kumamoto, Japan, where TSMC opened its JASM fab in 2024, the facility extracts roughly 7,500 tons of groundwater daily. Kumamoto relies heavily on groundwater for agriculture and residential use, sparking significant anxiety among local rice farmers. 

Additionally, TSMC's use of Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances (PFAS)—"forever chemicals" vital to chip manufacturing—triggered local government water testing at the Kumamoto outfalls. While TSMC successfully deployed adsorption filtration technology hitting a 95% PFAS removal rate, keeping emission levels below international safety standards, the incident highlights the ongoing friction between hyper-scale industrial expansion and local ecological security.
