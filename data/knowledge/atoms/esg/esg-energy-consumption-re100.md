### The Voracious Appetite of Advanced Nodes

TSMC's position as the manufacturer of the world's most advanced silicon comes with an immense, escalating energetic cost. In 2023, TSMC consumed 24.78 Terawatt-hours (TWh) of electricity, accounting for a massive 8.9% of Taiwan's total national energy consumption and over 41% of its industrial power use. 

The physics of process shrinkage dictate that energy intensity increases with every new node. TSMC's upcoming 2nm (N2) and A16 nodes consume 20-30% more power per wafer than the 5nm generation. This is driven by the extensive use of ASML’s EUV machines (each requiring ~1 Megawatt of power to operate) and the increased etching/deposition steps required for Gate-All-Around (GAA) architectures. Greenpeace estimates that by 2030, TSMC's electricity demand will surge by 267% from 2021 levels, consuming roughly 53 TWh—equivalent to 24% of Taiwan's total electricity output, or the power needs of 5.8 million citizens.

### Taiwan's Grid Bottleneck and Scope 2 Emissions

TSMC's primary sustainability hurdle is not a lack of capital, but the physical constraints of the host grid. Taiwan imports 97% of its energy, and its grid remains highly carbon-intensive (over 80% fossil fuels, predominantly coal and LNG), exacerbated by the government's mandate to decommission all nuclear power by 2025.

Consequently, TSMC's absolute greenhouse gas emissions are climbing. In 2024, total GHG emissions reached 22.7 million tons of CO₂ equivalent (an increase of ~9.9% YoY). Scope 2 emissions (indirect emissions from purchased electricity) account for 48.2% of this footprint. TSMC committed to the SBTi (Science Based Targets initiative) pathway, aiming for emissions to peak in 2025. However, with the rapid ramp-up of the highly energy-intensive N3 and N2 nodes, absolute emissions are highly likely to keep rising, making the 2025 peak target effectively unreachable without massive, immediate grid decarbonization.

### The RE100 Feasibility Gap

TSMC has pledged to reach 100% renewable energy (RE100) by 2040, with an interim target of 60% by 2030 (RE60). As of 2024, renewables constituted only 13-14% of the company's energy mix. 

The math for the 2030 target presents a structural impossibility based on physical infrastructure. TSMC will need approximately 32 TWh of renewable energy to hit its 60% mandate. However, in 2024, the *entire island of Taiwan* generated only 30.02 TWh of renewable energy. TSMC is already the largest corporate green power buyer in Taiwan, famously signing a 20-year Corporate Power Purchase Agreement (CPPA) with Ørsted to buy the entire 920 MW output of the Greater Changhua offshore wind farm. 

### The T-REC Monopoly and ESG Scrutiny

To bridge the gap between actual green electrons and accounting mandates, TSMC aggressively purchases Renewable Energy Certificates. In 2024, TSMC purchased approximately 99% of all available Taiwan Renewable Energy Certificates (T-RECs). Environmental groups, including the Citizen of the Earth Taiwan (CET), have heavily criticized this, arguing that TSMC’s monopoly on certificates crowds out small and medium enterprises from meeting their own climate goals.

If Taiwan’s offshore wind (Round 3) and solar buildouts continue to experience delays due to permitting and supply chain bottlenecks, TSMC will be forced to rely heavily on International RECs (I-RECs)—a practice criticized by Greenpeace as an accounting trick rather than actual decarbonization. While TSMC boasts an MSCI AAA ESG rating (reflecting excellent governance and disclosure), the physical reality of powering the AI supercycle on a fossil-fuel-dependent island remains the company's deepest sustainability vulnerability.
