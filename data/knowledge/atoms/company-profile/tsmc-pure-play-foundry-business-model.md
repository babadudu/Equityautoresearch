## TSMC Pure-Play Foundry: Business Model DNA

TSMC's business model is a "technology-as-a-service" platform. The company does not design chips; instead, it provides complete manufacturing services to semiconductor design companies worldwide — from process development, mask fabrication, and wafer manufacturing to advanced packaging. As of 2024, TSMC serves approximately 465 customers, manufactures over 9,920 product types, and covers all application domains from AI accelerators to automotive chips.

### The Founding Vision

Morris Chang founded TSMC in 1987 and invented the pure-play foundry model — a company that manufactures chips exclusively for others and never competes with its customers. Chang described his ambition at inception: "When I founded a semiconductor company, there was only one path: to make it a world-class one." ([Source: CommonWealth Publishing, 2024-12-02](https://english.cw.com.tw/article/article.action?id=3849))

His most fundamental insight was about the identity of the customer: "The biggest discovery was who the customers were." — TSMC's destiny is entirely bound to its customers' success. ([Source: Interconnected Blog — Morris Chang's Last Speech, 2023-04](https://interconnected.blog/morris-changs-last-speech/))

### Pricing Philosophy: Value-Based, Not Cost-Plus

TSMC's pricing is anchored to the value delivered to customers, not cost-plus arithmetic. Chang articulated this principle: "What TSMC is good at is making reasonable profits out of the price that is acceptable to the customer." He further clarified the foundry service philosophy: "Unwilling to take the price that customers can afford means the same that they were not good at doing dedicated foundry service." ([Source: TechSoda, Morris Chang autobiography analysis](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/tsmc-founder-morris-changs-autobiography))

CEO C.C. Wei extended this philosophy explicitly: "We price our technology based on the value we bring to customers, not just cost-plus." This strategy enables TSMC to sustain 55%+ gross margins on advanced nodes. ([Source: TSMC Q4 2024 Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/01/16/taiwan-semiconductor-manufacturing-tsm-q4-2024-ear/))

For overseas fabs (Arizona, Japan), Wei was unambiguous about the premium positioning: "Do we charge a little bit higher? Yes, we did because we have a value of geographics of flexibility, right? And you guys know Made in U.S.A is a premium product." — Customers not only accept the premium but actively request fabs in their home countries. ([Source: TSMC Q4 2024 Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/01/16/taiwan-semiconductor-manufacturing-tsm-q4-2024-ear/))

### The Six-Factor Profitability Framework

CFO Wendell Huang articulated a structured framework for understanding TSMC's gross margin drivers: "There are six factors affecting the profitability. Every year, different factors play different roles." The six factors are: (1) process mix, (2) capacity utilization, (3) foreign exchange, (4) pricing, (5) overseas fab costs, and (6) depreciation. ([Source: TSMC Q4 2024 Transcript](https://investor.tsmc.com/chinese/encrypt/files/encrypt_file/reports/2025-01/84aeb15bbe33894365d33f52e027c5268ba95dcf/TSMC%204Q24%20Transcript.pdf))

Notably, Huang emphasized that pricing is only one lever: "Pricing is only one element of our broader profitability... the more decisive driver of profitability is a high utilization rate supported by strong demand and disciplined capacity planning." ([Source: TrendForce, 2026-01-15](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2026/01/15/news-tsmc-addresses-speculations-of-a-20-wafer-asp-increase-says-price-is-not-the-main-profit-driver/))

On structural gross margin floor, Huang stated: "Thus, even with the unfavorable foreign exchange rate, we believe a long-term gross margin of 53% and higher remains well achievable." — 53% is the structural floor; the current 62%+ reflects the AI supercycle premium. ([Source: TSMC Q2 2025 Transcript](https://investor.tsmc.com/chinese/encrypt/files/encrypt_file/reports/2025-07/1f4f86c935f1de837672a6973154e64b26bdae57/TSMC%202Q25%20Transcript.pdf))

### The 8% R&D Discipline

Morris Chang institutionalized a fixed ~8% of revenue as R&D spending, a discipline that has held across economic cycles. He explained the intent: "I wanted to make him at ease, you know what I mean? He doesn't have to argue, he doesn't have to request every year." — By fixing the ratio, TSMC prevents R&D from being cut during downturns, ensuring generational technology leadership. ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

### Customer Success as the Measure of TSMC's Success

CEO Wei articulated an unusual success metric in a 2025 Fortune interview: "It's a great encouragement to go up on the Fortune Global 500, but the best sign of our success is if our customers are on the list—or even higher than TSMC." — The deeper customers succeed, the deeper TSMC's moat. ([Source: Fortune Asia, 2025-07-30](https://fortune.com/asia/2025/07/30/tsmc-ai-boom-cc-wei-wendell-huang-global-500/))

This philosophy is structurally reinforced: TSMC's top 10 customers account for 76% of revenue (2024), up from 70% in 2023 and 68% in 2022. Apple ~22%, NVIDIA <12%, AMD <10%. This deep interdependency creates multi-decade trust relationships with the world's most successful fabless chip companies. ([Source: TechSoda](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/explainer-tsmcs-2024-annual-report))

When capacity is constrained, the dynamic inverts: customers proactively prepay cash to secure production slots. In 2024, TSMC received NT$291.1 billion in customer prepayments for future fab capacity, demonstrating the extreme demand for TSMC's manufacturing services. Wei confirmed: "We have a very tight capacity and cannot even meet customers' need." ([Source: TSMC Q4 2024 Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/01/16/taiwan-semiconductor-manufacturing-tsm-q4-2024-ear/))
