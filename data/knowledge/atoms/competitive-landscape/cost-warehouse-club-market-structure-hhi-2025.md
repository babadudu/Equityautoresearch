## Warehouse Club Market Structure: High-Concentration Duopoly

The U.S. warehouse club market is effectively a duopoly, with Costco and Sam's Club (Walmart subsidiary) together commanding over 85% market share. BJ's Wholesale is a distant third, operating primarily in the eastern United States. This level of concentration is extremely rare in retail — most retail subcategories (department stores, supermarkets) have at least 5–6 major players — but the high capital requirements and scale economies of warehouse clubs make new entrants virtually impossible.

## HHI Quantitative Analysis

The U.S. Department of Justice (DOJ) and Federal Trade Commission (FTC) use the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) as a standard measure of market concentration. HHI is computed as the sum of squared market share percentages: HHI < 1,500 = "unconcentrated"; 1,500–2,500 = "moderately concentrated"; > 2,500 = "highly concentrated" ([DOJ/FTC Horizontal Merger Guidelines](https://www.justice.gov/atr/herfindahl-hirschman-index)).

Using 2024 global warehouse club sales data (Costco $275B / $431B = 63.8%; Sam's Club ~$90B = 20.9%; BJ's $20B = 4.6%; PriceSmart $4.5B = 1.0%; other 9.6%):

| Sam's Club | 20.9% | 437 |

| PriceSmart | 1.0% | 1 |
| Other | 9.6% | 92 |
| **HHI Total** | | **4,621** |

**HHI = 4,621** — nearly double the DOJ "highly concentrated" threshold of 2,500. This figure is among the highest in any major U.S. retail subcategory (compare: U.S. supermarkets HHI ~800–1,200; department stores HHI ~600–900), surpassed only by certain utilities or defense subcategories. Calculated for the U.S. market alone (Costco ~60%, Sam's Club ~25%, BJ's ~6%), the HHI remains approximately **~4,261** (60² + 25² + 6² + 9² = 3,600 + 625 + 36 + 81).

## Structural Causes of Extreme Concentration

This extreme concentration is **structural**: the warehouse club model simultaneously requires (1) ultra-large purchasing volumes to compress supplier prices, (2) ultra-large store footprints (14–20 acres) to enable warehouse-style display, and (3) a member base at critical mass to cover fixed costs. These three requirements create a natural monopoly tendency — the market capacity cannot support a fourth national participant.

Noteworthy: Costco's "pass-through" pricing strategy (gross margin cap of 14–15%) means its high HHI does not constitute antitrust risk. FTC scrutiny of predatory pricing focuses on "whether consumers are harmed" — Costco's low prices are a direct benefit to consumers, not a harm.

## Why This HHI Is Self-Reinforcing

The duopoly is not merely a snapshot; it is self-reinforcing across three mechanisms:

1. **Purchasing flywheel**: More members → larger purchase volumes → lower unit costs → lower prices → more members. Costco's $240B+ annual purchasing volume gives it unit cost advantages estimated at 10–15% below any would-be entrant.

2. **Fixed-cost absorption**: The warehouse model has extremely high fixed costs (land, building, staffing, infrastructure). Once fixed costs are absorbed across a large member base, incremental membership is nearly pure profit — a structural advantage that grows with scale.

3. **Regulatory inoculation**: Because Costco's market dominance is expressed through lower consumer prices rather than higher margins, it is structurally resistant to antitrust intervention — precisely the opposite of typical monopoly risk.
