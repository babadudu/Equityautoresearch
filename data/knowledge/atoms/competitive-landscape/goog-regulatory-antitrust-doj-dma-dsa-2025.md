## Google Regulatory & Antitrust Gauntlet: DOJ, EU DMA, DSA, and Global Scrutiny (2025)

Alphabet faces the most complex multi-jurisdictional regulatory environment of any technology company. The following documents each active legal proceeding, regulatory framework, and enforcement action with specific statutes and rulings.

---

### 1. U.S. DOJ Antitrust — Search Monopoly Case (Case #1)

**Filing:** October 2020. DOJ sued Google for maintaining an illegal monopoly in search, with the primary allegation that Google paid Apple approximately **$20 billion per year** in exclusive default search placement agreements — locking distribution and foreclosing rivals.

**Ruling:** August 2024. Federal Judge Amit P. Mehta ruled Google had indeed maintained an illegal search monopoly.

**Final Remedy (September 2025):** The court **did not require breakup** of Android or Chrome. Instead, behavioral remedies were imposed:
- Google is prohibited from entering or maintaining exclusive distribution contracts for its search engine.
- Google must open certain search and user data to "qualified competitors."

Significance: The behavioral remedy preserves Google's structural integrity but restricts its most effective distribution lever. The $20B annual Apple TAC payment may be eliminated or restructured — simultaneously reducing a major cost (2024 total TAC: $51 billion, of which Apple represented the largest share) while potentially reducing search traffic volume ([Financial Content / WRAL, Sep 2025](https://markets.financialcontent.com/wral/article/marketminute-2025-9-18-alphabet-navigates-regulatory-gauntlet-antitrust-ruling-spares-breakup-eu-slaps-hefty-ad-tech-fine)).

### 2. U.S. DOJ Antitrust — Ad Tech Case (Case #2)

**Filing:** January 2023. The DOJ filed a second antitrust case targeting Google's advertising technology stack, alleging Google simultaneously controls:
- **Buy-side:** DV360 (advertiser demand-side platform)
- **Sell-side:** Ad Manager (publisher supply-side platform)
- **Exchange:** AdX (Google Ad Exchange)

The DOJ's analogy: this is equivalent to "simultaneously owning the New York Stock Exchange, Goldman Sachs, and Merrill Lynch" — a structural conflict of interest in every programmatic auction.

**Status:** Entered trial phase in late 2024. If Google loses, it may be required to divest ad tech assets such as Ad Manager or AdX.

**Revenue at Risk:** Google Network revenue (~$31.5 billion/year) is the most directly exposed segment. Core search advertising would be less affected, as it does not rely on the programmatic ad stack.

### 3. EU Digital Markets Act (DMA)

**Passage:** November 2022. **Effective:** May 2023.

**Gatekeeper Designation (September 6, 2023):** The European Commission designated Alphabet as a gatekeeper across **eight core platform services**:
1. Google Search
2. YouTube
3. Android
4. Chrome
5. Google Maps
6. Google Play
7. Google Shopping
8. Alphabet's online advertising services
([EC Gatekeepers Portal](https://digital-markets-act.ec.europa.eu/gatekeepers-portal_en))

**Key Obligations by Article:**

- **Article 5(2):** Prohibits combining personal data across different core platform services for targeted advertising without explicit user consent (GDPR standard).
- **Article 5(4):** Prohibits preventing business users from steering users to transactions outside the gatekeeper's platform.
- **Article 5(9):** Requires advertising services to provide advertisers and publishers with transparent per-transaction pricing and performance data. This directly challenges Google's historically opaque "black box" ad auction pricing.
- **Article 6(5):** Prohibits self-preferencing — giving Google's own services preferential rankings in search results. **Enforcement Action (March 2025):** The EC formally notified Alphabet that Google Shopping, hotel bookings, transportation, financial products, and sports search results violate Article 6(5), constituting unfair ranking of competing services ([TechPolicy.Press, Mar 2025](https://www.techpolicy.press/digital-markets-act-roundup-march-2025/)).
- **Article 6(9):** Requires gatekeepers to facilitate effective data portability to reduce user dependency on a single platform.

**DMA Penalty Structure:**
- First violation: up to 10% of global annual revenue (Alphabet 2024 revenue: $350 billion → maximum fine: **$35 billion**)
- Repeat violations: up to 20% of global annual revenue
- Systematic infringement: potential structural remedies including divestiture

### 5. EU Digital Services Act (DSA)

**Full Implementation:** February 2024.

Google Search, YouTube, and Google Maps are designated as **Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs)** under the DSA. Key obligations include:
- Stricter content moderation requirements
- Mandatory transparency reporting
- Algorithmic recommendation disclosures
- Enhanced protections for minors

**Penalty Structure:** Up to 6% of global annual revenue for violations.

In December 2024, the EC launched a new investigation into Google's AI practices, focusing on whether Gemini model integration with Search constitutes a new form of self-preferencing.

---

### 6. Global Regulatory Trend

Multiple jurisdictions are independently strengthening digital platform regulation:

- **Japan:** Transparency Act (effective 2021) requiring disclosure of platform algorithms and terms
- **South Korea:** Telecommunications Business Act revision (2021) restricting app store payment policies
- **India:** Competition Commission of India (2022) ruling against Android monopoly — fined ₹1.34 billion rupees
- **Australia:** News Media and Digital Platforms Mandatory Bargaining Code (2021) requiring payment negotiations with news publishers
