## TSMC Bear Case: Geopolitical Tail Risk, Overseas Dilution, and Long-Cycle Vulnerabilities

### Bear #1: Taiwan Strait Geopolitical Risk Is an Unhedgeable Tail Risk

The Council on Foreign Relations has classified a Taiwan Strait crisis as a Tier I risk for 2026 — the highest severity category — with the probability characterized as an "even chance." In a blockade scenario, global GDP losses are estimated at approximately $5 trillion, and TSMC's own revenue loss is estimated at 60–80% of total revenues. Taiwan imports 97% of its energy; under a naval blockade, energy reserves would be exhausted within approximately two weeks.

At least one consulting firm has estimated the five-year probability of a blockade at 60%. Even if near-term invasion probability is assessed as low, the presence of a non-zero Tier I tail risk compresses the price-to-earnings multiple that rational investors are willing to pay for TSMC equity. The geopolitical risk discount is structural and not eliminable through operational actions. ([Council on Foreign Relations — Conflicts to Watch 2026](https://www.cfr.org/report/conflicts-watch-2026); [Rhodium Group](https://rhg.com/research/taiwan-economic-disruptions/))

### Bear #2: Overseas Fab Costs Will Structurally Dilute Profitability Over the Long Term

U.S. fabrication costs are estimated at 30–50% higher than equivalent production in Taiwan. The key drivers — smaller scale, immature supplier ecosystem, higher domestic labor and construction costs — are structural disadvantages that cannot be resolved in the short to medium term. TSMC's $165 billion U.S. investment commitment represents the largest capital allocation in the company's history.

Management has acknowledged that early-phase overseas fabs dilute gross margin by 2–3 percentage points, with later-phase impact estimated at 3–4 percentage points. The Arizona fab experienced reported cultural friction and construction delays during its initial ramp. CFO Huang acknowledged the cost gap directly: "The U.S. fab cost, it is more expensive in the U.S., mainly because of several reasons" — citing three structural disadvantages (scale, supply chain, ecosystem) that the company cannot resolve quickly. ([TSMC Q4 2024 Earnings Transcript](https://www.fool.com/earnings/call-transcripts/2025/01/16/taiwan-semiconductor-manufacturing-tsm-q4-2024-ear/))

The $6.6 billion in CHIPS Act grants represents only a 4% recovery rate on the $165 billion total investment, meaning the subsidy offsets only a small fraction of the incremental cost burden.

### Bear #3: Customer Concentration Continues to Rise — Key Customer Risk Is Elevated

The top 10 customers accounted for approximately 68% of TSMC revenue in 2022, rising to 76% by 2024. Apple represents approximately 22% of total revenue; NVIDIA represents under 12%. This concentration trend is moving in the wrong direction for risk diversification.

If Apple slows iPhone unit volumes, accelerates in-house component design (reducing wafer orders), or if NVIDIA's AI accelerator cycle experiences a slowdown, TSMC's revenue would face material pressure. TSMC's pure-play foundry model means it has no ability to create its own end-market demand — it is entirely dependent on its fabless customers' commercial success. Any scenario in which Samsung or Intel improves their leading-edge yields to competitive levels could create credible alternatives for large customers. ([TechSoda](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/explainer-tsmcs-2024-annual-report))

### Bear #4: AI CapEx Cycle May Be Overheating — Bubble Risk Is Non-Zero

Global hyperscaler capital expenditures for 2026 are estimated at approximately $600 billion, yet the commercial return-on-investment timelines for AI infrastructure remain unclear. Even C.C. Wei himself expressed concern about the pace: "I'm also very nervous about it...If we didn't do it carefully, that would be a big disaster to TSMC."

The semiconductor industry historically experiences inventory correction cycles every 3–4 years (2023 being the most recent example, with consumer electronics demand collapsing sharply). If AI infrastructure investment slows — analogously to the 2000 dot-com bubble correction or the 2023 consumer electronics downturn — TSMC's $52–56 billion annual CapEx commitments would face significant under-utilization risk. Committed capital expenditures at this scale cannot be rapidly unwound. ([The Next Platform, 2026-01-16](https://www.nextplatform.com/compute/2026/01/16/tsmc-has-no-choice-but-to-trust-the-sunny-ai-forecasts-of-its-customers/4092173))

### Bear #5: SMIC's Accelerating Catch-Up May Erode Long-Term Monopoly Position

SMIC's 7nm-equivalent production capacity is projected to double in 2026. SMIC has completed development of a 5nm-equivalent process. China's stated national objective is to scale advanced domestic semiconductor production from under 20,000 wafers per month to 100,000 wafers per month within 1–2 years, with a further target of 500,000 wafers per month by 2030. China's 15th Five-Year Plan designates advanced logic semiconductor manufacturing as a priority strategic objective. YMTC has established a trial production line using entirely domestically sourced equipment.

While SMIC's yields and per-wafer costs remain inferior to TSMC's, a "good enough" domestic alternative could progressively capture TSMC's potential market share within mainland China — particularly in applications where Chinese customers are incentivized by government policy or geopolitical risk aversion to reduce dependence on Taiwan-based supply. ([TrendForce, 2026-02](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2026/02/25/news-china-reportedly-aims-to-boost-7nm-5nm-output-fivefold-in-two-years-driven-by-smic-and-hua-hong/); [Yole Group, 2025](https://www.yolegroup.com/strategy-insights/chinas-next-move-the-five-year-plan-that-could-reshape-semiconductors/))

### Bear #6: Environmental and Energy Constraints May Cap Taiwan Capacity Expansion

TSMC consumed 24.78 TWh of electricity in 2023, representing 8.9% of Taiwan's total national electricity consumption. Greenpeace projects this figure will rise to approximately 24% of Taiwan's total electricity by 2030, equivalent to 267% of TSMC's 2021 consumption levels. Taiwan imports 97% of its energy, its electrical grid suffers from a north-south supply imbalance, and Taiwan Power Company has accumulated losses of NT$350 billion.

TSMC has committed to the RE100 target (100% renewable electricity by 2040), requiring it to scale renewable energy sourcing from approximately 13–14% of consumption in 2024 to 100% — a goal constrained by Taiwan's limited land area for solar and wind expansion. Taiwan's 2021 drought — the most severe in 56 years — forced farmers to fallow agricultural land in order to prioritize semiconductor manufacturing water supply, illustrating the fragility of the resource base. S&P Global has formally identified water risk as a credit risk factor for TSMC. TSMC currently consumes approximately 150,000 tonnes of water per day and produces approximately 11.42 million tonnes of CO2-equivalent annually.

If Taiwan's electricity and water supply infrastructure cannot scale in parallel with TSMC's production expansion ambitions, TSMC may face regulatory or physical constraints on Taiwan-based capacity growth, accelerating the cost-dilutive shift of incremental capacity to overseas locations. ([Greenpeace East Asia, 2023](https://www.greenpeace.org/eastasia/press/7930/semiconductor-industry-electricity-consumption-to-more-than-double-by-2030-study/); [Global Taiwan Institute, 2026](https://globaltaiwan.org/2026/02/taiwans-bumpy-road-to-energy-resilience/); [S&P Global, 2024](https://www.spglobal.com/ratings/en/regulatory/article/240226-sustainability-insights-tsmc-and-water-a-case-study-of-how-climate-is-becoming-a-credit-risk-factor-s12992283))
