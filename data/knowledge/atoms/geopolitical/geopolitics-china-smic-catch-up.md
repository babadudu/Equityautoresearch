### The Impact of Export Controls on TSMC

Since 2022, the United States has enacted stringent export controls designed to choke China's access to advanced semiconductors and the equipment required to manufacture them. The immediate collateral damage to TSMC has been a dramatic shift in its geographic revenue mix. In 2020, Chinese clients accounted for 22% of TSMC's revenue. By the end of 2024, following proactive compliance and the loss of Huawei's advanced node business, that figure plummeted to 9%.

However, TSMC easily absorbed this loss. CEO C.C. Wei noted in late 2025, "If the China market is not available, but I still think the AI's growth will be very dramatically." The insatiable demand from North American hyperscalers simply backfilled the abandoned Chinese capacity.

### SMIC's "Brute Force" Advancement

Despite being cut off from ASML's Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) lithography tools—the industry standard for anything below 7nm—China has not surrendered. The Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation (SMIC) has aggressively utilized older Deep Ultraviolet (DUV) immersion lithography, combined with complex multi-patterning techniques, to force silicon advancement.

In December 2025, TechInsights confirmed that the Huawei Mate 80 Pro's Kirin 9030 processor was manufactured on SMIC's N+3 (5nm-class) node. This marked a monumental, heavily state-subsidized breakthrough, demonstrating that China can manufacture 5nm chips without EUV. SMIC is also heavily supporting Huawei's AI ambitions, producing the Ascend 910C and upcoming 920 AI accelerators.

### The Cost of Sovereignty: Yields and Economics

While SMIC has achieved technological parity with TSMC's ~2020 capabilities (N5), the commercial realities are grim. DUV multi-patterning for 5nm creates exponentially higher defect rates. SMIC's 5nm yield is estimated at just 30-40%, compared to TSMC's >90% benchmark. Consequently, production costs are over 50% higher than TSMC's.

The financial toll on SMIC is visible. Despite record revenues of $9.3 billion in 2025, SMIC's gross margin collapsed to 19.2% in Q4 2025 (compared to TSMC's 62.3%). Expanding advanced capacity actively destroys their profitability. Furthermore, volume remains a massive bottleneck; SMIC's 5nm capacity is estimated at roughly 10,000 wafers per month, a mere fraction of the hundreds of thousands of leading-edge wafers TSMC churns out globally.

### The True Risk: A "Good Enough" Parallel Ecosystem

China's 15th Five-Year Plan (2026-2030) has pivoted away from rigid 70% self-sufficiency targets, implicitly acknowledging the impossibility of fully catching the West in the short term. Instead, it aims to rapidly expand 7nm and 5nm production to 100,000 wafers per month within two years.

For TSMC investors, the threat from SMIC is not that it will steal Apple or NVIDIA as customers; SMIC cannot compete in the open, global merchant market on cost, yield, or scale. The systemic risk is that China develops a "good enough" parallel ecosystem. If SMIC can supply sufficient volumes of functional AI and smartphone chips to satisfy domestic Chinese demand, TSMC (and its Western fabless clients) may permanently lose access to the world's second-largest consumer economy, fragmenting the global tech market into two mutually exclusive spheres.
