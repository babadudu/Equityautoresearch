## Smartphone Industry: From IBM Simon (1992) to iPhone (2007) and the Duopoly Era

### Origins: IBM Simon and the Pre-iPhone Era (1992–2006)

The smartphone industry's origins predate Apple's entry by 15 years. In 1992, IBM unveiled what is widely recognized as the world's first smartphone: the **IBM Simon**. Priced at $1,100, it featured a touchscreen (stylus-operated), a built-in calendar, contacts, calculator, and email — groundbreaking capabilities for its era. In its first six months on the market, IBM Simon sold approximately 50,000 units ([Textedly: Smartphone History](https://textedly.com/blog//smartphone-history-when-were-smartphones-invented)).

In 1997, Ericsson formally coined the term "smartphone." Through the late 1990s and early 2000s, Nokia, BlackBerry (RIM), Palm, and Windows Mobile each built out distinct smartphone ecosystems:

- **Nokia** dominated global feature-phone and early smartphone markets through the Symbian OS platform
- **BlackBerry** owned the enterprise market with its physical QWERTY keyboard and secure email infrastructure
- **Palm** pioneered the PDA-phone convergence with PalmOS
- **Windows Mobile** (Microsoft) targeted enterprise users with a Windows-like interface

Despite these efforts, the pre-iPhone smartphone market was constrained by small screens, physical keyboards, and the limited WAP (Wireless Application Protocol) browsing experience. The primary use cases remained voice calls and SMS. Market penetration stayed low, and the concept of a smartphone as a mass consumer product had not yet materialized.

### The iPhone Inflection Point: January 9, 2007

On January 9, 2007, Steve Jobs took the stage at Macworld Conference & Expo in San Francisco and announced the original iPhone with the now-famous framing: Apple was introducing "a revolutionary mobile phone, a widescreen iPod with touch controls, and a breakthrough Internet communications device" — three devices in one ([Apple Newsroom, 2007-01-09](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2007/01/09Apple-Reinvents-the-Phone-with-iPhone/)).

The iPhone's key innovations over existing smartphones:
1. **Capacitive multi-touch display** — eliminated physical keyboards and styluses
2. **Full desktop-class web browser** — rendered actual websites, not WAP-stripped mobile pages
3. **Single-surface UI** — all glass, no physical buttons beyond the home button
4. **Tight hardware-software integration** — iOS (then called iPhone OS) was purpose-built for the device

The **App Store**, launched in July 2008, was arguably as transformative as the iPhone hardware itself — it created a two-sided marketplace that generated network effects: more users attracted more developers, more apps attracted more users. This flywheel became the economic engine of Apple's Services business.

**Commercial trajectory:**
- Year 1 (2007): 1.4 million iPhones sold
- Year 2 (2008): 11.6 million units ([Textedly](https://textedly.com/blog//smartphone-history-when-were-smartphones-invented))
- 2025: 247 million units (all-time record)

The iPhone's introduction drove Nokia and BlackBerry from industry leadership to near-irrelevance within a decade — one of the fastest major market-share collapses in consumer technology history.

### The iOS–Android Duopoly (2008–Present)

Google's Android, launched in 2008, emerged as the primary competitive response to iOS. Rather than competing as a hardware OEM, Google licensed Android freely to OEMs (Samsung, LG, Huawei, Xiaomi, etc.), creating a broad hardware ecosystem competing on price and feature diversity. This created the current iOS–Android duopoly that defines the global smartphone market structure:

- **Apple (iOS):** ~17% global unit share, ~37–40% global revenue share — premium segment dominance
- **Android OEMs (collectively):** ~83% global unit share — broad mid-range and budget coverage

No single Android OEM approaches Apple's installed base cohesion or revenue capture, making Apple the effective winner of the smartphone market's economic value even without unit-share leadership.

---

### Scale of the Industry Apple Created

The smartphone industry that iPhone catalyzed has become one of the largest in the world:
- **Annual revenue:** The iPhone segment alone generates approximately $58.7B annually ([History Cooperative](https://historycooperative.org/the-history-of-the-iphone/))
- **Employment:** The iPhone ecosystem employs approximately 19 million people globally
- **Ecosystem spillover:** App Store, mobile advertising, digital payments, mobile cloud services, and the entire mobile-first software economy trace their commercial viability to iPhone's mass adoption

### Apple's Product Philosophy: Integration as Competitive Advantage

Cook has articulated Apple's core product philosophy — the belief that integrated hardware-software-services systems create value greater than the sum of parts — as foundational to understanding iPhone's sustained market position:

"That one plus one is equal to three, not two. That if you share an idea and debate it, it gets bigger and better" ([Fortune, 2026-03-11](https://fortune.com/2026/03/11/apple-ceo-tim-cook-late-predecessor-steve-jobs-unusual-advice-never-ask-what-i-would-do/)).

On the emotional dimension of the product, Cook reflected in a 2026 GMA interview: "Oh, it brings me such joy because we make products that empower other people to express themselves" ([GMA, 2026-03-17](https://abcnews.com/GMA/Culture/tim-cook-talks-apples-50th-anniversary-future-ai/story?id=131137939)).

On the iPhone Air launch, Cook framed the premium positioning around personal identity rather than specification: "When something becomes so much a part of you, it needs to reflect your style... We're saying this product is so personal that it needs to reflect you" ([WSJ / 9to5Mac, 2025-09-10](https://9to5mac.com/2025/09/10/tim-cook-iphone-air-interview/)).

### Industry Structure Summary

| Era | Key Players | Defining Feature | Apple Role |
|-----|-------------|-----------------|------------|
| 1992–2006 | IBM, Nokia, BlackBerry, Palm, Windows Mobile | Physical keyboards, WAP browsing | Absent |
| 2007–2010 | Apple, Nokia (declining), BlackBerry (declining) | Full touchscreen, App Store | Category creator |
| 2010–Present | Apple, Samsung, Xiaomi, Oppo, Huawei | iOS/Android duopoly, ecosystem competition | Premium segment leader |

The smartphone is now the most personal computing device in human history, and Apple's design of iPhone as an extension of personal identity — rather than merely a communication tool — remains the key to understanding its durable pricing power within a commoditizing hardware category.
