## From IBM Simon to iPhone: The Origins and Evolution of the Smartphone Industry

### Pre-iPhone Era (1992–2006)

The smartphone industry traces its origins to 1992, when IBM unveiled what is widely recognized as the first "smartphone" — the IBM Simon. Priced at $1,100, the Simon featured a touchscreen (stylus-operated), built-in calendar, address book, calculator, and email. It sold approximately 50,000 units in its first half-year on the market ([Textedly: Smartphone History](https://textedly.com/blog//smartphone-history-when-were-smartphones-invented)). In 1997, Ericsson formally coined the term "smartphone."

Over the following decade, Nokia, BlackBerry (RIM), Palm, and Windows Mobile each launched competing smartphone platforms. However, constrained by small screens, physical keyboards, and the limited browsing experience of WAP, the market remained niche — its primary use cases anchored to voice calls and SMS. The incumbents dominated their respective segments: Nokia led global handset volumes, BlackBerry owned enterprise email, Palm served personal organizer users.

### The iPhone Rupture (2007)

On January 9, 2007, Steve Jobs took the stage at the Macworld Conference & Expo and introduced a product that would permanently restructure the industry. He described the iPhone as the convergence of three devices: "a revolutionary mobile phone, a widescreen iPod with touch controls, and a breakthrough Internet communications device" ([Apple Newsroom, 2007-01-09](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2007/01/09Apple-Reinvents-the-Phone-with-iPhone/)).

The iPhone's full-touch interface, desktop-grade web browsing, and — following the launch of the App Store in 2008 — its developer ecosystem, fundamentally rewrote the industry's competitive dynamics. Sales figures reflect the velocity of disruption: 1.4 million units in the first year (2007), rising to 11.6 million in 2008 ([Textedly](https://textedly.com/blog//smartphone-history-when-were-smartphones-invented)). Nokia and BlackBerry, dominant in 2007, were relegated to marginal roles within five years. The iPhone industry now generates approximately $587 billion in annual revenue globally and employs around 19 million people worldwide ([History Cooperative](https://historycooperative.org/the-history-of-the-iphone/)).

The iPhone's success triggered the formation of a duopoly: Apple's iOS ecosystem and Google's Android platform. This structure has persisted for over fifteen years, with third parties unable to sustain meaningful alternatives.

### Apple's Foundational Product Philosophy

Tim Cook has repeatedly tied the iPhone's success to Apple's integration philosophy — the idea that tightly coupling hardware, software, and services produces a product greater than the sum of its parts: "That one plus one is equal to three, not two. That if you share an idea and debate it, it gets bigger and better" ([Fortune, 2026-03-11](https://fortune.com/2026/03/11/apple-ceo-tim-cook-late-predecessor-steve-jobs-unusual-advice-never-ask-what-i-would-do/)).

On the deeply personal nature of the iPhone as a product category, Cook said in a 2025 WSJ interview following the launch of iPhone Air: "When something becomes so much a part of you, it needs to reflect your style... We're saying this product is so personal that it needs to reflect you" ([WSJ / 9to5Mac, 2025-09-10](https://9to5mac.com/2025/09/10/tim-cook-iphone-air-interview/)).

In a 2026 GMA interview marking Apple's 50th anniversary, Cook spoke to the broader purpose the iPhone serves: "Oh, it brings me such joy because we make products that empower other people to express themselves" ([GMA, 2026-03-17](https://abcnews.com/GMA/Culture/tim-cook-talks-apples-50th-anniversary-future-ai/story?id=131137939)).

Cook has also publicly grappled with the device's potential for compulsive use: "My fundamental belief is, if you're looking at your phone more than you're looking in somebody's eyes, that's a problem" and "I worry about people endlessly scrolling. That's the reason we do things like Screen Time, to try to guide people" ([WIRED, 2024-12](https://www.macrumors.com/2024/12/04/apple-ceo-tim-cook-opens-up-in-new-interview/)).

### Apple's Pre-iPhone History: Context for the iPhone's Significance

Apple Computer Company was founded on April 1, 1976, by Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak, and Ronald Wayne in a garage in Los Altos, California. The first product, the Apple I, was a hand-assembled circuit board priced at $666.66 ([PBS: Apple Timeline](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/science/a-timeline-of-apples-most-influential-product-announcements)). The Apple II (1977) became one of the most commercially successful mass-produced personal computers in history, establishing the personal computer as a consumer product category.

The 1984 Macintosh 128K introduced the graphical user interface and mouse-driven interaction to mainstream consumers — accompanied by Ridley Scott's iconic Super Bowl advertisement, widely regarded as a landmark in technology marketing ([Britannica: Apple Inc.](https://www.britannica.com/money/Apple-Inc)).

Apple nearly collapsed in the 1990s following Jobs's forced departure in 1985: the product line lost focus, and market share plummeted. Jobs returned in 1997, and the subsequent product arc — iMac (1998), iTunes and iPod (2001) — rebuilt Apple's brand and financial base, creating the conditions for the iPhone's launch six years later. Jobs reflected on his ouster in his 2005 Stanford commencement address: "I didn't see it then, but it turned out that getting fired from Apple was the best thing that could have ever happened to me" ([Stanford Commencement, 2005-06-12](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2005/06/youve-got-find-love-jobs-says)).
