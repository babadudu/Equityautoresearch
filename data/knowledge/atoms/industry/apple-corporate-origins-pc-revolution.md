## Apple and the Personal Computer Revolution: From Garage (1976) to iPhone Platform (2007)

### Founding and the PC Category Origin

Apple Computer Company was founded on April 1, 1976, by Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak, and Ronald Wayne in a garage in Los Altos, California. The company's first product, the **Apple I**, was a hand-assembled motherboard sold for $666.66 ([PBS: Apple Timeline](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/science/a-timeline-of-apples-most-influential-product-announcements)).

The **Apple II** (1977) became one of the most commercially successful mass-produced personal computers in history, establishing the personal computer as a consumer product rather than a hobbyist tool. It marked the beginning of the PC as a distinct consumer electronics category.

The **Macintosh 128K** (1984) was the next category-defining product: the first mass-market PC with a graphical user interface (GUI) and mouse-based interaction, fundamentally changing how humans interacted with computers. The Mac's launch was accompanied by the "1984" Super Bowl advertisement, directed by Ridley Scott, which remains one of the most celebrated pieces of technology marketing in history ([Britannica: Apple Inc.](https://www.britannica.com/money/Apple-Inc)).

### The Near-Death Decade (1985–1997)

Apple's trajectory from 1985 to 1997 constitutes one of the most dramatic corporate near-collapses in technology history:

- **1985:** Steve Jobs was forced out of Apple following a boardroom conflict with CEO John Sculley
- **Late 1980s–early 1990s:** Product line fragmentation, margin erosion, loss of PC market share to the IBM-compatible Windows ecosystem
- **1996:** Apple's market share in PCs collapsed; the company was weeks from insolvency at certain points
- **1997:** Apple acquired NeXT (Jobs' post-Apple venture) for $429M, bringing Jobs back as interim CEO

Jobs reflected on his firing in his 2005 Stanford commencement address: "I didn't see it then, but it turned out that getting fired from Apple was the best thing that could have ever happened to me" ([Stanford Commencement, 2005-06-12](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2005/06/youve-got-find-love-jobs-says)). The forced departure sent him to NeXT and Pixar, experiences that would shape the product philosophy and aesthetic sensibility he brought back to Apple.

### The Revival Arc: iMac → iTunes/iPod → iPhone (1998–2007)

Jobs' return produced a sequence of category-defining products:

1. **iMac (1998):** Repositioned Apple as a design-forward consumer brand; the translucent colorful enclosure signaled aesthetic differentiation from grey-box PC commoditization

2. **iTunes + iPod (2001):** Redefined digital music consumption; the iPod achieved 100M units by 2007. More importantly, iTunes established Apple's ability to own end-to-end digital distribution — a template for the App Store model that followed

3. **iPhone (2007):** The synthesis of everything learned in the prior decade — hardware design, software integration, digital distribution ecosystem

This arc was not accidental. It represented a deliberate strategy of competing in categories where tight hardware-software-services integration could create a defensible premium position unavailable to commodity hardware OEMs.

### Tim Cook's Arrival and the Operational Foundation

Tim Cook joined Apple in 1998, at the height of the post-Jobs revival. His decision to join the then-struggling company was immediate upon meeting Jobs:

"No more than five minutes into my initial interview with Steve, I wanted to throw caution and logic to the wind and join Apple" ([Auburn University Commencement, 2010](https://humsci.auburn.edu/iqla/cook.php)).

Cook's contribution to Apple's scale was primarily operational: he dismantled Apple's internal manufacturing (outsourcing to Foxconn and other contract manufacturers), rationalized the supply chain from months of inventory to days, and built the logistics infrastructure that enabled iPhone's global launch volumes. While Jobs provided the product vision, Cook built the machine that manufactured and delivered it at scale.

### The PC Industry as Apple's Origin TAM

Understanding Apple's origin in the PC market matters for TAM analysis because the personal computer industry created the technological and economic infrastructure that made the smartphone revolution possible:

- The GUI paradigm pioneered by Mac became the template for touchscreen mobile interfaces
- iTunes (a desktop application) created the digital media distribution model that became the App Store
- Apple's component sourcing relationships (displays, processors, storage) developed for Mac directly enabled iPhone production
- The Mac's OS X (now macOS) operating system — derived from the NeXT acquisition — provided the codebase from which iOS was derived

Apple's PC heritage is therefore not merely historical context but a structural input to its current competitive position. The company's ability to design its own silicon (Apple Silicon, M-series), control its operating systems across all device categories, and integrate services across Mac/iPhone/iPad/Watch derives directly from capabilities built in the PC era.

### Historical Timeline Summary

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|--------|--------------|
| 1976 | Apple Computer Company founded | First product (Apple I): $666.66 hand-assembled board |
| 1977 | Apple II | First mass-market consumer PC |
| 1984 | Macintosh 128K | First mass-market GUI computer; "1984" Super Bowl ad |
| 1985 | Jobs ousted | Boardroom conflict with Sculley |
| 1997 | Jobs returns via NeXT acquisition | Near-bankruptcy reversed |
| 1998 | iMac; Cook joins Apple | Design renaissance begins; supply chain overhaul |
| 2001 | iTunes + iPod | Digital music ecosystem; distribution model template |
| 2007 | iPhone launch | Smartphone category redefined |
| 2008 | App Store launch | Two-sided marketplace; Services business engine |
| 2020 | Apple Silicon (M1) | In-house chip design; PC competitive differentiation |

The garage-to-empire arc makes Apple an unusual case study in technology history: a company that was near-extinct in its home market (PCs) that used the skills developed in that market to create an entirely new category (smartphones) and then built a recurring-revenue services business on top of it.
