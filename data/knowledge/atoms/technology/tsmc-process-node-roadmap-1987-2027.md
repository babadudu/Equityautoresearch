## TSMC Process Node Roadmap: 3μm to A14 (1987–2027E)

### Moore's Law as Industry Outcome, Not Physical Law

Since the invention of the integrated circuit in the 1960s, the semiconductor industry has followed Moore's Law — roughly doubling transistor density every two years. TSMC has been a central force in sustaining this trend, advancing from 3μm at founding in 1987 to 2nm (N2) in 2025. Morris Chang, in his final public speech in 2023, offered a nuanced perspective on this trajectory: *"It was at first only a prediction, which might not have been true. In fact, if MOS was not invented... Moore's Law would not have been accurate."* — reminding observers that Moore's Law was never a physical inevitability but rather the product of collective industry effort, with TSMC as its foremost enabler. ([Source: Interconnected Blog — Morris Chang's Last Speech, 2023-04](https://interconnected.blog/morris-changs-last-speech/))

Chang also emphasized that manufacturing learning is fundamentally local and concentrated: *"It works, the learning curve, the experience curve, it works only when you have a common location. Learning is local."* ([Source: MIT News, 2023-10-25](https://news.mit.edu/2023/morris-chang-describes-secrets-semiconductor-success-1025)) This insight explains why TSMC's dense cluster of fabs in Taiwan generates compounding yield and density advantages that cannot be easily replicated by geographically dispersed operations.

### The 28nm "Sweet Spot" and Strategic Inflection Points

In a 2025 Acquired podcast interview, Chang described the strategic significance of the 28nm node using a Shakespearean metaphor: *"There's a tide in the affairs of man, which taken at its flood leads on to fortune."* He characterized 28nm as TSMC's sweet spot: *"28 is going to be the sweet spot. It's just like tennis racket — you hit the ball with a sweet spot."* The success of that generation, coinciding with the smartphone explosion, cemented TSMC's leadership position for the following decade. ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang))

### R&D Commitment: 8% of Revenue Through All Cycles

A structural pillar of TSMC's technology leadership is its unwavering R&D commitment. Chang established a fixed R&D budget of 8% of revenue, regardless of economic conditions: *"8%, regardless of whether there's a recession or not. And it's just 8% of revenue."* He described the intent behind fixing the ratio rather than negotiating it annually: *"I wanted to make him at ease, you know what I mean? He doesn't have to argue, he doesn't have to request every year."* ([Source: Acquired Podcast, 2025-01](https://podscripts.co/podcasts/acquired/tsmc-founder-morris-chang)) TSMC's 2024 R&D spending grew 12% year-over-year (NT$21,812M), focused on N2 and A14/A16 process development. ([Source: TechSoda, TSMC 2024 Annual Report](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/explainer-tsmcs-2024-annual-report))

### Current R&D Focus (2025)

CFO Wendell Huang confirmed TSMC's near-term technology focus: *"We are focusing on 14-angstrom, 16-angstrom, and 2-nanometer process technologies."* ([Source: TechSoda, TSMC 2024 Annual Report](https://techsoda.substack.com/p/explainer-tsmcs-2024-annual-report))

For the post-Nanosheet era (2030s), TSMC R&D Director Carlos Diaz outlined exploratory directions at the SEMI Strategic Materials Conference: *"need to continue to push new frontiers, explore new technology"* — including 2D transition metal dichalcogenides (TMD, such as MoS₂ channel materials), carbon nanotube transistors (CNT), and graphene nanoribbons (GNR). ([Source: Mark LaPedus Substack](https://marklapedus.substack.com/p/tsmcs-roadmap-and-other-takeaways))
