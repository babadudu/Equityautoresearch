## GLP-1 S-Curve Diffusion and Structural Demand Trends

### Statin Analogy: Historical S-Curve Reference

GLP-1 technology diffusion closely parallels the historical trajectory of statin drugs. In 1987, Mevacor launched into a market where high cholesterol was widely regarded as a lifestyle problem rather than a medical condition requiring pharmacotherapy. By 2006, Lipitor had become the world's best-selling drug (peak revenue ~$12.9B annually) with over 200 million users globally.

GLP-1 is following the same path: obesity is being redefined from a failure of willpower to a chronic metabolic disease, and GLP-1 drugs are the catalytic agent of that cognitive reframing. IQVIA designated 2025 as the year GLP-1 completed its transition from "niche to mainstream" ([IQVIA, 2026-01](https://www.iqvia.com/locations/emea/blogs/2026/01/outlook-for-obesity-in-2026)).

Two key differences versus statins favor a faster and larger GLP-1 adoption curve:
1. **TAM:** Global obesity population (~1 billion) is roughly 2.5–3x the addressable hypercholesterolemia population at statins' peak.
2. **Iteration speed:** Three generations of GLP-1 technology have been developed in ~20 years; statins took ~30 years for equivalent progression.

### Cognitive Revolution: Obesity as Chronic Disease

The single most important structural demand driver is the reclassification of obesity from a behavioral issue to a chronic medical condition. This shift has direct implications for insurance coverage, prescribing rates, and societal acceptance of pharmacotherapy.

Dave Ricks noted: "The drugs work and they add to the diet exercise regime for people who are already overweight" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)). Italy enacted legislation in 2025 formally defining obesity as a chronic disease ([IQVIA, 2026-01](https://www.iqvia.com/locations/emea/blogs/2026/01/outlook-for-obesity-in-2026)). This legal precedent, if replicated across major markets, would shift GLP-1 treatments from elective/cosmetic to medically necessary—triggering broader insurance reimbursement and dramatically increasing the pool of covered patients.

### Indication Expansion: The Keytruda Analogy

GLP-1 clinical evidence is expanding well beyond obesity and type 2 diabetes. Demonstrated or emerging indications include:
- Cardiovascular risk reduction (SELECT trial, FDA-approved label for Wegovy)
- NASH/MASH (non-alcoholic steatohepatitis)
- Sleep apnea
- Addictive behavior modulation
- Potential kidney and heart failure benefits

Each new indication materially expands TAM and attracts distinct payer pools. Insurers historically reluctant to cover weight loss drugs are more receptive when the same agent demonstrably reduces cardiovascular events. This "indication stacking" strategy mirrors Keytruda's (pembrolizumab) path in oncology—from a single cancer type to 30+ indications and eventual status as the world's best-selling drug.

### Alzheimer's: Lilly's Second Growth Curve

Lilly's Kisunla (donanemab) represents a second structural demand opportunity. Ricks stated the drug achieves "more than 80% reduction [in amyloid plaques] in like a year. Even to undetectable levels" ([T. Rowe Price, 2025-11](https://www.troweprice.com/en/us/insights/the-long-view-eli-lilly)). He framed the next decade as "the decade of the brain... brain health is probably the biggest category" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)).

With ~55 million Alzheimer's patients globally and current penetration below 1%, the addressable market is potentially $50B+. The convergence of an approved amyloid-clearing therapy with an aging global population creates a multi-decade demand tailwind independent of the GLP-1 opportunity.

### AI in Drug Discovery: Lilly's Framework

Ricks offers a grounded assessment of AI's role in pharmaceutical R&D. He acknowledges the field is in "very early innings" but sees asymmetric upside: "A few are billion-dollar items with a $250,000 investment."

He specifically discussed AlphaFold-type protein folding prediction capabilities, acknowledging both its power and its current limits: "It solves a pretty discrete problem really pretty well. Not perfectly, not good enough actually, for drugging the target" ([Fortune, 2024-11](https://fortune.com/2024/11/13/eli-lilly-ceo-dave-ricks/)). This is a nuanced view—protein structure prediction is necessary but not sufficient for drug design.

His framing of the opportunity centers on "repurposed AI"—applying general large language models to pharmaceutical-specific problems. He drew the analogy: "DNA is another language. It's got four letters... You can train it on the human genome and predict things" ([Chief Executive, 2025](https://chiefexecutive.net/ceo-of-the-year-a-conversation-with-eli-lillys-dave-ricks/)). He characterized the current moment as "a kind of a golden era... for medical discovery" ([Washington Reporter, 2025-03](https://washingtonreporter.news/p/interview-eli-lilly-ceo-david-ricks)).

Lilly's AI approach is more conservative than pure-play AI biotech firms but more pragmatic—applying proven LLM architectures to genomic sequence prediction rather than building novel AI platforms from scratch. The risk-adjusted value proposition may be higher given Lilly's existing wet-lab validation infrastructure.

### Synthesis: Structural Demand Drivers Summary

1. **Cognitive reframing** of obesity as chronic disease → insurance coverage expansion
2. **Oral delivery** (Orforglipron) → eliminates injection barrier for ~30–40% of needle-averse patients
3. **Indication stacking** → cardiovascular, NASH, sleep apnea attract incremental payer pools
4. **Alzheimer's second curve** → 55M patients, <1% penetration, ~$50B+ TAM
5. **AI-accelerated pipeline** → faster candidate identification, lower discovery cost per approved drug

The convergence of these five demand drivers over a 5–10 year horizon represents a compounding TAM expansion unlike most pharmaceutical precedents, closest in scale to the statin revolution but with faster velocity and broader indication scope.
